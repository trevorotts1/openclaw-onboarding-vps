### 1. Author Background and Authority

Jim Collins is a management researcher and former faculty member at the Stanford University Graduate School of Business, where he received the Distinguished Teaching Award. He is the co-author of *Built to Last*, a six-year research project conducted at Stanford Business School in the early 1990s. Collins operates from a research laboratory in Boulder, Colorado.

The authority for this book derives from a rigorous five-year research project (1996-2001) involving a team of twenty-one researchers who contributed approximately 15,000 hours of work. The team systematically analyzed companies that made the leap from good results to great results (defined as cumulative stock returns at least 3 times the general market for fifteen years following their transition points) and sustained those results for at least fifteen years. The research involved reading and systematically coding nearly 6,000 articles, generating more than 2,000 pages of interview transcripts, and creating 384 million bytes of computer data. The study identified eleven "good-to-great" companies from an initial universe of 1,435 Fortune 500 companies, comparing them to carefully selected comparison companies to isolate distinguishing variables.

Collins was inspired to undertake this research after Bill Meehan, managing director of the San Francisco office of McKinsey & Company, challenged him by saying that while *Built to Last* was "a very fine job on the research and writing," it was "useless" for the vast majority of companies that "wake up partway through life and realize that they're good, but not great." This planted the seed for the central question: "Can a good company become a great company and, if so, how?"

Collins fundamentally believes that "almost any organization can substantially improve its stature and performance, perhaps even become great, if it conscientiously applies the framework of ideas we've uncovered" and that "greatness is largely a matter of conscious choice."

---

### 2. The Central Problem

The central problem is that **"good is the enemy of great."** Companies (and individuals) settle for being good when they could be great. As Collins states: "We don't have great schools, principally because we have good schools. We don't have great government, principally because we have good government. Few people attain great lives, in large part because it is just so easy to settle for a good life. The vast majority of companies never become great, precisely because the vast majority become quite good—and that is their main problem."

The specific problem the book addresses is how a company can make the transformation from being a good company (with fifteen-year cumulative stock returns at or below the general stock market) to a great company (with cumulative returns at least three times the market over the next fifteen years) and sustain those results. This requires overcoming the "disease of 'just being good'" which appears to be curable through specific disciplined practices.

The book is written for leaders of established organizations who have achieved goodness but sense they could achieve greatness, as well as entrepreneurs building companies from scratch who want to build enduring greatness rather than merely temporary success.

---

### 3. The Root Cause of Human Struggle

The root cause of organizational struggle and mediocrity is **the acceptance of "good enough" and the failure to confront brutal facts while maintaining unwavering faith**. Collins identifies several interconnected root causes:

**The Curse of Competence:** Just because something is your core business—just because you've been doing it for years or perhaps even decades—does not necessarily mean you can be the best in the world at it. "If you cannot be the best in the world at your core business, then your core business absolutely cannot form the basis of a great company."

**Failure to Get the Right People:** "The old adage 'People are your most important asset' turns out to be wrong. People are not your most important asset. The right people are." When companies fail to practice rigorous discipline in people decisions—getting the wrong people off the bus and the right people in the right seats—they cannot achieve greatness.

**Lack of Disciplined Thought and Action:** Collins states that "when you have disciplined people, you don't need hierarchy. When you have disciplined thought, you don't need bureaucracy. When you have disciplined action, you don't need excessive controls." The absence of this discipline creates mediocrity.

**The Doom Loop:** Comparison companies fell into a pattern of "skipping buildup and jumping right to breakthrough," launching radical change programs and wrenching restructurings that failed to produce sustained results because they lacked the underlying flywheel momentum.

**Ego and Hierarchy:** The presence of "Level 4" leaders—those with gargantuan personal egos who look in the mirror to take credit for success but out the window to assign blame for failure—prevents the Level 5 leadership required for greatness.

### 4. The Complete Methodology - Every Step

The methodology consists of a framework of concepts arranged in a sequence: **disciplined people, disciplined thought, and disciplined action**, wrapped around by the flywheel concept.

**Stage 1: Disciplined People**
1. **Level 5 Leadership:** Cultivate leaders who embody "a paradoxical blend of personal humility and professional will." These leaders are ambitious first and foremost for the company, not themselves; they look out the window to apportion credit for success and in the mirror to apportion blame for poor results.
2. **First Who... Then What:** "First get the right people on the bus, the wrong people off the bus, and the right people in the right seats—and then figure out where to drive it." This means rigorously selecting people based on character traits and innate capabilities rather than specific knowledge or skills, and being willing to wait until you find the right person rather than compromising.

**Stage 2: Disciplined Thought**
3. **Confront the Brutal Facts (Yet Never Lose Faith):** Create a climate where the truth is heard through four practices: leading with questions not answers; engaging in dialogue and debate not coercion; conducting autopsies without blame; and building red flag mechanisms. Maintain the Stockdale Paradox: "You must maintain unwavering faith that you can and will prevail in the end, regardless of the difficulties, AND at the same time have the discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality, whatever they might be."
4. **The Hedgehog Concept (Simplicity within the Three Circles):** Develop a simple, crystalline concept that flows from deep understanding about the intersection of three circles: (1) What you can be the best in the world at (and what you cannot), (2) What drives your economic engine (the single denominator—profit per x—that has the greatest impact), and (3) What you are deeply passionate about.

**Stage 3: Disciplined Action**
5. **A Culture of Discipline:** Build a culture full of self-disciplined people who take disciplined action within the three circles. Create "freedom and responsibility within a framework"—a consistent system with clear constraints, but giving people freedom within that framework. Avoid bureaucracy, which arises to compensate for incompetence and lack of discipline.
6. **Technology Accelerators:** Think differently about technology—never use it as the primary means of igniting a transformation, but become pioneers in the application of carefully selected technologies that fit directly with the Hedgehog Concept.

**Wraparound Concept**
7. **The Flywheel and the Doom Loop:** Transformations never happen in one fell swoop or through a single defining action. Instead, they resemble "relentlessly pushing a giant heavy flywheel in one direction, turn upon turn, building momentum until a point of breakthrough, and beyond." Each push builds on the previous work, creating cumulative momentum.

### 5. Non-Negotiable Principles

**Absolute Principles that Cannot Be Compromised:**

- **"Good is the enemy of great."** Settling for good performance prevents the achievement of great performance.
- **First Who, Then What:** "Who" questions must come before "what" decisions—before vision, before strategy, before organization structure, before technology.
- **The Stockdale Paradox:** You must maintain unwavering faith that you will prevail in the end AND simultaneously have the discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality.
- **Discipline over Tyranny:** A culture of discipline is not the same as a tyrannical disciplinarian. "When you have disciplined people, you don't need hierarchy. When you have disciplined thought, you don't need bureaucracy. When you have disciplined action, you don't need excessive controls."
- **Hedgehog Concept Clarity:** If you cannot be the best in the world at your core business, then your core business absolutely cannot form the basis of a great company. It must be replaced.
- **No Technology Fix:** "Technology cannot turn a good enterprise into a great one, nor by itself prevent disaster." Technology is only an accelerator of momentum, not a creator of it.
- **The Flywheel Effect:** "No matter how dramatic the end result, the good-to-great transformations never happened in one fell swoop. There was no single defining action, no grand program, no one killer innovation, no solitary lucky break, no miracle moment."
- **Level 5 Requirement:** Ten of eleven good-to-great CEOs came from inside the company; the evidence does not support the idea that you need an outside leader to come in and shake up the place.
- **No Acquisitions for Momentum:** "Two big mediocrities joined together never make one great company." Major acquisitions come after breakthrough to accelerate momentum, not before to create it.
- **Executive Compensation Irrelevance:** "We found no systematic pattern linking executive compensation to the shift from good to great." The structure of executive compensation is not a key driver.
- **Stop Doing:** "Much of the answer to the question of 'good to great' lies in the discipline to do whatever it takes to become the best within carefully selected arenas and then to seek continual improvement from there."

### 6. The Transformation Arc

**BEFORE the Methodology:**
- **Performance:** Fifteen-year cumulative stock returns at or below the general stock market; average or mediocre performance.
- **Leadership:** Often characterized by "Level 4" leadership—egocentric, charismatic leaders who look in the mirror for credit and out the window for blame; "genius with a thousand helpers" model where the company is a platform for the talents of an extraordinary individual.
- **People:** Wrong people on the bus; people in wrong seats; hiring based on skills rather than character; tolerating mediocrity.
- **Strategy:** Lacking a clear Hedgehog Concept; diversification without discipline; chasing growth for growth's sake; reacting to technology fads; lurching from one program to another.
- **Culture:** Bureaucratic cultures to manage the small percentage of wrong people; lack of disciplined thought and action; inability to confront brutal facts; filtering of bad news due to charismatic leaders.
- **Mindset:** "Good is good enough"; hope for single-stroke solutions; "built to flip" mentality; looking for miracle moments.

**AFTER the Methodology:**
- **Performance:** Cumulative stock returns at least three times the general market over fifteen years; sustained great results. Example: "$1 invested in a mutual fund of the good-to-great companies in 1965... would have multiplied 471 times, compared to a 56-fold increase in the market."
- **Leadership:** Level 5 leadership—self-effacing, quiet, reserved, humble yet fearless; leaders who look out the window to apportion credit and in the mirror to take responsibility; setting up successors for even greater success.
- **People:** The right people on the bus and in the right seats; rigorous culture where people debate vigorously in search of best answers yet unify behind decisions; "first who" discipline applied consistently.
- **Strategy:** Crystal clear Hedgehog Concept at the intersection of the three circles; fanatical adherence to staying within the three circles; "stop doing" list to eliminate extraneous activities.
- **Culture:** Culture of discipline with ethical entrepreneurship; freedom and responsibility within a framework; red flag mechanisms ensure truth is heard; autopsies conducted without blame.
- **Mindset:** Understanding that "greatness is largely a matter of conscious choice"; patient building of flywheel momentum; confronting brutal facts while maintaining faith; technology used as accelerator, not creator.

**Regarding Brutal Facts:**

- "What are the 'squiggly things' under the rocks that we need to look at?"
- "How do we create mechanisms that turn information into information that cannot be ignored?"
- "What would Mr. Hartford do?" (The wrong question—avoiding this trap)

**Regarding the Hedgehog Concept:**
- "What can we be the best in the world at?"
- "What can we not be the best in the world at?" (Equally important)
- "What is the single denominator—profit per x—that has the greatest impact on our economic engine?"
- "What are we deeply passionate about?"
- "If you could pick one and only one ratio—profit per x—to systematically increase over time, what x would have the greatest and most sustainable impact on your economic engine?"

**Regarding Discipline and Execution:**
- "What should we stop doing?"
- "Would you spend your own money this way?"
- "Does the technology fit directly with our Hedgehog Concept?"

- "Do you look in the mirror or out the window when apportioning responsibility?"
- "What is the window and the mirror?"
- "Can you learn to become Level 5?"

### 8. Tools, Exercises, and Practices

**The Council:**
A device for gaining understanding about important issues facing the organization. Characteristics:
- Assembled and used by the leading executive; usually consists of five to twelve people.
- Each member has the ability to argue and debate in search of understanding, not from egoistic need to win a point or protect a parochial interest.
- Each Council member retains the respect of every other Council member without exception.
- Council members come from a range of perspectives, but each has deep knowledge about some aspect of the organization and/or the environment.
- Includes key members of management team but not limited to them; not every executive is automatically a member.
- A standing body, not an ad hoc committee.
- Meets periodically, as much as once a week or as infrequently as once per quarter.
- Does not seek consensus; final decision responsibility remains with the leading executive.
- Informal body—"usually quite innocuous names" like Long-Range Profit Improvement Committee, Corporate Products Committee, Strategic Thinking Group.

**Red Flag Mechanisms:**
Systems that turn information into information that cannot be ignored. Examples:
- **Short Pay:** Graniterock's device giving customers full discretionary power to decide whether and how much to pay on an invoice based on subjective evaluation of satisfaction. Customer circles offending item on invoice, deducts it, and sends check for balance without needing permission.
- **Professor's Red Flag:** Bright red sheet of paper given to each student allowing them to stop class for any observation, disagreement, or question—used to force real-time confrontation of brutal facts.

**Stop Doing List:**
Institutionalized discipline to systematically unplug anything extraneous to the Hedgehog Concept. Example: Darwin Smith eliminating annual forecasts to Wall Street, removing titles, and eliminating layers at Kimberly-Clark.

**Autopsies Without Blame:**
Clinical analysis of mistakes without finger-pointing. Example: Joe Cullman dedicating five pages to dissecting the 7UP disaster, pointing the finger at himself, and giving credit to those who were prescient.

**Packard's Law:**
"No company can grow revenues consistently faster than its ability to get enough of the right people to implement that growth and still become a great company." Used to limit growth based on ability to attract talent.

**Practical Disciplines for Being Rigorous (People Decisions):**
1. When in doubt, don't hire—keep looking.
2. When you know you need to make a people change, act.
3. Put your best people on your biggest opportunities, not your biggest problems.

**The Blue Plans (Abbott Laboratories):**
Mechanism for managing short-term Wall Street pressures: Tell analysts expected growth (e.g., 15%), set internal goal higher (e.g., 25-30%), keep rank-ordered list of unfunded projects (Blue Plans), and at year-end, channel difference between "make analysts happy" growth and actual growth into Blue Plans.

**Five-by-Five Matrix:**
Tap code system used by Stockdale to communicate in POW camp—symbolic of maintaining communication and discipline under brutal facts.

### 9. Objection Handling and Resistance

**Resistance to "First Who":**
- **Objection:** "We need to set vision and strategy first, then get people."
  - **Counter:** "If you begin with 'who,' rather than 'what,' you can more easily adapt to a changing world... If you have the right people on the bus, the problem of how to motivate and manage people largely goes away."
- **Objection:** "We can't afford to wait for the perfect person."
  - **Counter:** "When in doubt, don't hire—keep looking... A company should limit its growth based on its ability to attract enough of the right people."

**Resistance to Brutal Facts:**
- **Objection:** "We need to maintain morale by holding out hope."
  - **Counter:** "One of the single most de-motivating actions you can take is to hold out false hopes, soon to be swept away by events... Winston Churchill understood the liabilities of his strong personality, and he compensated for them beautifully... by creating the Statistical Office to feed him—continuously updated and completely unfiltered—the most brutal facts of reality."
- **Objection:** "The optimists are the ones who survive."
  - **Counter:** (Stockdale Paradox) "The optimists were the ones who said, 'We're going to be out by Christmas.' And Christmas would come, and Christmas would go. Then they'd say, 'We're going to be out by Easter.' And Easter would come, and Easter would go. And then Thanksgiving, and then it would be Christmas again. And they died of a broken heart."

**Resistance to Hedgehog Concept:**
- **Objection:** "We need to diversify to grow."
  - **Counter:** "The more an organization has the discipline to stay within its three circles, the more it will have attractive opportunities for growth... A great company is much more likely to die of indigestion from too much opportunity than starvation from too little."
- **Objection:** "We should acquire companies to create momentum."
  - **Counter:** "You can buy your way to growth, you absolutely cannot buy your way to greatness. Two big mediocrities joined together never make one great company."

**Resistance to Technology:**
- **Objection:** "We need to adopt the latest technology to transform."
  - **Counter:** "Technology cannot turn a good enterprise into a great one, nor by itself prevent disaster... Technology by itself is never a primary, root cause of either greatness or decline... The good-to-great companies used technology as an Accelerator of momentum, not a creator of it."

**Resistance to Level 5 Leadership:**
- **Objection:** "We need a charismatic, larger-than-life leader to transform us."
  - **Counter:** "The evidence does not support the idea that you need an outside leader to come in and shake up the place to go from good to great... Ten of eleven good-to-great CEOs came from inside the company... The good-to-great leaders seem to have come from Mars. Self-effacing, quiet, reserved, even shy."

**The "New Economy" Challenge:**
- **Objection:** "Your findings are for the old economy; we need to throw out all the old ideas."
  - **Counter:** "While the practices of engineering continually evolve and change, the laws of physics remain relatively fixed... The specific application will change (the engineering), but certain immutable laws of organized human performance (the physics) will endure."

### 12. The Repeatable Execution System

**The Flywheel Buildup to Breakthrough Process:**

**Phase 1: Disciplined People (Foundation)**
- Install Level 5 Leadership: Identify or develop leaders who blend personal humility with professional will, who look out the window for credit and in the mirror for blame.
- Practice First Who, Then What: Rigorous selection of team members based on character and values before determining strategy. Remove wrong people immediately when identified.

**Phase 2: Disciplined Thought (Clarity)**
- Confront the Brutal Facts: Establish mechanisms (red flags, Socratic dialogue, autopsies) to ensure truth is heard without filtering.
- Develop the Hedgehog Concept: Use the Council mechanism for iterative dialogue guided by the Three Circles until a simple, crystalline concept emerges at the intersection of passion, excellence, and economics.

**Phase 3: Disciplined Action (Execution)**
- Build Culture of Discipline: Create freedom and responsibility within a framework; eliminate bureaucracy; ensure people "rinse their cottage cheese" (extreme diligence).
- Apply Technology Accelerators: Selectively pioneer technologies that directly accelerate the Hedgehog Concept; ignore technologies that don't fit.
- Push the Flywheel: Accumulate visible results turn by turn; under-promise and over-deliver to Wall Street; channel resources into the Hedgehog Concept while systematically eliminating extraneous activities (stop doing list).

**Phase 4: Breakthrough and Sustained Momentum**
- Reach Breakthrough: When the flywheel hits sufficient momentum, breakthrough results occur.
- Maintain Discipline: Continue fanatical adherence to the three circles; make major acquisitions only to accelerate existing momentum; ensure Level 5 succession.

### 13. The Quality Bar

**Excellence Defined by the Methodology:**

**Quantitative Standards:**
- **Sustained Performance:** Cumulative stock returns at least 3 times the general market for a minimum of fifteen consecutive years following the transition point.
- **Economic Engine:** Systematic increase in the key economic denominator (profit per x) year over year.
- **Industry Position:** Attaining number one or number two position in markets served (for retail/manufacturing contexts).

**Operational Standards:**
- **Best in the World:** Clear understanding of what the organization can be the best in the world at, with equal clarity about what it cannot be the best at.
- **Decision Quality:** Decisions made consistently within the three circles; "stop doing" list implemented rigorously.
- **People Quality:** 100% of key seats filled with the right people; executive churn pattern showing either long tenure or quick removal (not lingering mediocrity).
- **Cultural Quality:** Climate where truth is heard; red flag mechanisms functioning; autopsies conducted without blame; dialogue and debate encouraged.

**Leadership Standards:**
- **Level 5 Attributes:** Leader displays compelling modesty; sets up successors for success; attributes success to factors outside self, blame to self.
- **Succession Quality:** Company performs well after leader departs; no vacuum left by departure.

**Strategic Standards:**
- **Hedgehog Clarity:** Simple, crystalline concept understood by all; not just a strategy but an understanding.
- **Technology Use:** Pioneering application only of technologies that fit the Hedgehog Concept; parity or ignorance of irrelevant technologies.
- **Acquisition Discipline:** Acquisitions made only after breakthrough to accelerate momentum, not to create it.

### 14. Non-Negotiable Execution Rules

**Rules That Must Never Be Violated:**

1. **Never Compromise on First Who:** "When in doubt, don't hire—keep looking." Do not hire until the right person is found. Limit growth based on ability to attract right people (Packard's Law).

2. **Never Delay People Changes:** "When you know you need to make a people change, act." Do not wait, do not try alternatives, do not compensate with systems.

3. **Never Put Best People on Problems:** Always put best people on biggest opportunities, not biggest problems.

4. **Never Ignore Brutal Facts:** Must confront reality while maintaining faith (Stockdale Paradox). Never allow charisma or ego to deter bad news.

5. **Never Violate the Three Circles:** Do not diversify outside the intersection of what you can be best at, what drives economic engine, and what you are passionate about.

6. **Never Use Technology to Ignite Transformation:** Technology is an accelerator only, never a creator of momentum. Never adopt technology because it is trendy; only if it fits the Hedgehog Concept.

7. **Never Acquire to Create Momentum:** Only acquire to accelerate existing momentum. Two mediocrities never make one great company.

8. **Never Skip the Buildup:** Do not attempt breakthrough without the buildup phase. No miracle moments, no single defining actions.

9. **Never Lose Faith:** While confronting brutal facts, must maintain unwavering faith in eventual prevails.

10. **Never Use Layoffs as Primary Strategy:** "Rigorous, not ruthless"—layoffs should not be chronic or used to improve performance; used only in specific restructuring contexts.

11. **Never Look in Mirror for Success:** Level 5 leaders must attribute success to luck and others, not self.

12. **Never Look Out Window for Failure:** Must take full responsibility for poor results.

### 15. Failure Patterns

**The Doom Loop Characteristics:**

**Pattern 1: Skipping Buildup**
- Attempting to jump immediately to breakthrough without accumulated momentum.
- Looking for the "one big push" or "miracle moment."
- Chronic restructuring and radical change programs.

**Pattern 2: Lurching and Reaction**
- Shifting direction frequently; "180-degree turns."
- Responding to technology fads or market pressures without Hedgehog discipline.
- Inconsistent strategy: "If you pick strong generals for key positions, their competitors will leave. But if you pick weak generals—placeholders..."

**Pattern 3: The Genius with a Thousand Helpers**
- Building organization around a charismatic genius (Level 4).
- Failure to build strong executive team; "helpers" rather than leaders.
- Collapse after genius departs (Teledyne, Rubbermaid examples).

**Pattern 4: Tyrannical Discipline**
- Confusing culture of discipline with dictatorship.
- Personal discipline by force of personality rather than system discipline.
- Eventual decline when disciplinarian leaves or becomes undisciplined (Chrysler, Rubbermaid).

**Pattern 5: Undisciplined Diversification**
- "Growth for growth's sake" outside the three circles.
- Acquisitions in arenas where company cannot be best in world (R.J. Reynolds with Sea-Land).
- Diversification to escape core business problems rather than solving them.

**Pattern 6: Technology Addiction**
- Grasping technology as "easy solution" without deep understanding of how it links to Hedgehog Concept.
- Reactive adoption due to "fear of being left behind."
- Using technology to accelerate self-created demise.

**Pattern 7: Blame Externalization**
- Looking "out the window" to assign blame for poor results (imports, unions, regulations).
- Credit external factors rather than confronting internal reality.
- "The sky is falling" panic diversification (Pitney Bowes initially, Addressograph).

**Pattern 8: Inconsistent Leadership**
- Each new leader bringing new program, stopping predecessor's momentum.
- Leaders undoing work of previous generations.
- Lack of Level 5 succession.

**Pattern 9: Selling the Future**
- Compensating for lack of current results with hype about future potential.
- "Built to flip" mentality rather than built to last.
- Stock volatility without substance.

**Pattern 10: Compromising on People**
- Keeping wrong people on the bus too long due to convenience.
- Hiring for skills rather than character.
- Failing to limit growth based on ability to get right people.

### 16. Decision Logic

**Framework for Making Choices:**

**The Three Circles Filter:**
Every decision must be evaluated against the three circles:
1. Can we be the best in the world at this?
2. Does this drive our economic engine (profit per x)?
3. Are we passionate about this?
- **If YES to all three:** Full commitment, pioneer in technology application, fully fund.
- **If NO to any one:** Do not pursue; add to "stop doing" list; settle for parity or ignore.

**The Flywheel Test:**
- Does this decision add momentum to the flywheel in a consistent direction?
- Is this an accumulation of effort or a lurch in a new direction?
- "Will this help us push the flywheel or does it require a new flywheel?"

**The People Test:**
- "First who" logic: Do we have the right people to execute this? If not, do not proceed regardless of strategy attractiveness.
- "Would you hire this person again?"
- "If they left, would you be disappointed or relieved?"

**The Technology Test:**
- Does this technology fit directly with our Hedgehog Concept?
- If YES: Become a pioneer in its application.
- If NO: Settle for parity or ignore entirely.

**The Stockdale Paradox Filter:**
- Are we confronting the brutal facts of how this might fail while maintaining faith that we will prevail?
- Are we holding out false hopes soon to be swept away?

**The Budgeting Logic:**
- "Which arenas best fit with the Hedgehog Concept and should be fully funded and which should not be funded at all?"
- Not "how much does each activity get" but "what should we stop doing?"

**Succession Logic:**
- Is this successor candidate a Level 5 leader?
- Will they set the company up for even greater success in the next generation?
- Is this an internal candidate who embodies the culture?

---

### 17. Self-Review Protocol

**How to Evaluate Your Own Work:**

**The Mirror and Window Test:**
- When things go poorly: Look in the mirror, take full responsibility. "What could I have done differently?"
- When things go well: Look out the window, attribute success to others and luck. "I am a lucky guy."

**Autopsy Without Blame:**
- Conduct post-mortems on failures to extract learning without finger-pointing.
- "I will take responsibility for this bad decision. But we will all take responsibility for extracting the maximum learning from the tuition we've paid."

**Red Flag Mechanisms:**
- Create systems that force you to confront data you cannot ignore (like short pay or the Statistical Office).
- "If you have a red flag, the classroom will stop for you."

**The Council Review:**
- Regular sessions with the Council to test thinking: "They would debate, disagree, pound on tables, raise voices, pause and reflect, debate some more."
- Check: Are we engaging in dialogue and debate or coercion?

**The Three Circles Audit:**
- Regularly re-verify: Are we still at the intersection of what we can be best at, what drives our economics, and what we are passionate about?
- "Stop doing" list review: What are we doing that falls outside the circles?

**Packard's Law Check:**
- Is our growth rate in revenues outpacing our growth rate in right people? If so, we are violating Packard's Law and cannot become great.

**Level 5 Check:**
- Am I being ambitious for the company or for myself?
- Am I looking in the mirror or out the window?

**The Flywheel Check:**
- Are we seeing tangible results accumulating?
- Are we creating alignment through results and momentum, or through motivational hoopla?
- "Does it feel like an organic, cumulative process or a revolutionary event?"

### 18. Definition of Done

**When is the Work Complete:**

**Transition Criteria:**
- **Sustained Results:** Fifteen consecutive years of cumulative stock returns at least 3 times the general market.
- **Sustainability:** The company continues to perform well after the transition leader departs (succession success).
- **Hedgehog Clarity:** The organization has a simple, crystalline concept at the intersection of the three circles that guides all decisions.

**Execution Criteria:**
- **People:** 100% of key seats filled with right people; wrong people removed; Council functioning effectively.
- **Culture:** Culture of discipline established; red flag mechanisms in place; truth is heard without filtering.
- **Strategy:** "Stop doing" list implemented; resources fully channeled into three circles; technology accelerating momentum.
- **Flywheel:** Breakthrough achieved (momentum self-sustaining); buildup phase complete.

**Leadership Criteria:**
- **Level 5 Succession:** Successor is Level 5 or Level 5-capable; company set up for even greater success in next generation.
- **Institutionalization:** The company no longer depends on any single individual; "clock building, not time telling."

**Measurement Criteria:**
- **Economic Denominator:** Consistent improvement in profit per x (the key metric identified in the Hedgehog Concept).
- **Industry Standing:** Attained "best in the world" status in the chosen arena.

**Note:** The work is never truly "done" in the sense of completion, but rather enters a phase of "sustained greatness" where the challenge becomes "how to go from great to enduring great" (the subject of *Built to Last*).

### 19. The Amateur-to-Expert Gap

**Specific Dimensions Separating Amateurs from Experts:**

**1. Approach to Transformation:**
- **Amateur:** Looks for single defining action, miracle moment, or killer innovation to skip buildup. Jumps to breakthrough.
- **Expert:** Pushes the flywheel turn by turn; understands "no matter how dramatic the end result... never happened in one fell swoop."

**2. Use of Technology:**
- **Amateur:** Grasps technology as primary means of igniting transformation; reacts to fads out of fear of being left behind.
- **Expert:** Uses technology only as accelerator within Hedgehog Concept; ignores technologies that don't fit; pioneers those that do.

**3. People vs. Strategy Priority:**
- **Amateur:** Sets vision and strategy first ("first what"), then tries to get people to implement it.
- **Expert:** Gets right people on bus first ("first who"), then figures out where to drive it.

**4. Leadership Style:**
- **Amateur:** Relies on charismatic, egocentric "savior" CEOs from outside; "genius with a thousand helpers."
- **Expert:** Develops Level 5 leadership from within; self-effacing, modest, builds enduring executive team.

**5. Response to Facts:**
- **Amateur:** Holds out false hopes; looks out window to blame external factors for failure; looks in mirror to take credit for success.
- **Expert:** Confronts brutal facts while maintaining faith (Stockdale Paradox); conducts autopsies without blame.

**6. Diversification:**
- **Amateur:** Diversifies outside three circles for growth; acquires to create momentum.
- **Expert:** Maintains fanatical adherence to three circles; acquires only to accelerate existing momentum.

**7. Change Management:**
- **Amateur:** Launches radical change programs, restructurings, and "revolutions" to motivate troops.
- **Expert:** Creates climate where commitment and alignment take care of themselves through flywheel momentum; spends little energy "motivating" people.

**8. Time Horizon:**
- **Amateur:** "Built to flip"; manages for short-term stock price; sells the future to compensate for lack of results.
- **Expert:** Manages for long-term value creation; under-promises and over-delivers; builds to last.

**9. Discipline Type:**
- **Amateur:** Tyrannical discipline through force of personality; dependent on individual leader.
- **Expert:** Culture of discipline with freedom and responsibility within a framework; endures beyond any individual.

**10. Understanding of Excellence:**
- **Amateur:** "Good is good enough"; suffers from curse of competence.
- **Expert:** "Rinses the cottage cheese"—goes to extreme lengths for incremental improvements; never satisfied.

### 20. Professional Application

**Roles, Departments, and Contexts Where This Methodology Applies:**

**Corporate Sectors:**
- **Executive Leadership:** CEOs, COOs, and C-suite executives leading transformation initiatives.
- **Human Resources:** Talent acquisition, succession planning, and cultural development (implementing "first who" principles).
- **Strategic Planning:** Developing Hedgehog Concepts and ensuring disciplined adherence to three circles.
- **Operations:** Building cultures of discipline; eliminating bureaucracy while maintaining controls.
- **Technology Departments:** Evaluating technology as accelerators rather than creators of momentum.
- **M&A Divisions:** Screening acquisitions against the three circles; ensuring acquisitions only accelerate existing momentum.
- **Sales and Marketing:** Focusing on "profit per customer visit" or equivalent economic denominators; avoiding diversification outside core passion.

**Social Sector Organizations:**
- **Education:** School districts and universities seeking to transform from good to great; applying "first who" to teacher and administrator selection.
- **Government Agencies:** Overcoming bureaucratic inertia through culture of discipline; confronting brutal facts of budget constraints while maintaining mission focus.
- **Nonprofits and Churches:** Maintaining core values while stimulating progress; avoiding mission creep outside the three circles.

**Professional Services:**
- **Consulting Firms:** Moving from "good" project delivery to "great" through Level 5 leadership development and Hedgehog clarity.
- **Healthcare Organizations:** Applying discipline to patient care quality while confronting brutal facts of economic realities.

**Entrepreneurial and Start-up Contexts:**
- **Founders:** Building companies with enduring greatness from the ground up rather than just "built to flip."
- **Venture Capital:** Evaluating portfolio companies based on presence of Level 5 leadership, first who discipline, and clear Hedgehog Concepts.

**Individual Professional Development:**
- **Career Planning:** Individuals applying the three circles to their own careers (what can I be best at, what drives my economic engine, what am I passionate about).
- **Leadership Development:** Aspiring to Level 5 leadership characteristics regardless of current position.

**Universal Applicability:**
As Collins states: "This book is not about... the old economy, the new economy... Ultimately, it is about how you take a good organization and turn it into one that produces sustained great results. How best that applies to your organization... is for you to decide." The principles are described as "timeless physics" applicable to any organized human endeavor.
