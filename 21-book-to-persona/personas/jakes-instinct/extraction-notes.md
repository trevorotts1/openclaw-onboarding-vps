### 1. Author Background and Authority

T.D. Jakes is the CEO of TDJ Enterprises, a master communicator, philanthropist, and multidimensional business leader. He is the founder and pastor of The Potter’s House of Dallas, Inc., where he pastors over 30,000 members with millions of worldwide followers. His authority stems from a journey from poverty to prominence. Born in the hills of West Virginia to parents from the 1920s industrial age, he grew up as a "relatively poor boy" in a bedroom community with "meager means but with an enormous work ethic instilled by my parents." His father built a one-mop-one-bucket janitorial business into a fifty-two-employee company; his mother taught school and worked multiple jobs. Jakes worked as a "tent-maker" throughout early ministry—managing the paint department at Sears, selling men’s clothing, working assembly lines at Union Carbide, and starting a lawn service after being laid off during economic devastation.

He writes with authority derived from sitting "at the table with champions in almost every imaginable field," including award-winning entertainers, world-class athletes, and world-changing political leaders. He has visited the Oval Office, been on movie sets, and had "intense conversations that lasted until the restaurant closed" with global leaders. His expertise comes from observing that highly successful people "didn’t develop in an environment of success; they evolved into it." He fundamentally believes that humans are "inherently creative people" equipped with divine instincts for "purpose, fulfillment, and dominion," made in the image of a Creator who wants us to "see beyond the literal, above the bottom line, and beneath the surface of appearances."

### 2. The Central Problem

The central problem is widespread human disconnection from innate instincts leading to profound unfulfillment despite external success. People are "living by man-made rules," chasing "the goals of others instead of pursuing our own dreams," and "anesthetizing our despair with the next purchase, pill, or plunder." They suffer from the tragedy of "not discovering the people, culture, and careers that are part of your tribe and moving to the same beat."

Specifically, the suffering individual is one who has "checked off all the boxes on the recipe for success and yet find yourself falling short," who "dread[s] going to work, not because they are lazy but because they are unfulfilled," and who senses "deep within that we were created for so much more" yet remains "contained in the contrived cage of the ordinary." They are described as those who "mistake the prize for the art of winning and will ultimately buy a trophy without ever running a race," who "aren’t successful; they just have the props," and who "bust their gut to appear busy to everyone around them" without producing true results.

The problem manifests as misalignment—"your life somehow offbeat to your inner melody"—where people spend lives doing what they were trained to do, asked to do, or what others need them to do, "all the while wonder[ing] why the feeling of fulfillment eludes us."

---

### 3. The Root Cause of Human Struggle

The root cause is the suppression and ignoring of God-given instincts in favor of external conditioning, intellect without intuition, and conformity to social constructs. As stated: "Unfortunately, much of what I see today isn’t about fulfilling one’s true potential as much as it is about appearing to fulfill what other people expect."

Jakes identifies that "we’ve never learned to rely on our instincts," instead allowing ourselves to be "manipulated by the approval of others, the paycheck that supports them, and the lifestyle that has handcuffed them to the brass ring of perceived success." The deep cause is spiritual and biological: "We are assaulted by so much information each day that it’s easy to lose touch with the voice inside us," and we are often "conditioned to dismiss our instincts as primal and animalistic, subjective and unscientific."

He quotes Proverbs 4:23—"Out of the heart flow the issues of life"—to emphasize that we function from the heart/instinct, but "the heart can’t read. It can’t draw and it surely can’t drive." The tragedy occurs when people "adjust to the urgency of circumstances" rather than following their internal compass, becoming "slaves to a man-made system" because they haven't learned to "sift the stirrings within so that your survival instincts can surface."

### 4. The Complete Methodology - Every Step

The methodology is a comprehensive system for aligning life with internal instincts:

**Step 1: Recognition of Instinctual DNA**  
Acknowledge that "instinct is interwoven into the very fiber of our DNA" and that "our cells have instincts," vibrating to the tempo of their purpose before performing function.

**Step 2: Exposure and Awakening**  
"You cannot be what you do not see. It isn’t that exposure gives us instincts; it’s that exposure awakens instincts and stops us from ignoring what we know to be true within us."

**Step 3: Decoding Your Design**  
"Become a student of yourself" by examining bookmarked favorites, Pinterest images, saved Twitter feeds, magazines read, and childhood pleasures. "Dredge up your favorite memories of childhood and what gave you pleasure... Whatever once had the power to float your boat can still rock your world!"

**Step 4: Independence from Imitation**  
"Your exposure to others must become fuel and not the engine itself." Recognize that "the higher you ascend on your own unique path, the fewer the number of trailblazers ahead of you."

**Step 5: Taking Inventory**  
"Inventory what’s on the inside of you" before building teams or launching concepts. Identify "inclinations that you may not have acted upon but should at least explore."

**Step 6: Moving from Inside Out**  
"The art of being a visionary is to get the inside vision to materialize outside." Stop manufacturing synthetic ideas and "put your seal, your scent, your essence, your DNA, on what you produce."

**Step 7: Assembling the Diverse Team**  
"You must not limit yourself to any one singular viewpoint. You need a manufacturer in the room with a senator, a record producer next to the record breaker..." When ideas hang out with influence, income emerges.

**Step 8: Leaving the Cage**  
"Wean yourself from the nurturing state of normal and accepted practices." Recognize that "cages are comfortable" but "the lion longs for something he may never have experienced."

**Step 9: Gradual or Immediate Transition**  
Either "jump into the jungle" or "stroll out gradually," counting the cost before building. "Sometimes it’s better to remain in the cage until after feeding time rather than risk starving in the jungle!"

**Step 10: Adapting to New Environments**  
"Sniff out your new world before you do too much barking." Practice "360-degree thinking" considering all stakeholders.

**Step 11: Balancing Intellect and Instinct**  
"Walk a tightrope between what is verifiable and what is intangible." Combine "the Zulu" (instinct) with "the zoologist" (intellect).

**Step 12: Building Intersections**  
Find "the intersection points among diverse associations" where different directions meet. "Find the touchpoints of what you have in common with people."

**Step 13: Integration vs. Tolerance**  
Move beyond tolerating differences to integrating them so that "people feel that their uniqueness isn’t just tolerated but is respectfully integrated into the plan."

**Step 14: Execution**  
"Inspiration without execution will always lead to frustration." Turn integration strategy into action points with specific assignments.

**Step 15: Juggling Multiple Worlds**  
Find "common denominators" connecting diverse responsibilities. "You are the shared interest. You are the ultimate brand."

**Step 16: Continuous Adaptation**  
"Old ideas that are unchallenged become the gateway to antiquation." Maintain core values but evolve methods.

**Step 17: Treetop Living**  
"Feed from the tops of the trees." Do not "lower your head to respond" to ground-level critics.

**Step 18: Investment and Increase**  
Apply the "instinct to increase"—taking what is given and producing 100% increase, leaving every situation better than found.

**Step 19: Protection from Predators**  
Maintain your "base"—"that calm core of confidence within you"—while navigating new jungles.

**Step 20: Activation**  
"Pull!"—Release the trigger on prepared instincts with precise timing.

**The Instinctive Time Audit**: Evaluate whether you are spending 80% of time on highest/best use or on mundane tasks. "Switch those stats and spent 80 percent of your time doing what you were created to do."

### 9. Objection Handling and Resistance

**"I don't have the resources/capital"**: Countered by the principle that "when ideas hang out with influence, income will always emerge." Jakes argues: "You aren’t one bank loan away from a million dollars, but you are one creative idea from a million dollars."

**"I'm too busy"**: Countered by the distinction between busyness and productivity. "Being busy equates to being productive" is false. "Only 20 percent of what we do every day is our highest and best use." The solution is to "burn off the brush" of clutter.

**"I need more training/education first"**: Countered by examples of Steve Jobs, Bill Gates, and others who succeeded without traditional degrees in their fields. "Intellect without instinct can only explain and explicate but not execute."

**"It's too risky to leave my current situation"**: Addressed through the cage/jungle metaphor. While cages are comfortable, "where there’s nothing ventured, there’s of course nothing gained." However, he validates incremental transitions: "Sometimes we stroll out of our cage, explore the terrain, return to our cage for a while."

**"I tried and failed"**: Countered by "Stumble Toward Success" philosophy. "We need to fail boldly if we want to succeed extravagantly!" Failure provides "a clue to your future success."

**"The critics are too loud"**: Countered by the giraffe principle. "Once you reach a certain level, you can’t be offended by other species who continue looking up from the ground!" Don't "lower your head to respond" to turtles.

**"I don't know what I'm created to do"**: Addressed by examining "what you passionately hate" as well as what you love, and studying childhood pleasures and current fascinations.

**"I should wait for perfect timing"**: Countered by "the fierce urgency of now." "Lost time means irretrievably lost opportunities."

### 12. The Repeatable Execution System

**Phase 1: Pre-Execution (Inventory and Exposure)**  
Conduct internal inventory of gifts and instincts. Decode your design by examining fascinations, childhood pleasures, and patterns. Simultaneously, expose yourself to diverse environments and high achievers while maintaining independence from imitation.

**Phase 2: Environmental Assessment (The Zulu Phase)**  
Before acting, "sniff out" the new jungle. Research due diligence, identify predators and allies, understand unwritten codes, and survey the "property" (360-degree thinking) to identify all stakeholders and intersections.

**Phase 3: Team Assembly**  
Assemble diverse team with "similar instincts but diverse perspectives." Include manufacturers with senators, artists with scientists. Avoid monolithic relationships. Hire for instinct over résumé alone.

**Phase 4: Transition Strategy**  
Determine whether to "jump" (immediate) or "stroll" (gradual) from cage to wild. Count the cost. Maintain safety nets if possible, but recognize when "the gate closes" and retreat is impossible.

**Phase 5: Internal-to-External Manifestation**  
Turn "inside vision to materialize outside." Apply your DNA/seal to all output. Do not copy; discover intrinsic value. Move from inspiration to intersection (finding common ground with diverse partners).

**Phase 6: Integration and Execution**  
Move beyond tolerating differences to integrating them. Negotiate win-win scenarios. Create action points with specific assignments. Execute with urgency—"the fierce urgency of now."

**Phase 7: Juggling and Maintenance**  
Find common denominators connecting diverse responsibilities. "Feed what feeds you"—reinvest in core resources (heart/engine). Practice controlled burns to eliminate brush/busyness.

**Phase 8: Adaptation and Evolution**  
Continuously assess "what was vs. what is." Maintain core values but evolve methods. Avoid "stiff-necked" approaches when environments change. Reexamine team members and self for growth rather than past definitions.

**Phase 9: Elevation**  
Maintain "treetop" perspective. Do not lower neck to engage with critics. Continue "eating from the top" while others graze at ground level.

### 15. Failure Patterns

**The Trophy Buyer**: "Buying a trophy without ever running a race"—seeking appearance of winning without practices of champions.

**The Zoologist Without the Zulu**: Having intellect/explanation without instinct/execution. "Intellect can make a salesman knowledgeable about his product, but it cannot tell him how to read his client."

**The Caged Lion**: Staying in "cycles of the cage" until instincts atrophy. "Caged lions don’t mate with free ones."

**The Stiff-Necked Apatosaurus**: Continuing past practices when environments change, leading to self-destruction "simply because it didn’t instinctively adapt." "Stiff-necked people will destroy vital opportunities."

**The Problem-Reiterator**: "People who gain access to my office merely to reiterate problems" without participating in solutions. "If they weren’t participating in the solution, they were by default adding to the problem!"

**The Busy Non-Producer**: Spending 80% of time on tasks "that could be done by numerous others" rather than unique contributions.

**The Immediate Partisan**: Taking sides too quickly in new environments without "sniffing" first. "He who walks in the middle of the road gets hit by both sides" is preferable to choosing sides while still disoriented.

**The Treetop Turtle**: "Think[ing] like giraffes but eat[ing] like turtles"—allowing low-level critics to determine high-level actions. "I walked away from a twenty-foot opportunity because of the chatter I heard from two-foot thinkers!"

**The Rusty Iron**: Failing to maintain sharpness through new challenges, resulting in "rust also results in rust"—stagnancy spreading through organization.

**The Independent Lone Wolf**: Operating independently in midst of team, stunting growth of both self and others. Sending signal that you don't need help.

### 16. Decision Logic

**When Choosing Paths**: Select the path that aligns with "inner melody" and "tempo" over external expectation. "If you listen, it will guide you like a magnet to steel. All else is misguided."

**When Hiring**: Balance "the zoologist" (résumé/intellect) with "the Zulu" (instinctive assessment). Question how candidates would handle situations to see "how their mind works" rather than relying on "airbrushed" résumés.

**When Expanding/Moving**: Apply "360-degree thinking"—consider wife, children, finances, employees, reputation, community. "Count the cost before you build your house."

**When Retaining vs. Releasing**: Determine whether team member is "builder" (needs challenge/innovation) or "banker" (needs maintenance/stability). Do not place bankers in charge of development or builders in charge of maintenance.

**When Networking**: Seek "intersections" where different directions meet. Look for "touchpoints of what you have in common" rather than focusing on divisions. Choose "completion" (those who complement) over competition.

**When Facing Criticism**: Determine if critic is at "treetop" or "turtle" level. "Once you get to a certain stature, you can’t find nourishment in low places." Do not lower neck to engage.

**When Allocating Time**: Apply 80/20 principle—"20 percent of what we do every day is our highest and best use." Switch to spend 80% on unique contributions.

**When Adapting**: Ask "Is your inability to adapt to the world around you your greatest enemy?" Maintain core values (unchanging) vs. methods (changing).

**When Taking Risk**: Evaluate whether fear is of staying (claustrophobic) or going (unknown). "I am afraid of spending my whole life with the deceptive deduction that my cage is the world!"

### 19. The Amateur-to-Expert Gap

**Intellect vs. Instinct Integration**: Amateurs rely on "training, education, or relationships or resources" alone (external data); experts combine "instincts with intellect to discover a new way of seeing what’s missing in plain sight."

**Command vs. Initiative**: Amateurs wait for command to perform ("If you’re waiting on someone to command you to perform your best... you’re not in touch with your instincts"); experts operate from "unspoken expectation" of increase.

**Problem Reiteration vs. Solution Creation**: Amateurs "gain access to my office merely to reiterate problems"; experts "bring innovative solutions." Amateurs identify problems; experts resolve them.

**Retention vs. Pruning**: Amateurs keep everyone from past phases due to loyalty or fear; experts "prune the vine they worked all their lives to grow," recognizing that "you can’t take everyone with you just because they were with you where you were before."

**Tolerance vs. Integration**: Amateurs "tolerate" differences temporarily; experts "integrate" differences so that people feel "their uniqueness isn’t just tolerated but is respectfully integrated into the plan."

**Busyness vs. Productivity**: Amateurs wear busyness as badge of honor; experts know "only 20 percent of what we do every day is our highest and best use" and strive to flip the ratio.

**Copying vs. Discovering**: Amateurs engage in "low-grade plagiarism of popularity"; experts "stop copying and start discovering what is intrinsically within you," putting "your seal, your scent, your essence, your DNA, on what you produce."

**Failure Response**: Amateurs quit after "spectacular flops"; experts "stumble toward success," understanding that "we need to fail boldly if we want to succeed extravagantly!"

**Turtle vs. Giraffe Engagement**: Amateurs lower their neck to fight with "turtles" (ground-level critics), losing leverage; experts maintain "treetop" perspective and refuse to "stoop to prove a point."

**Imitation vs. Innovation**: Amateurs surround themselves with "monolithic relationships" (people just like them); experts build "nets" of diverse strands, recognizing that "you can’t be surrounded with monolithic relationships and tap into a full release of your potential."

### 20. Professional Application

**Leadership Roles**: CEOs, pastors, and department heads who must transition from "pastoral tendencies" to "management," distinguishing "ministry from management and potlucks from portfolios." Specifically applies to leaders moving from retention models to release models, or from single-venue leadership to juggling multiple "jungles."

**Entrepreneurship and Business Development**: Founders transitioning from "cage" of employment to "wild" of entrepreneurship, and serial entrepreneurs who repeatedly enter "new jungles." Includes specific application for "builders" vs. "bankers" in organizational roles.

**Creative Industries**: Artists, musicians, filmmakers, writers, and designers who must move from "inside out"—transforming internal vision to external reality without copying others. Includes application for those producing "something new from more malleable materials such as words, images, and music."

**Human Resources and Talent Development**: Professionals responsible for hiring, particularly using the "Zulu" method (instinctive assessment) alongside the "zoologist" method (résumé/intellect). Includes frameworks for determining "retention vs. release" and identifying "stiff-necked" employees who cannot adapt.

**Strategic Planning and Operations**: Managers responsible for "360-degree thinking," ensuring all stakeholders are considered before execution. Includes "brush burning" protocols to eliminate obsolete practices and "feeding what feeds you" resource allocation.

**Sales and Marketing**: Professionals who must balance data with instinct—"intellect can make a salesman knowledgeable about his product, but it cannot tell him how to read his client." Includes networking through "intersections" rather than monolithic relationships.

**Career Transition Coaching**: Application for individuals moving between industries or roles, particularly the "stroll vs. jump" methodology for leaving cages safely, and the "immigrant" protocol for entering new organizational cultures.

**Relationship and Partnership Management**: Marital, business partnership, and alliance formation using "integration" (not just tolerance) frameworks, and the "intersection" model for finding common ground across differences.

**Personal Productivity Consulting**: Application of 80/20 time management, "controlled burns" of busyness, and "juggling" multiple responsibilities through common denominator identification.

**Crisis Management and Security**: Application of instinctive leadership under pressure—distinguishing between those who can "teach the role" vs. those who can perform under "gunplay" (referencing the security agent who ran during airport incident).
