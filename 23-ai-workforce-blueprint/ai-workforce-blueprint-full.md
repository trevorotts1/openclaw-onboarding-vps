## Overview

The AI Workforce Blueprint is a step-by-step process for building the folder and file system that turns your AI agent into a trained employee who knows your business inside and out.

Here is what it does:

1. It organizes your entire business into departments and roles - just like a corporate org chart
2. It creates numbered .md files (Markdown files) inside each role that teach the AI exactly how to do every job
3. It gives your AI a "Start Here" document in every folder so it always knows where to begin, what the rules are, and where to find the knowledge it needs
4. It becomes the intelligence layer that powers your OpenClaw AI agent - so when a task comes in, the AI knows which department owns it, which role handles it, and which file tells it how

Think of it this way: if you hired a new employee, you would not just throw them into the job with no training manual. You would give them an org chart, a job description, SOPs for how to do their tasks, examples of good and bad work, and rules for how to use the company tools.

That is exactly what this blueprint does - except the employee is an AI agent, and the training manual is a folder full of .md files.

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: You are not just making folders. You are building a workplace. Each file is a piece of training, each folder is a department, and each role folder is a desk with the exact tools and instructions that role needs.

### A Simple Analogy: The Real Office

Imagine walking into a real corporate office building:

- You do not see a giant pile of random papers on the floor.
- You see labeled departments like Sales, Marketing, and Operations.
- Inside each department, you see specific roles and job desks.
- At each desk, you see a clear job description, a training binder, and examples of good work.

Your folder structure is that office building. If the folders are messy, the office is messy. If the folders are clear, the AI walks in, sits down, and gets to work.

> 🧭 **Real-World Example**: A hospital has departments (ER, Radiology, Billing). Each has roles (triage nurse, radiologist, billing clerk). Each role has protocols and checklists. That is the difference between chaos and safe care. Your AI needs the same structure.

### What Happens WITHOUT This System vs WITH This System

> ⚠️ **Without the System**
> - You repeat the same instructions over and over.
> - The AI gives you different answers each time.
> - You cannot trust the output.
> - You become the bottleneck for every task.
> - The AI never gets better because it is not learning from documentation.
>
> ✅ **With the System**
> - The AI knows where to look before it acts.
> - The output becomes consistent and predictable.
> - You can hand work off and walk away.
> - You can scale to multiple departments without losing quality.
> - The AI improves because the knowledge base grows over time.

## Purpose

Why does this matter? Why can't you just talk to the AI and tell it what to do?

You can. But here is what happens when you do:

1. You repeat yourself constantly - the AI forgets between sessions
2. The quality is inconsistent - sometimes great, sometimes terrible
3. You become the bottleneck - the AI can't do anything without you sitting there directing it
4. You can't scale - one AI doing one thing at a time, with you babysitting

When you build the knowledge architecture:

1. The AI knows what to do without being told every time
2. The quality is consistent because it follows documented SOPs
3. You can walk away and the AI keeps working
4. You can have multiple AI agents handling multiple departments simultaneously

This is the difference between having an AI assistant and having an AI workforce.

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: A single AI assistant is helpful. A structured AI workforce is transformational. The difference is the system you build.

> 💡 **Tip**: If you ever feel like you are "babysitting" your AI, that is a signal you need better documentation. The system fixes the babysitting problem.

## Things to Think About (Before You Start)

Before you create a single folder, take a moment to think about these things. Do not rush this part. The thinking is the work.

### 1. What departments does your business actually need?

Not what departments a Fortune 500 company needs. What does YOUR business need? If you are a one-person operation, you might only need 3-4 departments. That is perfectly fine.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- What are the top 3 buckets of work that keep your business running?
- If you had to explain your business to a 10 year old, what would you say you do all day?
- Which parts of your business make money, and which parts support the money making parts?

### 2. What roles exist inside each department?

A department is the team. Roles are the people on that team. The Marketing department might have a Content Creator, a Social Media Manager, and an Ad Copywriter. Or it might just have one person doing all three. Either way works - you decide the structure.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- If you hired three people tomorrow, what would you hire them to do?
- Which tasks require deep focus and should be owned by one role, not shared?
- Which tasks are currently bouncing between people because nobody clearly owns them?

### 3. What does each role actually do day to day?

Before you write any SOPs, you need to be able to list the 3-7 main tasks each role performs. If you can't list them, you're not ready to write the documents yet.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- What are the top tasks you do in that role every single week?
- Which tasks are high impact and repeat often?
- What does a good week look like for that role? What does a bad week look like?

### 4. Do you already have SOPs or training materials?

If you have existing documents, scripts, checklists, or training videos - great. Those become the raw material for your .md files. You don't have to start from scratch.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- Where are your current SOPs living right now? Google Docs, Notion, paper notes?
- Which SOPs are the most complete and could be copied into .md files today?
- Which SOPs are missing and need to be created from scratch?

### 5. What tools does your business use?

QuickBooks, Convert and Flow, Calendly, Google Workspace, social media platforms - make a list. Each role needs to know which tools it has access to.

**Follow-up prompts:**
- What tools are used by everyone, and what tools are role specific?
- Which tools are confusing or error prone? Those need extra SOP detail.
- Which tools have logins, permissions, or security rules the AI must respect?

### Quick Self-Assessment

✅ **Before you build anything, can you answer YES to these?**

- I can name my departments without guessing.
- I can list the main roles inside each department.
- I can list 3-7 tasks for each role.
- I know which tools each role should use.
- I know where my current SOPs and training materials live.

> ⚠️ **If you cannot answer YES to all five, pause here.** This is the foundation. Building folders without clarity is like pouring concrete before you mark the property lines.

## The Folder Structure

Your entire system lives inside one root folder. From there, everything branches by department, then by role.

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: The folder structure is the skeleton. The files are the muscles. The AI cannot move without both.

### Step 1: Check for an Existing Master Folder

Before creating anything new, check your Downloads folder. Do you already have a master folder called something like:

- openclaw-master-files
- OpenClaw master files
- OpenClaw master documents
- openclaw master docs
- master-docs
- master-files
- Or anything similar

If yes:
- **Do not create a new master folder.**
- Put your department folders INSIDE the existing master folder.

If no:
- Create a new folder in your Downloads folder called **openclaw-master-files**.
- Use this exact name. All lowercase. Hyphens between words.

> 💡 **Tip**: Your AI should never have to guess which master folder is the real one. One master folder only.

### Step 2: Create Department Folders

Inside your openclaw-master-files folder, create one folder for each department your business needs.

**Naming rules for department folders:**
- All lowercase
- Hyphens instead of spaces
- End every department folder name with "-dept" so it is immediately clear this is a department
- No special characters (no &, @, #, etc.)

| Do This | Not This |
|---------|----------|
| sales-dept/ | Sales Department/ |
| marketing-dept/ | Marketing_Team/ |
| customer-support-dept/ | Customer Support & Success/ |
| operations-dept/ | Ops/ |
| creative-dept/ | creative/ (missing -dept suffix) |
| billing-dept/ | billing/ (missing -dept suffix) |

> 💡 **Why do department folders end in -dept?**
> - It lets the AI scan the folder tree and instantly know which folders are departments.
> - It prevents confusion with role folders that might have similar names.
> - It makes search and sorting faster because all departments have the same suffix.

### Step 2A: Create the Master Orchestrator Department (Big Boss)

In addition to your normal departments, you must create ONE special department folder:

- **master-orchestrator-dept/**

This is the only "department" that does not have role folders inside it.

Why?
- The Master Orchestrator is a single employee.
- Its job is to be the Big Boss over the entire system.
- All other departments report back to it.

**What it must contain:**

```
master-orchestrator-dept/
  00-00-Master-Orchestrator-Start-Here.md
  01-How-to-Route-Work-to-Departments.md
  02-How-to-Create-Missing-How-To-Files.md
```

**What the Master Orchestrator must understand (non-negotiable):**
1. **All departments report back to it.**
2. **It decides which department owns a task** and routes the work there.
3. **If a department says it has no training**, the Master Orchestrator creates the missing how-to.md file inside that department so the department is never missing documentation again.

### Step 3: Create the Department Overview File

Every department folder gets ONE file at the top level, before any role subfolders:

```
00-Department-Overview-[Department-Name].md
```

Examples:
- 00-Department-Overview-Sales.md
- 00-Department-Overview-Marketing.md
- 00-Department-Overview-Operations.md

This file is the shared context for every role in that department. It contains the department's tech stack, team structure, standards, and how it hands off work to other departments.

### Step 4: Create Role Folders Inside Each Department

Each department has different roles. Each role gets its own subfolder.

**Naming rules for role folders:**
- All lowercase
- Hyphens instead of spaces
- Named after what the role actually is
- No abbreviations

| Do This | Not This |
|---------|----------|
| appointment-setter/ | Appointment Setter/ |
| content-creator/ | content_creator/ |
| closer/ | The Closer/ |
| account-manager/ | AM/ |

### Step 5: Add Files Inside Each Role Folder

Every role folder follows this structure:

```
role-name/
  00-Start-Here-[Role-Name].md          (The orchestrator)
  01-How-to-[First-Task].md             (Knowledge base)
  02-How-to-[Second-Task].md            (Knowledge base)
  03-How-to-[Third-Task].md             (Knowledge base)
  ...
  intelligent-routing.md                 (Which AI model for which task)
  [N]-Good-Examples-[Role-Name].md       (Always second to last)
  [N+1]-Bad-Examples-[Role-Name].md      (Always last)
```

## Naming Conventions

Naming is not optional styling. It is the routing logic. Embedded in the name of everything is its purpose. If you name something outside of its purpose, you will cause it to be misunderstood and misused.

> 📣 **Operator Note**
> "Embedded in the name of everything is its purpose. If you name something outside of its purpose, you will first cause that thing to be misunderstood and as a result you will cause it to be misused."

### The Numbering System

Every file gets a two-digit number at the BEGINNING, followed by a dash, then a clear description.

In the world of code, counting starts at zero - not one. Zero is always at the front, never at the end.

| Number | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| 00 | Start Here / Instructions (the orchestrator) |
| 01 | First knowledge base file |
| 02 | Second knowledge base file |
| 03+ | Continue as needed |
| Last-1 | Good Examples (always second to last) |
| Last | Bad Examples (always last) |

### Why Two Digits Matter

If you use single digits (1, 2, 3), your computer sorts them wrong at 10:
```
1, 10, 11, 2, 3, 4...
```

With two digits (01, 02, 03), they sort correctly:
```
01, 02, 03, 04, 05... 10, 11, 12
```

### File Name Format

```
[number]-[Clear-Descriptive-Name].md
```

Good examples:
- 00-Start-Here-Appointment-Setter.md
- 01-How-to-Set-an-Appointment.md
- 02-How-to-Handle-Objections.md
- 05-Good-Examples-Appointment-Setter.md
- 06-Bad-Examples-Appointment-Setter.md

Bad examples:
- stuff.md (what is in it? nobody knows)
- file1.md (meaningless)
- create images.md (no number, AI doesn't know order)
- instructions.md (ambiguous - Start Here is clearer)

### More Bad Examples and Why They Fail

- **Sales Overview.md**
  - ❌ No number, so the AI cannot tell where it fits.
  - ❌ Capitalization is inconsistent with the rest of the system.
- **00-start-here-appointment setter.md**
  - ❌ Spaces break consistency. The AI reads this as messy and inconsistent.
- **01-SetAppointment.md**
  - ❌ No "How-to" and unclear task description.
- **02-how_to_follow_up.md**
  - ❌ Underscores break the folder system style.
- **05-GoodExamples.md**
  - ❌ Missing the role name. The AI cannot tell which role it belongs to.

> ⚠️ **Common Mistake**: If you are unsure, keep it boring. Clear, simple, consistent names always win.

## The Department Overview File

This file sits at the top level of each department folder, before any role subfolders. When your AI enters a department, this is the first file it reads.

### What Goes in a Department Overview

1. **Department Name and Purpose** - What is this department? Why does it exist?

2. **Team Structure** - What roles exist? How do they relate to each other? A table showing each role, its function, and who it reports to.

3. **Shared Tech Stack** - What tools does this entire department use? Organized by category (CRM, communication, scheduling, analytics).

4. **Department-Wide Standards** - Response times, CRM discipline, communication tone, meeting standards.

5. **Inter-Department Handoffs** - How does work come IN from other departments? How does work go OUT to other departments? What triggers those handoffs?

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: The Department Overview is the "house rules" for that department. Every role inside that department follows it.

> 💡 **Tip**: Keep the Department Overview high level. Do not bury step by step SOPs here. Those live in the role folders.

### Minimum 7 Setup Questions (Required)

For each department, the AI must ask at least **7 questions** so it can generate the Department Overview, the role Start Here files, and the knowledge base files correctly.

**Interview-Style Question Format:**
Each question should include an example answer. This helps the user understand what kind of information you're looking for. Questions should be conversational and warm.

**Before asking, check existing context:**
- MEMORY.md for business info
- USER.md for preferences and tools
- AGENTS.md for known integrations
- HEARTBEAT.md for active projects
- IDENTITY.md for business context

Skip any question where the answer is already known from these files.

#### The 7 Required Questions (With Examples)

After each question, add this fallback line: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**1. What is the purpose of this department?**
   - Example: "The Sales department converts leads into paying customers through calls, demos, and proposals"
   - Example: "The Marketing department generates awareness and brings new leads into the business"
   - Fallback: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**2. What roles exist inside this department?**
   - Example: "Appointment Setter, Closer, Account Manager"
   - Example: "Content Writer, Social Media Manager, Ads Specialist"
   - Example: "Just one person - a generalist who does everything"
   - Fallback: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**3. What does each role do day to day?**
   - Example: "The Appointment Setter makes 50-80 calls per day, books 3-5 appointments"
   - Example: "The Content Writer writes blog posts, emails, and social media content"
   - Fallback: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**4. What does this department receive from other departments and what does it hand off to other departments?**
   - Example: "Receives qualified leads from Marketing, hands closed deals to Operations"
   - Example: "Receives content requests from Sales, hands finished content to everyone"
   - Fallback: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**5. What is the tech stack for this department (tools and logins used daily)?**
   - Example: "GoHighLevel for CRM, Slack for communication, Google Calendar for scheduling"
   - Example: "Canva for design, Buffer for social posting, Google Drive for storage"
   - Fallback: "If you are not sure, just ask me to research best practices for your industry. We will figure it out together."

**6. What are the KPIs for this department (goals, targets, definition of success)?**
   - Example: "Sales: 3-5 appointments booked per day, 75% show rate, $50k monthly revenue"
   - Example: "Marketing: 10,000 impressions per week, 50 new leads per month"

## The Start Here Document Structure

The Start Here file (00) is the single most important file in every role folder. It is the orchestrator - not the worker. It tells the AI who to be and where to find what it needs. It does NOT contain the step-by-step how-to instructions.

The most common mistake people make: they try to put all the instructions in the Start Here document. That is wrong. The Start Here document is 10% overview and 90% references to the knowledge base files.

> ⚠️ **Common Mistake**: If the Start Here file is longer than all the other files combined, it is too heavy. It should point, not do.

### Every Start Here File Must Include

1. **Role Overview** - Name, department, reports to, collaborates with

2. **Purpose** - Why this role exists. What problem it solves.

3. **Skill Sets to Embody** - Hard skills (CRM management, script execution) and soft skills (active listening, empathy, resilience)

4. **Experience to Embody** - What professional background should this role reflect? "Operate as if you have 3-5 years of experience in..."

5. **Role-Specific Tech Stack** - What tools this role uses day to day (subset of department tech stack plus any role-specific tools)

6. **Tone and Communication Style** - How should this role sound? What energy level? What phrases to use and avoid?

7. **Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)** - What metrics define success? What are the targets?

8. **Boundaries and Constraints** - What should this role NEVER do? When should it escalate?

9. **Edge Case Protocol** - What happens when it encounters something not covered? Default: escalate and document. If same edge case happens 2-3 times, create a new knowledge base file.

10. **Knowledge Base Roadmap (When-to Reference Map)** - THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SECTION IN THE ENTIRE FILE. This is the roadmap. It tells the AI: "When you are about to do THIS task, go read THAT knowledge base file FIRST before you act."

Without this map, the AI has a folder full of files and no idea which one to open when. With this map, it routes itself instantly to the right instructions every time.

```
When you need to book a meeting    -> Read 01-How-to-Set-an-Appointment.md FIRST
When a prospect pushes back        -> Read 02-How-to-Handle-Objections.md FIRST
When you need to qualify a lead    -> Read 03-How-to-Qualify-a-Lead.md FIRST
When a prospect goes silent        -> Read 04-How-to-Follow-Up.md FIRST
When you need quality reference    -> Read 05-Good-Examples.md
When you need to see mistakes      -> Read 06-Bad-Examples.md
```

**The Edge Case Rule:** If the AI encounters a task that has NO matching knowledge base file in the roadmap, it should:
1. Still handle the task intelligently using its general knowledge and the role context from the Start Here file
2. Flag that this is an edge case - there was no SOP for this task
3. Create a NEW knowledge base file for that task so next time there IS an SOP
4. Update the When-to Reference Map to include the new file
5. This is how the system grows - edge cases become documented processes

Think of it like this: the first time a new situation comes up, the AI handles it the best it can. But it also writes down what it did and how, so the NEXT time the same situation comes up, there is a real SOP to follow. Over time, your knowledge base fills itself in.

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: The Start Here file is your role map. It does not do the work. It points to the work.

> 💡 **Tip**: Keep the roadmap simple. Short, clear trigger phrases and exact file names.

> 🔁 **Edge Case Self-Growth Callout**
> - If a task shows up and there is no SOP, you still complete the task.
> - You then write the SOP and add it to the map.
> - This is how the knowledge base grows without you manually doing all the work.

## Knowledge Base File Structure

Every file after 00 is a knowledge base file. These are the SOPs - the actual how-to documents that teach the AI (or a human) how to do a specific task.

### Every Knowledge Base File Must Include

1. **Objective** - What is the goal? What does "done well" look like?

2. **Step-by-Step Process** - Walk through the procedure in order. Be specific enough that someone with zero context could follow it. Include decision points (if the customer says X, do Y).

3. **Scripts, Templates, or Examples** - Sample language, message templates, scripts. Show what a good version looks like.

4. **Common Mistakes to Avoid** - What are the typical failure points? What should the role watch out for?

5. **Success Criteria** - How do you KNOW this was done correctly? What is the measurable outcome?

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: A knowledge base file is not a paragraph. It is a recipe. Clear steps, clear outcomes.

### Routing Cues for the Master Orchestrator (Big Boss)

When you write knowledge base files (01, 02, 03, etc.), you must write them so the Master Orchestrator can route work correctly.

That means your how-to files should include clear language like:
- "This is a Sales task. Go to sales-dept and follow the Appointment Setter role instructions."
- "This is a Marketing task. Go to marketing-dept and follow the Facebook Ads role instructions."
- "This is an Operations task. Go to operations-dept and follow the Admin role instructions."

**When it is unclear which department owns the task:**
1. The Master Orchestrator must look at the department list.
2. It must review the role folders and role Start Here files.
3. It must choose the best department based on the actual role responsibilities.

**When a department reports back that it has no training:**
- The Master Orchestrator must create a new how-to.md file in that department.
- The file name must be appropriate for the department and the task.
- The goal is that the department is never missing documentation for that task again.

### Writing SOPs for AI vs Humans

People need verboseness. AIs need conciseness.

When writing for AI to execute:
- Be clear and direct
- Don't over-explain
- Use numbered steps
- Be specific about what to do

When writing for humans to follow:
- Be detailed and warm
- Don't assume knowledge
- Explain what they'll see after each step
- Include what to do if something looks different

#### Side by Side Comparison

| Element | SOP for AI | SOP for Humans |
|--------|------------|----------------|
| Voice | Direct and concise | Warm and explanatory |
| Steps | Short, numbered commands | Detailed, numbered actions |
| Context | Minimal, task focused | Full context and reasoning |
| Edge Cases | Simple flags | Multiple explanations |
| Mistakes | Short list of warnings | Detailed examples and corrections |

### Complete Example: Knowledge Base File (How to Set an Appointment)

Below is a full copy-pasteable knowledge base file. This is exactly how detailed a good SOP should be.

```markdown
# 01-How-to-Set-an-Appointment.md

## Objective
Book a qualified appointment in the calendar with all details captured in the CRM, while keeping the prospect feeling respected and understood.

## Step-by-Step Process
1. **Open the conversation**
   - If inbound, respond within 5 minutes
   - If outbound, acknowledge it is a cold outreach
2. **Confirm the prospect's interest**
   - Ask one open-ended question about their current situation
3. **Identify the pain point**
   - Ask what is not working for them right now
4. **Bridge to the appointment**
   - Explain that the appointment is a strategy session, not a sales pitch
5. **Offer two specific times**
   - Never ask "When are you free?"
   - Always offer two specific options
6. **Confirm and book**
   - Book the appointment immediately while you are live with them
7. **Send confirmation**
   - Send calendar invite and reminder text
8. **Log everything in CRM**
   - Update notes, stage, and any key details

## Scripts and Templates

### Inbound Opening Script
"Thanks for reaching out. I want to make sure I point you in the right direction. Can I ask what prompted you to reach out today?"

### Outbound Opening Script
"Hey [Name], this is [Your Name]. Quick note, this is a cold call. Do you have 30 seconds for me to tell you why I called?"

### Bridging Script
"I do not want to waste your time. The best next step is a short strategy session with our specialist so we can see if this is even a fit. Does that sound fair?"

### Two Time Options Script
"I have openings Tuesday at 2 PM or Wednesday at 11 AM. Which one works better for you?"

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Talking too much and not listening
- Pitching before qualifying
- Saying "I will send you a link" without booking live
- Forgetting to log notes in the CRM

## Success Criteria
- Appointment is booked on the correct calendar
- Prospect confirms the time in writing or verbally
- CRM notes include pain point, goal, and next step
- Follow-up reminder is scheduled

## Quality Check
Before you move on, confirm:
- The calendar invite is sent
- The CRM stage is updated
- The appointment details match what was agreed
```

## The Intelligent Routing File

This is a NEW addition to every role folder. It tells the AI which language model (LLM) to use for which type of task inside that role.

Not all AI models are equal. Some are better at writing. Some are better at analysis. Some are cheaper for simple tasks. The intelligent routing file makes sure your AI is using the most effective and cost-effective model for each task.

### What Goes in intelligent-routing.md

```markdown
# Intelligent Routing - [Role Name]

## Task-to-Model Map

| Task Type | Recommended Model | Why |
|-----------|------------------|-----|
| Quick lookups and simple responses | GPT-5 Nano or Haiku | Cheapest, fastest |
| Writing client-facing content | Opus or Sonnet | Quality matters |
| Data processing and CRM updates | MiniMax M2.5 | Good with tools, cheap |
| Research and web search | Perplexity Sonar Pro | Built for search |
| Image creation | MiniMax (calls KIE.ai) | Just calls the tool |
| Complex analysis or strategy | Opus | Best reasoning |

## Cost Awareness
- Track which models are being used
- If Opus is doing grunt work, that is a problem
- Target: 80%+ of routine tasks on cheaper models
- Escalate to premium models only when quality demands it

## Update Protocol
- Review this file monthly
- Update when new models become available
- Monitor costs and adjust routing as needed
```

The AI should be checking this file whenever it starts a task, so it routes to the right model automatically.

> 💡 **Tip**: This file is your budget manager. It keeps you efficient without sacrificing quality.

> 🔑 **Key Concept**: These are calibration tools, not scripts to copy. They show quality, not templates.

### About the CRM Specialist Role

The CRM Specialist role appears in MULTIPLE departments. This is intentional.

Sales needs a CRM specialist to manage pipelines and contact records. Marketing needs one to manage campaigns, segments, and automations. Creative needs one to manage asset libraries and content calendars. Billing needs one to manage payment tracking and subscription records.

Each department's CRM Specialist has different knowledge base files because they use the CRM differently. The Sales CRM Specialist's SOPs are about pipeline management. The Marketing CRM Specialist's SOPs are about email sequences and tagging. They share the same tool but do different jobs.

> 💡 **Rule:** When a role appears in multiple departments, each version gets its own Start Here file and its own knowledge base files specific to how that department uses the role. Do NOT create one shared CRM Specialist folder. Each department gets its own version.

### About the Web Development and App Development Departments

These are technical departments focused on building things with code.

**web-development-dept** builds websites, sales funnels, landing pages, and anything that lives in a browser. Roles inside it know HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and tools like WordPress, GoHighLevel page builder, ClickFunnels, etc.

**app-development-dept** builds software applications, mobile apps, APIs, and custom tools. Roles inside it know programming languages, frameworks, databases, and deployment processes.

These departments are separate because the skills, tools, and processes are different. A funnel builder and a mobile app developer do very different work.

> ⚠️ **Not every client needs these.** If the client does not build websites or software, skip them. The AI should ask: "Does your business create websites, funnels, or landing pages?" and "Does your business develop software or mobile apps?" If the answer is no, do not create these departments.

This file walks through the complete process:
1. Open the conversation (separate scripts for inbound vs outbound)
2. Identify the pain (open-ended questions, let them talk)

4. Book it while you have them (offer two specific times, never "I'll send you a link")
5. Log everything in CRM immediately

Includes sample scripts for voicemail, text follow-up, and live calls. Lists the 5 most common mistakes (talking too much, pitching instead of listening, vague scheduling, etc.).

### Example: Good Examples File

Shows what quality output looks like:
- An inbound first-contact call that opens with curiosity, listens, validates, and bridges naturally to booking

### Example: Bad Examples File

Each bad example explains WHY it fails and points back to the correct knowledge base file.

## Signs Your Structure Is Working

How do you know you built it right? Look for these signs:

1. **The AI picks the right department without being told.** You say "write me a follow-up email for a lead" and it goes straight to the sales department, appointment setter role, follow-up knowledge base file.
2. **The AI handles edge cases by flagging them, not guessing.** When it gets a task that does not fit cleanly, it asks you instead of making something up.
3. **The AI produces output that matches your Good Examples more than your Bad Examples.** The quality of work improves because the AI has real standards to reference.
4. **You stop repeating yourself.** You used to explain the same process every time. Now it is in a knowledge base file and the AI just follows it.
5. **New tasks get documented automatically.** When the AI encounters something new, it creates the missing SOP, files it correctly, and updates the Start Here reference map.
6. **Routing is fast.** The Master Orchestrator receives work and sends it to the right place in seconds, not minutes of you explaining where it goes.

> 💡 **If none of these are happening:** Your structure is missing something. Go back to the self-check (Step 7 in the AI block) and rate yourself again.

## Glossary

Quick reference for terms used in this document.

| Term | What It Means |
|------|--------------|
| **Department** | A major function of your business (sales, marketing, billing, etc.). Each one gets its own folder ending in -dept. |
| **Role** | A specific job inside a department (appointment setter, content writer, etc.). Each one gets its own subfolder. |
| **Start Here (00-Start-Here)** | The identity document for a role. It tells the AI who it is, how it behaves, what its boundaries are, and where to find its knowledge base files. |
| **Knowledge Base File** | A numbered how-to document (01, 02, 03...) that teaches the AI one specific task or process. |
| **Department Overview** | The top-level file in a department folder that describes the department's purpose, team structure, shared tools, and handoff rules. |
| **Master Orchestrator** | The "Big Boss" department. It receives all incoming work, decides which department handles it, routes it there, and creates missing SOPs when needed. |
| **When-to Reference Map** | A table inside the Start Here file that maps every situation to the correct knowledge base file. |
| **Intelligent Routing** | A file inside each role folder that helps the AI decide which model (expensive vs. cheap) to use for different tasks. |
| **Edge Case** | A task or situation that does not match any existing knowledge base file. The AI should flag it, handle it, then create a new file so it is not an edge case next time. |
| **Good Examples / Bad Examples** | The last two files in every role folder. They show the AI what quality looks like and what failure looks like. |
| **SOP** | Standard Operating Procedure. A step-by-step process document. |
| **.md file** | A Markdown file. Plain text with simple formatting that every AI reads natively. |
| **Universal SOPs** | SOPs that apply across all departments (not owned by any single department). |

## Cross-Department References

Sometimes a role in one department needs to reference a file that lives in a different department or in the universal SOPs folder.

**The rule:** Never copy a file into two places. Reference it.

**How to reference across departments:**

In any knowledge base file, write a reference like this:

```
For brand voice guidelines, see: ../universal-sops/brand-voice-guidelines.md
For lead qualification criteria, see: ../sales-dept/closer/03-How-to-Qualify-Before-Close.md
```

The AI will follow the path to the correct file.

> ⚠️ **Do NOT duplicate files across departments.** One source of truth. Always reference, never copy.
