## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 -- Creative Brief Intake and Qualification

**When to run:** Every time a creative request is submitted by any department.
**Frequency:** On-demand (multiple times daily).
**Inputs:** Creative request submitted via the standard intake form in the project management platform.

**Steps:**
1. Receive creative request notification. Open the submitted brief.
2. Validate completeness: Is the business objective stated? Is the target audience defined? Are deliverable specs clear? Is the deadline realistic (minimum 48 hours standard, 24 hours expedited)?
3. If the brief is incomplete, reject it within 2 business hours with a specific list of missing fields. Do NOT start work on an incomplete brief.
4. If the brief is complete, classify it: Standard (normal turnaround), Expedited (24-hour turnaround, requires Director approval), or Strategic (multi-week, CDO involved).
5. Assign a unique project ID and add to the creative queue.
6. Match the project to the appropriate designer or team based on skill set, current capacity, and project type.
7. Notify the requester of the assigned designer, the project ID, and the confirmed delivery date.

**Outputs:** Qualified, actionable creative brief with assigned team and confirmed timeline.
**Hand to:** Assigned designer or art director for execution (SOP 9.2).
**Failure mode:** If the brief cannot be qualified (requester unresponsive, scope unclear, deadline impossible), escalate to the Director of {{DEPARTMENT_NAME}} within 4 business hours with a recommended resolution (scope reduction, deadline extension, or resource reallocation).

### SOP 9.2 -- Creative Production and Internal Review

**When to run:** Upon receipt of a qualified creative brief from SOP 9.1.
**Frequency:** Continuous, per active project.
**Inputs:** Qualified creative brief with project ID, assigned designer, confirmed delivery date.

**Steps:**
1. Designer opens the creative brief and reviews all requirements, reference materials, and brand guidelines.
2. Designer creates the first draft of the creative asset(s) using approved tools (Adobe CC, Figma, or AI-assisted tools per SOP 9.7).
3. Designer performs a self-check against the Quality Gates (Section 10, Gate 1) before submitting for review.
4. Designer submits the draft through the creative review/proofing platform with a note explaining creative choices aligned to the brief.
5. Senior Art Director or Creative Director reviews within 4 business hours. Review checks: alignment with brief, brand compliance, visual quality, technical specs.
6. If approved: asset advances to stakeholder review (SOP 9.3). If rejected: reviewer provides specific, actionable feedback through the platform's markup tools. Designer revises and resubmits (max 2 revision rounds before escalation per SOP 9.5).

**Outputs:** Internally reviewed and approved creative asset, ready for stakeholder review.
**Hand to:** Creative review platform triggers notification to stakeholders (SOP 9.3).
**Failure mode:** If internal review identifies a systemic issue (brief contradicts brand guidelines, requested format technically impossible), escalate to CDO for a design strategy decision. Do not proceed with flawed work.

### SOP 9.3 -- Stakeholder Review and Approval

**When to run:** After internal creative review (SOP 9.2) passes.
**Frequency:** Per project.
**Inputs:** Internally approved creative asset, stakeholder list with defined approval roles from the creative brief.

**Steps:**
1. Route the asset to stakeholders via the creative review platform. Use parallel routing when multiple stakeholders can review simultaneously; use sequential routing when Legal must review after creative.
2. Each stakeholder reviews within their defined SLA window (standard: 24 hours, expedited: 4 hours).
3. Stakeholders provide feedback through the platform's markup tools only -- no email, Slack, or verbal feedback accepted. All comments must explain what to change and why.
4. If no changes requested: stakeholder digitally approves. If changes requested: designer implements changes and resubmits. Max 1 stakeholder revision round per stakeholder group.
5. After all stakeholders approve, the platform captures a digital audit trail: who approved, when, and under what version.
6. Final approved asset is locked from further edits and auto-archived in the DAM.

**Outputs:** Fully approved, locked creative asset with complete digital audit trail.
**Hand to:** Distribution (SOP 9.4) or directly to the requesting department as "complete."
**Failure mode:** If a stakeholder fails to respond within the SLA window, the platform auto-escalates to their manager. If the manager does not respond within 4 hours, the CDO has authority to approve and advance the asset with a documented override. Stakeholder non-response cannot block delivery.

### SOP 9.4 -- Asset Distribution and DAM Archival

**When to run:** Upon final approval of any creative asset (after SOP 9.3).
**Frequency:** Per asset, every time.
**Inputs:** Fully approved creative asset, output specifications (format, resolution, color profile per channel).

**Steps:**
1. Export the final asset in all required formats per the creative brief: source file (AI/PSD/Figma), production file (PDF/TIFF), and web-optimized files (PNG/JPG/WEBP at specified resolutions).
2. Apply consistent file naming convention: [ProjectID]_[AssetDescription]_[Version]_[DateYYYYMMDD].[ext]
3. Upload all versions to the DAM with complete metadata: project ID, campaign name, asset type, designer, approval date, usage rights, expiration date, and relevant tags.
4. Verify metadata is searchable: confirm the asset appears in a test search before considering the step complete.
5. Notify the requesting department that assets are available in the DAM with direct links.
6. Deliver channel-specific assets directly to the appropriate platform (upload social graphics to the scheduling tool, send print files to the print vendor).

**Outputs:** Fully archived, searchable, properly formatted creative assets in the DAM with direct access links shared.
**Hand to:** Complete. The requesting department retrieves assets from the DAM.
**Failure mode:** If the DAM is unavailable, store assets in a secure shared drive with identical file naming and a README.txt documenting all metadata. Upload to DAM within 24 hours of platform restoration. Notify the Director immediately.

### SOP 9.5 -- Revision Loop Escalation

**When to run:** When any creative project exceeds 2 internal revision rounds OR 1 stakeholder revision round.
**Frequency:** On-demand, triggered by revision count threshold.
**Inputs:** Project that exceeded revision threshold, full revision history from the proofing platform.

**Steps:**
1. The creative review platform auto-flags the project when the revision threshold is exceeded.
2. The assigned designer notifies the CDO with a summary: original brief requirements, what was delivered in each round, and the specific feedback driving continued revisions.
3. CDO reviews the revision history to diagnose root cause:
   - **Scope creep:** Requester adding new requirements not in the original brief. Resolution: CDO contacts requester, confirms the brief is the contract, offers new project for additional scope.
   - **Brief quality failure:** Original brief was incomplete or contradictory. Resolution: CDO requires a revised brief before further work (re-trigger SOP 9.1).
   - **Design quality failure:** Designer not meeting standard. Resolution: CDO reassigns project to senior designer and schedules 1:1 coaching with original designer.
   - **Stakeholder indecision:** Multiple stakeholders giving contradictory feedback. Resolution: CDO convenes a 15-minute decision meeting with all stakeholders and makes the final creative call.
4. Document the root cause and resolution in the project record.
5. Resume the project under the new parameters.

**Outputs:** Diagnosed and resolved revision loop; project back on track.
**Hand to:** Assigned designer (same or reassigned) to continue production.
**Failure mode:** If CDO cannot resolve the stalemate (two C-suite stakeholders with irreconcilable creative preferences), escalate to Master Orchestrator for tie-breaking. The owner makes the call if needed.

### SOP 9.6 -- Brand Emergency Response

**When to run:** When a public-facing brand error is detected (incorrect logo usage, off-brand colors, typo in published ad, unauthorized brand usage by a partner).
**Frequency:** On-demand (rare, high-urgency).
**Inputs:** Brand violation alert from governance platform, or a report from any team member.

**Steps:**
1. Assess severity within 15 minutes of detection:
   - **Severity 1 (Public-facing, high visibility):** Live ad, homepage, investor materials, press release, physical signage. Immediate action required.
   - **Severity 2 (Public-facing, low visibility):** Social post, email footer, internal-but-widely-distributed.
   - **Severity 3 (Internal only):** Internal presentation, draft document.
2. For Severity 1: Immediately pull the asset if possible (contact web team, pause ad campaign, notify printer). Replace with correct approved version from the DAM.
3. For Severity 2: Flag for correction within 4 business hours. Generate corrected asset and replace.
4. For Severity 3: Flag for correction within 1 business day. No urgent pull required.
5. Root-cause analysis: How did the error occur? (Human error, tool misconfiguration, outdated DAM file, partner non-compliance.)
6. Implement preventive measure: Update SOP, add automated check, retrain responsible party, or tighten partner brand usage agreements.

**Outputs:** Corrected public-facing brand presence, documented root cause, implemented preventive measure.
**Hand to:** Complete internally. Notify any affected external partners.
**Failure mode:** If error cannot be immediately corrected (printed materials already distributed, third-party platform with delay), escalate to Director with estimated correction timeline. Prepare a brief holding statement if external stakeholders might notice.

### SOP 9.7 -- AI-Assisted Creative Production

**When to run:** When a designer or art director opts to use AI tools for ideation, concept generation, or production assistance.
**Frequency:** On-demand, per designer discretion.
**Inputs:** Qualified creative brief, approved AI tool subscription.

**Steps:**
1. Designer determines whether AI assistance is appropriate. AI recommended for: concept exploration, mood boards, background generation, image upscaling, variation testing, repetitive production tasks. AI NOT appropriate for: final client-facing assets without human review, legally sensitive materials, work requiring original IP where ownership matters.
2. Designer uses approved AI tools (Adobe Firefly, Midjourney Enterprise) per licensed terms. All AI prompts and outputs must be documented.
3. AI-generated output is treated as a DRAFT, not a final asset. Designer must apply human creative judgment: refine, correct, compose, quality-check.
4. After human refinement, asset enters standard review workflow (SOP 9.2) with disclosure: "AI-assisted: [tool used] for [specific purpose]."
5. Final approved asset must be indistinguishable in quality from fully human-produced work.

**Outputs:** AI-assisted creative asset, human-refined and approved through standard workflow with disclosure documentation.
**Hand to:** Standard review workflow (SOP 9.2).
**Failure mode:** If AI output is consistently low-quality or off-brand for a specific use case, designer switches to manual production. Report tool limitation to CDO for quarterly tool audit. Never ship obviously AI-generated work without human refinement.

## 10. Quality Gates

Before any output ships, it must pass these gates:

### Gate 1 -- Self-check (Designer)

- [ ] The asset fully addresses every requirement in the creative brief.
- [ ] The asset complies with {{COMPANY_NAME}} brand guidelines: correct logo usage, color palette, typography, imagery style, and tone of voice.
- [ ] Technical specifications are correct: file format, resolution (300 DPI print, 72 DPI web unless retina), color profile (CMYK print, sRGB digital), dimensions, bleed (if applicable).
- [ ] All copy proofread for spelling and grammar errors.
- [ ] All licensed assets (stock photography, fonts, icons) properly licensed for intended use and territory.
- [ ] The asset passes accessibility checks: sufficient color contrast (WCAG AA minimum), readable font sizes, alt-text prepared for web graphics.

### Gate 2 -- Department QC Review (Senior Art Director or Creative Director)

The QC role in {{DEPARTMENT_NAME}} reviews for:
- [ ] Creative excellence: Is this work at or above the quality bar set by {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s best previous work?
- [ ] Brand coherence: Does this asset feel like it belongs to the same family as all other {{COMPANY_NAME}} creative output?
- [ ] Strategic alignment: Does the creative approach support the business objective in the brief?
- [ ] Competitive differentiation: Would this work stand out in the {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} landscape?

### Gate 3 -- Devil's Advocate Review (only for outputs marked "high stakes")

The DA evaluates:
- [ ] Brand risk: Could this creative be misinterpreted, offend a segment, or create a PR liability?
- [ ] Legal risk: Are claims substantiated? Trademark symbols correct? Competitor references legally safe?
- [ ] Consistency risk: Does this creative establish a precedent that could fragment the brand if repeated?
- [ ] Strategic risk: Does this creative inadvertently position {{COMPANY_NAME}} in conflict with long-term brand strategy?

### Gate 4 -- Owner Approval (only for outputs marked "owner-required")

What requires the human owner's sign-off before going live:
- Any creative that redefines or significantly evolves the brand's visual identity (new logo, major rebrand elements).
- Creative for investor-facing materials, earnings reports, or public company announcements.
- Crisis communications creative.
- Any creative involving the owner's personal brand, likeness, or direct quotes.
- Creative with a production budget exceeding ${{OWNER_APPROVAL_THRESHOLD}}.

## 13. Good Output Examples

### Example A -- Campaign Visual Identity System

A new product launch campaign requires a cohesive visual identity. The CDO delivers:

**"{{COMPANY_NAME}} Q3 Product Launch -- Visual Identity System"**

- **Concept statement:** A single paragraph articulating the creative idea, emotional tone, and visual language direction, explicitly linked to the campaign's business objective ("drive 15% conversion lift among {{TARGET_AUDIENCE}}").
- **Mood board:** 10-15 curated reference images establishing color mood, photographic style, typographic attitude, and composition language. Each image annotated with what to borrow or avoid.
- **Design system specification:** Defined color palette with hex codes, primary/secondary typefaces with usage rules, photography style guide (lighting, composition, subject treatment), graphic element library, and motion behavior rules for animated assets.
- **Template suite:** 5-8 Figma templates covering all required deliverables (social media carousel, email hero, landing page hero, display ads, presentation deck). Each template uses real copy, not lorem ipsum.
- **Quality reference board:** 3 examples of final composed assets demonstrating the system executed at the highest standard.

**Why this is good:**
- Translates business objectives into actionable creative parameters, not abstract mood words.
- Gives the design team a complete operational system -- they can start designing immediately without interpretation gaps.
- Anticipates the full delivery ecosystem (social, email, web, ads, deck) so the brand feels unified across every touchpoint.
- Includes a quality reference board that says "this is the bar" -- preventing gradual quality erosion during production.
- Uses real copy, which catches layout problems early (lorem ipsum hides text length issues).

### Example B -- Monthly Brand Compliance Report

**"{{COMPANY_NAME}} Brand Compliance Audit -- {{MONTH}} {{YEAR}}"**

Opening with an executive summary: "This month, 94% of audited assets passed automated brand compliance checks (target: 95%). The 6% failure rate concentrated in social media graphics produced via self-serve templates. Root cause: an outdated Instagram Stories template was circulating. Corrected template deployed on [DATE]. All non-compliant assets replaced within 24 hours."

The report includes:
- Compliance score trend line over 6 months (showing improvement from 88% to 94%).
- Breakdown by channel: Web (98%), Email (97%), Social (89%), Print (100%), Partner/Co-branded (91%).
- Top 3 violation types: incorrect logo spacing (12 instances), off-brand color (8 instances), unapproved typography (5 instances).
- Heatmap of compliance by team/creator to distinguish between individual training issues and systemic tool failures.
- Recommended actions with owners and deadlines.

**Why this is good:**
- Tells a story with data, not just a number -- trend lines, root causes, corrective actions.
- Distinguishes between human error (training fix) and system failure (tool fix), preventing blame for broken processes.
- Names specific corrective actions with owners and deadlines, making the report immediately actionable.
- Credits improvement alongside flagging failures, which builds trust and avoids a "gotcha" dynamic.

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A -- The "Make It Pop" Creative Direction

A stakeholder submits a creative brief: "Make it pop. You know, more modern. Like Apple but for {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}}. We'll know it when we see it." The designer produces three completely different direction options over two weeks, each chasing a different interpretation of "make it pop." The project goes 4x over its time budget. The stakeholder remains unsatisfied.

**Why this fails:**
- The brief contains zero objective criteria for success. "Make it pop" is not creative direction; it is an emotional reaction waiting to happen.
- The designer tried to solve a briefing problem with design exploration, which is inefficient and demoralizing.
- Nobody intervened to stop the loop and force the stakeholder to articulate concrete requirements.
- The reference ("Like Apple") is aspirational but meaningless without specifying what aspects of Apple's design language apply.

**How to fix:**
- Reject the brief at SOP 9.1. Require the stakeholder to provide: 3 specific adjectives describing desired tone, 2 competitor/aspirational examples with annotations of what works, and 1 concrete business metric the creative should influence.
- If the stakeholder cannot articulate these, CDO convenes a 20-minute discovery session to extract them before any design work begins.
- Never let a designer start work on a vague brief. The cost of a rejected brief is minutes; the cost of chasing vague feedback is days.

### Anti-Pattern B -- The Orphaned Brand Deviation

A junior designer decides the official brand typeface "feels dated" and substitutes a trendy Google Font alternative. The asset passes peer review, gets approved by a product manager who does not check brand compliance, and ships to the live website. For three weeks, the product page uses an unapproved typeface. A customer emails asking if {{COMPANY_NAME}} has rebranded.

**Why this fails:**
- Individual creative preference overrode brand governance. Designers are stewards of a shared system, not free agents.
- The brand compliance check (Quality Gate 1) was skipped or performed superficially.
- No automated brand governance monitoring caught the deviation before it went live.

**How to fix:**
- Reinforce with the entire Graphics team: personal aesthetic preferences are subordinate to the brand system. If a designer believes a brand element needs updating, they propose it through the design system change request process -- not by unilaterally deviating.
- Enable automated brand compliance checks in design tools so designers get real-time feedback when using unapproved fonts, colors, or logo treatments.
- Implement automated daily brand scans on all web properties (see SOP 9.6).

## 15. Common Mistakes (Pre-Empted)

| # | Mistake | Root Cause | Prevention |
|---|---------|------------|------------|
| 1 | **Prioritizing aesthetics over effectiveness.** Designing beautiful work that does not drive the intended business outcome (clicks, conversions, recall). | Designer identity tied to craft, not business metrics. Briefs lack success criteria. | Every creative brief MUST include a measurable success metric. Design critiques evaluate both craft quality AND business problem-solving. |
| 2 | **Approval by committee.** Routing creative to too many stakeholders, collecting contradictory feedback, attempting to satisfy everyone -- producing diluted, generic work. | Lack of a clear decider role. Fear of excluding stakeholders drives over-inclusion. | The creative brief designates ONE decider per project. Other stakeholders are "consulted," not "approving." CDO enforces this. |
| 3 | **Designing in a vacuum.** Creating work without understanding context -- where the asset lives, what surrounds it, what the user was doing before, what action should follow. | Briefs specify deliverables but not context. Designers do not ask. | Every brief must include a "context map": where this asset appears, adjacent content, user state before/after. |
| 4 | **Tool inertia.** Clinging to familiar tools while competitors adopt AI-assisted workflows that produce higher volume at acceptable quality. | Comfort with known tools. No formal process for evaluating and adopting new tools. | Quarterly Tool Audit (Section 6) assesses tool vitality. CDO pilots at least one new tool per quarter. |
| 5 | **Brand rigidity suffocating creativity.** Applying guidelines so strictly that every asset looks identical -- the brand feels monotonous and lifeless. | Brand governance framed as "rule enforcement" rather than "creative enablement." | Brand guidelines include a documented "flex zone": explicit areas where designers have creative freedom (imagery style, composition, graphic treatment). |
| 6 | **Undocumented design rationale.** Making creative decisions without documenting reasoning. When questioned later, nobody can explain why. | Time pressure. Documentation habit never established. | Every major creative decision documented in one sentence in the project file. Creates institutional memory that educates future designers and defends decisions during review. |

## 17. Edge Cases for This Role

### Edge Case 17.1 -- The Owner's Family Member Requests Design Work

- **Trigger:** A family member or personal contact of the owner bypasses standard intake and directly requests design work (party invitation, personal business card, side-hustle logo).
- **Action:** Politely acknowledge but do NOT commit. Route the request to the Master Orchestrator: "Personal design request received from [NAME]. This is outside standard business scope. Please advise whether to proceed and how to bill/prioritize." If authorized, treat as a standard project (SOP 9.1-9.4) with the same quality bar. Track time spent; personal projects should not consume more than 5% of any designer's weekly capacity without explicit owner approval.
- **Escalate to:** Master Orchestrator. The human owner makes the final call.

### Edge Case 17.2 -- Competitor Copies {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s Visual Identity

- **Trigger:** Discovery that a competitor has adopted visual elements substantially similar to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s brand identity (color palette, typography pairing, illustration style, overall creative direction).
- **Action:** Document evidence with dated screenshots and side-by-side comparisons. Assess severity: minor similarity (common industry aesthetic) vs. deliberate copy that could confuse customers. If minor: note in competitive intelligence file, no immediate action. If deliberate and confusing: prepare brief for Director of Legal with evidence. Do NOT publicly call out the competitor on social media. Accelerate next brand differentiation work to widen visual gap.
- **Escalate to:** Director of Legal (legal options), Master Orchestrator (strategic response), human owner if legal action is considered.

### Edge Case 17.3 -- A-List Designer Wants to Break Brand Rules for "Creative Reasons"

- **Trigger:** A highly talented senior designer argues passionately that a specific project requires breaking an established brand guideline to achieve creative excellence.
- **Action:** Listen fully. Ask: "What specific rule is limiting this work? What would the work gain by breaking it? What would the brand lose?" Evaluate on merits. If you agree: grant a documented exception with scope, duration, and alternative guidance. The exception does not change the permanent guideline. If you disagree: explain reasoning; emphasize that the brand system is the team's collective creative achievement. Challenge the designer to achieve excellence within the system.
- **Escalate to:** Department Director only if the designer refuses to accept "no" -- then it becomes a performance conversation.

### Edge Case 17.4 -- Stakeholder Gives Contradictory Feedback Between Rounds

- **Trigger:** A stakeholder approves direction in Round 1, then in Round 2 requests changes contradicting their earlier approval (e.g., "Love the minimalist approach" becomes "It feels empty -- add more visual elements").
- **Action:** Designer flags the contradiction to CDO immediately. CDO contacts the stakeholder directly with documented feedback from both rounds and schedules a 10-minute call. Identify what changed: new input from their manager? Competitor work that shifted expectations? Misunderstanding of Round 1 direction? Reach a clear, documented creative direction agreement. May reset the revision count. If pattern repeats with same stakeholder, CDO addresses in a stakeholder management conversation.
- **Escalate to:** Master Orchestrator if stakeholder's manager needs involvement for feedback pattern resolution.

### Edge Case 17.5 -- AI Tool Produces Work That Passes All Gates but "Feels Soulless"

- **Trigger:** An AI-generated asset passes every technical and brand compliance check but multiple team members feel it lacks the human touch -- correct but uninspired.
- **Action:** Take the concern seriously. Convene a brief critique (3-4 senior designers) to articulate what is missing: overly symmetrical composition (AI tendency), flat lighting, unnatural expressions, mechanically perfect typography. Determine whether human refinement can fix the issue or the asset should be recreated manually. Document specific "soulless" indicators so future AI-assisted work can be preemptively corrected. Update SOP 9.7 with guidance (e.g., "AI-generated compositions must be asymmetrically adjusted before submission").
- **Escalate to:** Not escalated unless it becomes a recurring pattern indicating systemic AI quality issues requiring tool or training intervention.

## 19. Sub-Specialists (Named Roles Within the Graphics Department)

The Chief Design Officer oversees a department that includes these specialized sub-roles. Each has its own how-to.md in the role library and operates semi-autonomously within the framework set by the CDO.

### 19.1 -- Brand Identity Architect

This specialist owns the codification, maintenance, and evolution of the {{COMPANY_NAME}} visual identity system. They maintain the brand guidelines, design token library, logo usage rules, color system, typography hierarchy, and imagery standards. They are the final authority on "is this on-brand?" for any visual element before it reaches the CDO. When a brand refresh is commissioned, the Brand Identity Architect leads the research-phase audit (competitor analysis, customer perception, asset inventory) and presents strategic options. They are the primary liaison between design and engineering for design-to-code token translation using Figma variables and Style Dictionary.

**Key competencies:** Design systems architecture, token-based brand management, visual identity design, accessibility standards (WCAG 2.1 AA), cross-platform brand consistency.
**Reports to:** Chief Design Officer.
**Collaborates with:** Front-end engineering, Product Design, Marketing brand team.

### 19.2 -- Creative Operations (DesignOps) Lead

This specialist owns the creative production pipeline: intake, workflow orchestration, resource management, tool administration, and KPI tracking. They configure and maintain the project management platform, DAM, creative review/proofing system, and brand governance monitoring tools. They produce weekly capacity and utilization reports, track KPIs (on-time delivery, revision rounds, throughput), and identify process bottlenecks before they become crises. The DesignOps Lead is the first responder for any tool outage or workflow failure and manages the team's tool stack with an emphasis on integration and automation.

**Key competencies:** Creative operations management, workflow design and optimization, DAM administration, capacity planning, data analysis and dashboarding, tool integration and API management.
**Reports to:** Chief Design Officer.
**Collaborates with:** All Graphics department members, IT (tool procurement and integration), Marketing Operations.

### 19.3 -- Visual Storytelling Director

This specialist owns the narrative dimension of visual design: how images, motion, composition, and sequence tell the {{COMPANY_NAME}} story across campaigns and channels. They direct the photographic and illustrative style of the brand -- the "look and feel" beyond the logo and color palette. They oversee photoshoot art direction, illustration style guides, motion design language, and video creative direction. For major campaigns, they construct the visual narrative arc: what the audience sees first, next, and how the sequence builds to the desired emotional and behavioral response.

**Key competencies:** Art direction, photography and videography direction, motion design, narrative composition, campaign creative strategy, mood board and style frame creation.
**Reports to:** Chief Design Officer.
**Collaborates with:** Content team (narrative alignment), Video production, External creative vendors (photographers, illustrators, animators).

### 19.4 -- AI-Enhanced Design Specialist

This specialist owns the integration of AI tools into the Graphics department's creative workflow. They evaluate, pilot, and train the team on AI design tools (Adobe Firefly, Midjourney, DALL-E, Runway). They develop and maintain the department's AI usage guidelines: which tasks are appropriate for AI assistance, which require full human creation, and how AI-assisted work must be disclosed and reviewed. They maintain the prompt library and best-practice documentation so AI becomes a force multiplier for the entire team, not just a tool for early adopters. They monitor AI tool developments monthly and recommend additions to or retirements from the tool stack (Section 8).

**Key competencies:** AI prompt engineering for visual design, AI tool evaluation and benchmarking, creative workflow optimization via automation, team training and enablement, understanding of AI ethics and IP considerations in visual content.
**Reports to:** Chief Design Officer.
**Collaborates with:** All Graphics department designers, Creative Operations Lead (tool integration), Director of Legal (AI IP and usage rights policies).
