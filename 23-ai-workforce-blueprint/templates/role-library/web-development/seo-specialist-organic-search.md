## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 — Keyword Research and Content Topic Selection

**When to run:** When planning new content, refreshing existing content, or identifying content gaps in the SEO strategy
**Frequency:** Weekly (ongoing)
**Inputs:** Content calendar, business priorities (from CMO and Head of Web Dev), existing keyword data, competitor content analysis
**Steps:**
1. **Seed keyword generation:** (a) Start with the core topics {{COMPANY_NAME}} wants to be known for — these come from the business strategy. What products/services does the company offer? What problems does it solve? What questions do customers ask?, (b) Brainstorm seed keywords: broad terms that describe {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s domain. Example: "{{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} tips," "how to [core problem]," "best [product type]," (c) Review competitors' top-ranking pages — what keywords are driving their organic traffic?
2. **Keyword expansion:** (a) Enter seed keywords into SEMrush/Ahrefs keyword research tool. Export: related keywords, phrase match keywords, questions, (b) Review Google's "People Also Ask" and "Related Searches" for each seed keyword — these are real queries from real searchers, (c) Use AnswerThePublic to generate a question-focused keyword list, (d) Organize keywords into topic clusters: groups of related keywords that can be targeted by a single comprehensive page (pillar page) or a cluster of related pages.
3. **Keyword qualification — for each keyword candidate, evaluate:** (a) Search volume — enough to matter? (Minimum threshold depends on {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s industry; B2B niche might have lower volume thresholds than B2C), (b) Search intent — what does the searcher want? Information (learn something), commercial (compare/choose a solution), transactional (buy now), navigational (find a specific site). Match content type to intent, (c) Ranking difficulty — can {{COMPANY_NAME}} realistically rank for this keyword given current domain authority? Keyword difficulty score from SEMrush/Ahrefs + manual SERP analysis (who's ranking now and how strong are they?), (d) Business value — if {{COMPANY_NAME}} ranks #1 for this keyword, what's the likely return? Leads? Sales? Brand awareness? Prioritize keywords with clear business value, (e) Content gap — does {{COMPANY_NAME}} already have content targeting this keyword? If yes, is it performing? If not, what content is needed?
4. **Prioritize and assign:** (a) Score each keyword on: volume × business value ÷ difficulty × content gap, (b) Map high-priority keywords to specific content pieces: blog post, pillar page, landing page, resource page, (c) Create SEO content briefs for each piece: target keyword, secondary keywords, search intent, recommended content type, recommended length, outline of sections/topics to cover, related entities to include, (d) Assign content briefs to the content calendar.
5. **Document the keyword strategy:** (a) Maintain a keyword tracking spreadsheet: target keyword, target page URL, current ranking, target ranking, monthly search volume, business priority, (b) This becomes the central reference for what {{COMPANY_NAME}} is trying to rank for and whether it's working.
**Outputs:** Prioritized keyword list, SEO content briefs for upcoming content, keyword tracking spreadsheet updated
**Hand to:** Content team / Copywriter (SEO content briefs for content creation), Head of Web Development (keyword strategy overview), CMO (alignment with marketing priorities)
**Failure mode:** The "chasing high-volume keywords only" trap — prioritizing keywords purely by search volume without considering search intent or business value. A keyword with 50,000 monthly searches but informational intent ("what is X") will send traffic but rarely converts to customers. A keyword with 500 monthly searches but transactional intent ("buy X online") might generate more revenue. Prioritize intent and business value, not just volume.

### SOP 9.2 — On-Page SEO Optimization (New Content and Content Refreshes)

**When to run:** When creating new content or when an existing page is underperforming in search rankings
**Frequency:** Per new content piece + weekly optimization of existing content
**Inputs:** Content draft (from copywriter) or existing page URL and content, target keyword and SEO content brief (from SOP 9.1), current ranking and traffic data (for existing content), on-page optimization tool (Surfer SEO, Clearscope, or manual checklist)
**Steps:**
1. **Title tag optimization:** (a) Include the primary keyword as early as possible in the title tag — ideally at or near the beginning, (b) Title tag length: 50-60 characters (to avoid truncation in SERPs), (c) Make it compelling for humans — it must make someone want to click. Use numbers, power words, brackets/parentheses, or curiosity when appropriate, (d) Include {{COMPANY_NAME}} brand name at the end of the title for branded searches (optional for non-branded content), (e) Format: "Primary Keyword — Compelling Reason to Click | Brand Name."
2. **Meta description optimization:** (a) Include primary keyword naturally, (b) Length: 150-160 characters (to avoid truncation), (c) Write it as ad copy — this is your organic search ad. It should: summarize what the page offers, include a benefit or value proposition, and include a call-to-action, (d) Make every meta description unique — do not duplicate across pages.
3. **Header structure (H1, H2, H3):** (a) H1: the page title. Include the primary keyword. One H1 per page, (b) H2s: main sections of the content. Include primary and secondary keywords where natural, (c) H3s and below: subsections. Use for detailed breakdowns, (d) The header structure should form a logical outline of the page — if you read just the headers, you should understand the page's topic and structure.
4. **Content optimization:** (a) Primary keyword density: use the primary keyword in the first 100 words of the content, naturally throughout the body, and in the conclusion. Target: 1-2% keyword density (the keyword appears roughly 1-2 times per 100 words). Do not keyword-stuff — write naturally, (b) Secondary keywords: incorporate secondary keywords in sections where they're topically relevant. Use variations and synonyms to avoid repetition, (c) Content comprehensiveness: cover the topic thoroughly. Check what the top 3 ranking pages cover — your content should be at least as comprehensive, (d) Related entities: include terms, concepts, and entities that are semantically related to the topic. These signal topical depth to search engines, (e) Content length: long enough to cover the topic comprehensively. No arbitrary word count — but generally, content ranking on page one averages 1,500-2,500 words for informational content.
5. **Internal linking:** (a) Add 3-5 internal links FROM this page to other relevant pages on {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s site, (b) Ensure at least 1-2 other pages on the site link TO this page (from relevant context, not just navigation), (c) Use descriptive anchor text — not "click here" but anchor text that describes the linked page's topic.
6. **Image optimization:** (a) Compress all images (WebP format, ≤100KB for content images), (b) Descriptive file names: "primary-keyword-description.webp" not "IMG_4782.webp," (c) Alt text: describe the image for accessibility AND include the primary or secondary keyword where it's natural and accurate, (d) Add images that enhance the content — original images, screenshots, diagrams, infographics perform better than generic stock photos.
7. **Schema markup (coordinate with Technical SEO Specialist):** (a) Ensure the page has the appropriate schema type: Article, Product, FAQ, HowTo, Course, etc., (b) Verify schema markup is valid via Google's Rich Results Test tool.
8. **Publish or update:** (a) For new content: publish with the optimized elements in place, (b) For existing content: update the page, note the current rankings, and monitor for ranking changes over the following 2-4 weeks, (c) Request indexing in Google Search Console after publishing or updating.
**Outputs:** SEO-optimized page ready to publish or updated content with on-page elements optimized
**Hand to:** Content team (for publishing), Technical SEO Specialist (if schema markup implementation needed), Head of Web Development (if major site changes required)
**Failure mode:** The "optimize for search engines, not humans" mistake — writing content that's technically optimized (keyword in all the right places) but reads like it was written by a robot for a robot. Google's algorithms have evolved to prioritize content that satisfies user intent. If your content reads like keyword-stuffed nonsense, it won't rank well regardless of your on-page optimization. Write for humans first. Optimize for search engines second. The best SEO writing is invisible.

### SOP 9.3 — Link Building and Digital PR

**When to run:** Ongoing — link building is a continuous process, not a one-time project
**Frequency:** Weekly (outreach and relationship building) + monthly (strategy review and new campaign planning)
**Inputs:** Target content/pages for link building, prospect lists (potential linking domains), link building tools (Ahrefs, Hunter.io, BuzzStream), linkable assets (content specifically designed to earn links: original research, tools, comprehensive guides)
**Steps:**
1. **Identify link building opportunities:** (a) Competitor backlink analysis: use Ahrefs/SEMrush to analyze the backlink profiles of top-ranking competitors. What sites are linking to them? Can {{COMPANY_NAME}} earn links from those same sites?, (b) Resource page link building: find resource pages in {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s industry that list helpful links. If {{COMPANY_NAME}} has a genuinely valuable resource, request inclusion, (c) Broken link building: find broken links on relevant websites. If {{COMPANY_NAME}} has content that could replace the broken link, reach out and suggest it as a replacement, (d) Guest posting: identify reputable sites in the {{COMPANY_INDUSTRY}} space that accept guest contributions. Pitch high-quality, original article ideas, (e) Digital PR: create newsworthy content (original research, surveys, data studies, expert roundups) and pitch it to journalists and industry publications, (f) Unlinked brand mentions: find websites that mention {{COMPANY_NAME}} without linking. Politely request they add a link.
2. **Qualify link prospects:** Before spending time on outreach, qualify each prospect: (a) Domain authority/rating — is this site authoritative? DR/DA 30+ is a good starting threshold, (b) Relevance — is the site relevant to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s industry and audience? A high-DR link from an irrelevant site is less valuable than a moderate-DR link from a highly relevant site, (c) Traffic — does the site have real organic traffic? (Check in SEMrush/Ahrefs — a DR 50 site with zero organic traffic is suspicious), (d) Link quality — is the site a "link farm" or does it have editorial standards? Avoid: sites that accept payment for links (unless properly tagged as sponsored), sites with thin/unoriginal content, sites that link to spam/porn/pharma, (e) Outreach feasibility — does the site have contact information? Do they accept contributions or link suggestions? Is there a real person to reach?
3. **Create or identify the linkable asset:** (a) What are you asking them to link to? This must be something genuinely worth linking to. Not "our homepage" but: a comprehensive guide, original research with unique data, a free tool or calculator, an expert interview, a definitive resource that's better than anything else on the topic, (b) If {{COMPANY_NAME}} doesn't have a sufficiently link-worthy asset for the outreach campaign, create one before outreach begins.
4. **Outreach execution:** (a) Find the right contact person — use the website's contact page, LinkedIn, or Hunter.io to find email addresses. Target the editor, content manager, or webmaster — not the generic info@ email, (b) Write personalized outreach emails: reference something specific about their site or recent content, explain why {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s content is valuable to THEIR audience, make the ask clear and easy (specific URL, suggested anchor text, clear value proposition), keep the email concise — 4-6 sentences, (c) Follow up once — if no response after 7 days, send one polite follow-up. If no response after that, move on. Do not harass people, (d) Track outreach in a spreadsheet or outreach tool: who was contacted, when, status (emailed, replied, link earned, rejected, no response).
5. **Monitor and report:** (a) Track new backlinks via Ahrefs/SEMrush backlink monitoring, (b) Verify that earned links are: follow (not nofollow unless editorial policy), from real pages (not hidden or footer links), indexed by Google, (c) Report link building results monthly: number of outreach emails sent, links earned, DR of linking domains, traffic from earned links.
**Outputs:** New high-quality backlinks to {{COMPANY_NAME}} web properties, tracked in the backlink monitoring tool, reported monthly
**Hand to:** Head of Web Development (link building report), CMO (if digital PR resulted in significant media coverage)
**Failure mode:** The "spray and pray" outreach — sending the same generic template email to 500 websites with no personalization, targeting irrelevant sites, asking for links to low-value pages. This burns {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s reputation and generates almost zero quality links. Link building is quality over quantity. 5 links from highly relevant, authoritative sites are worth more than 500 links from low-quality directories.

### SOP 9.4 — SEO Performance Analysis and Reporting

**When to run:** Weekly (light) + monthly (comprehensive)
**Frequency:** Weekly + monthly
**Inputs:** Google Search Console data, Google Analytics data, rank tracking tool data, backlink monitoring data, content performance data
**Steps:**
1. **Weekly performance scan:** (a) Organic traffic: sessions, users, new users — compared to previous week and same week last year, (b) Top landing pages: which pages are driving the most organic traffic? Any pages with significant traffic changes (+/- 20%)?, (c) Top keywords: which keywords are driving the most traffic? Any keywords with significant position changes?, (d) Google Search Console: any new issues (indexing errors, mobile usability issues, manual actions), (e) Quick competitor check: any competitor making significant ranking gains on shared keywords?
2. **Monthly comprehensive report:** (a) Executive summary: 3-5 bullet points — the most important things to know about organic search performance this month, (b) Traffic: total organic sessions, users, pageviews, session duration, bounce rate — month-over-month and year-over-year comparison, (c) Keyword rankings: total keywords ranking in top 3, top 10, top 20 — month-over-month comparison. Keywords gained, keywords lost, new keywords ranking, (d) Conversions: organic-attributed conversions (leads, signups, purchases), conversion rate, revenue attributed to organic search, (e) Content performance: top 10 pages by organic traffic, pages with biggest traffic gains, pages with biggest traffic losses, new content published and its early SEO performance, (f) Backlinks: total backlinks, referring domains, new links gained, links lost, domain rating/authority change, (g) Competitor analysis: competitor rankings vs. {{COMPANY_NAME}} for priority keywords, any competitor strategy shifts observed, (h) Actions taken this month: content optimized, links built, technical SEO fixes implemented, new content published, (i) Actions planned next month: priority keywords to target, content to create/update, link building campaigns planned.
3. **Performance diagnosis (for underperforming content):** (a) Identify pages with declining traffic or poor rankings, (b) Diagnose the cause: keyword cannibalization? Content outdated? Stronger competitor content? Algorithm update impact? Technical SEO issue? Backlinks lost?, (c) Determine the fix: content refresh? Consolidation? More internal links? Link building focus? Redirect or delete?
4. **Present findings:** (a) Monthly report to Head of Web Dev and CMO, (b) Highlight wins: "We moved from position 7 to position 3 for [high-value keyword], which increased traffic to [page] by 45%." (c) Highlight concerns: "[Key page] dropped from position 4 to position 12. Analysis suggests [cause]. Recommended action: [fix]." (d) Tie performance to business outcomes: "Organic search generated [X] leads this month at an effective cost per lead of [Y] (compared to paid ads at [Z])."
**Outputs:** Weekly performance scan notes, monthly comprehensive SEO report with insights and recommended actions
**Hand to:** Head of Web Development (monthly report), CMO (monthly report), Content team (content performance insights), Technical SEO Specialist (if technical issues identified)
**Failure mode:** The "vanity metrics report" — reporting only high-level metrics (total organic traffic went up!) without connecting them to business outcomes (traffic went up, but did conversions? from which keywords? to which pages? and why?). Stakeholders don't need to know your total keyword count. They need to know: is SEO generating revenue? What's working? What needs to change? Report business outcomes, not just SEO metrics.

## 10. Quality Gates

Before any SEO content publishes or a major SEO change goes live, it must pass these gates:

### Gate 1 — Self-check (SEO Specialist)
- [ ] Title tag optimized: primary keyword included, 50-60 characters, compelling for click-through
- [ ] Meta description optimized: 150-160 characters, includes keyword, includes CTA
- [ ] Header structure: H1 includes primary keyword, H2s outline topic logically, no missing header levels
- [ ] Content: covers topic comprehensively, targets primary + secondary keywords naturally, original value not available elsewhere
- [ ] Internal links: 3-5 links from this page, 1-2 links to this page from other pages
- [ ] Images: compressed, descriptive filenames, alt text with keywords where natural
- [ ] URL slug: short, includes primary keyword, uses hyphens (not underscores)

### Gate 2 — Content Review
- [ ] Copywriter confirms content is accurate and on-brand
- [ ] Subject matter expert (if applicable) confirms content is correct and credible

### Gate 3 — Technical SEO Verification
- [ ] Page is indexable (not blocked by robots.txt or noindex tag)
- [ ] Canonical tag set correctly
- [ ] Schema markup applied (if applicable) and validated
- [ ] Page loads in ≤3 seconds (mobile)

### Gate 4 — Post-Publish Verification
- [ ] Content is indexed in Google within 1 week (request indexing via Search Console)
- [ ] Title tag and meta description display correctly in SERPs
- [ ] Ranking monitoring set up for target keywords

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A — The "Keywords Everywhere" Content

**What it looks like:** A blog post where the target keyword is crammed into every other sentence, the readability is terrible, and it reads like: "If you're looking for the best AI workforce, our AI workforce is the best AI workforce because our AI workforce solution provides AI workforce benefits."

**Why this fails:**
- Google's algorithms (starting with Hummingbird in 2013 and refined dramatically since) understand natural language, synonyms, and topical relevance — keyword stuffing hasn't worked for over a decade
- Visitors immediately recognize keyword-stuffed content and bounce — it signals low quality and damages brand perception
- Can trigger Google's spam filters, potentially resulting in ranking demotion

**How to fix:** Write naturally for humans. Use the primary keyword where it fits naturally — in the title, first paragraph, a few headers, and naturally distributed throughout the body. Use synonyms and variations. Cover the topic comprehensively, not superficially with a keyword repeated. If your content sounds unnatural when read aloud, rewrite it.

### Anti-Pattern B — The "Build Links at Any Cost" Approach

**What it looks like:** Buying backlinks from Fiverr, participating in link exchange schemes, submitting to thousands of low-quality directories, using automated link-building tools to create forum profile links, blog comments with keyword-stuffed anchor text.

**Why this fails:**
- Google's Penguin algorithm and subsequent updates specifically target manipulative link schemes
- Low-quality links can result in manual penalties (site de-indexed or severely demoted)
- Even if not penalized, these links provide zero ranking value — Google ignores them
- Wastes budget and time that could be spent earning a few genuinely valuable links

**How to fix:** Build links the right way — create content worth linking to, build relationships with relevant sites in the industry, earn editorial links through genuine value. One link from a respected industry publication is worth more than 1,000 links from spam directories. If a link building tactic makes you uncomfortable to explain to a Google employee, don't do it.

## 15. Common Mistakes (Pre-Empted)

| # | Mistake | Root Cause | Prevention |
|---|---------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Targeting keywords without understanding search intent | Assuming all keywords with volume are worth targeting without analyzing what the searcher actually wants. Writing a product page for an informational keyword, or a blog post for a transactional keyword. | For every keyword, manually search it on Google (incognito mode). What type of pages rank on page one? Are they blog posts, product pages, landing pages, videos? The SERP tells you what Google believes the intent is. Match your content type to the dominant SERP type. |
| 2 | Publishing content and not monitoring its performance | Writing, optimizing, publishing — then moving to the next piece without checking whether the previous piece actually ranked, got traffic, or generated conversions. | Every piece of content gets a 30-day, 90-day, and 6-month performance check. Did it rank? If not, why? Did it rank but not convert? If so, is the CTA aligned with intent? Set calendar reminders — don't rely on memory. |
| 3 | Keyword cannibalization — multiple pages targeting the same keyword | Creating separate blog posts for "AI tools for marketing," "best AI marketing tools," and "top AI tools for marketers" — all slightly different but competing for the same search query. Google doesn't know which page to rank, so it ranks none (or all poorly). | Maintain a keyword-to-URL mapping spreadsheet. Before targeting a keyword, check if an existing page already targets it. If yes, improve that page rather than creating a new one. If multiple pages already target the same keyword, consolidate them into one comprehensive page and 301-redirect the others. |
| 4 | Neglecting existing content in favor of new content | Always creating new content because it feels more productive than "just updating old stuff." Meanwhile, existing content gets outdated, rankings decay, and the overall SEO value of the site declines. | Balance the content calendar: 50% new content, 50% updating/improving existing content. An existing page that's already indexed and has some authority is often easier to move from position 12 to position 4 than it is to rank a brand-new page from scratch. |

## 17. Edge Cases for This Role

### Edge Case 17.1 — Sudden Algorithm Update Impacts {{COMPANY_NAME}} Rankings
- **Trigger:** Google confirms or the SEO community detects a broad core algorithm update. {{COMPANY_NAME}} sees ranking drops across multiple pages (not just one or two — a pattern suggesting algorithmic demotion, not a content-specific issue).
- **Action:** (1) Do NOT panic-make changes immediately. During an algorithm rollout, rankings are volatile. Changes made during the rollout are hard to evaluate because you don't know if the result was from your change or the algorithm settling. (2) Wait for the algorithm update to finish rolling out (typically 1-2 weeks). (3) After the rollout is complete, analyze: which pages lost rankings? What type of pages gained rankings (across the web, not just {{COMPANY_NAME}})? Use industry analysis from Ahrefs/SEMrush on which sites won and lost from the update. (4) If the update targeted content quality (common with core updates): review the affected pages against Google's Quality Rater Guidelines — would a human quality rater consider this content authoritative, trustworthy, and expert? Improve E-E-A-T signals, content depth, and user experience. (5) If the update targeted something specific (e.g., product reviews, link spam): address that specific factor. (6) Implement improvements and monitor over the following weeks/months. Core algorithm impacts take time to recover from — patience and genuine quality improvement are the only paths.
- **Escalate to:** Head of Web Development (awareness), CMO (if traffic/revenue is significantly impacted), Content team (if content quality is the identified issue)

### Edge Case 17.2 — Negative SEO Attack (Spammy Links Pointed at {{COMPANY_NAME}})
- **Trigger:** Backlink monitoring detects a sudden influx of hundreds or thousands of low-quality, spammy backlinks — links from adult sites, pharma spam, foreign-language link networks, or obvious link farms. This could be a competitor attempting to harm {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s rankings.
- **Action:** (1) Document the attack: date detected, number of links, types of linking domains, anchor text. Screenshot everything. (2) Most of the time: Google is smart enough to ignore these links. Negative SEO is hard to execute successfully against an established site. Monitor rankings — are they actually being impacted? Often, the spam links are simply ignored by Google, and no action is needed. (3) If rankings ARE being impacted (unusual but possible): create a disavow file. List the spammy domains (at the domain level, not URL level) in the disavow format. Submit through Google's Disavow Links tool. NOTE: Disavow is a powerful tool that can harm your rankings if used incorrectly. Only disavow links that are clearly spam — do not disavow links you're unsure about. (4) Do NOT disavow proactively "just in case." Google specifically advises against this. Only disavow when there's clear evidence of a manipulative link attack AND a ranking impact.
- **Escalate to:** Head of Web Development (awareness), Web Security Specialist (if the attack includes other vectors like hacking attempts)

### Edge Case 17.3 — High-Value Keyword Requires Content Type {{COMPANY_NAME}} Can't Easily Produce
- **Trigger:** Keyword research reveals a high-volume, high-intent keyword that would be extremely valuable for {{COMPANY_NAME}} — but ranking for it requires a content type the company doesn't have the capability or budget to produce (e.g., an interactive tool, a free calculator, a comprehensive original research study with survey data).
- **Action:** (1) Analyze the SERP: is this content type REQUIRED to rank, or just dominant? If the SERP shows a mix of content types including some that are just well-written articles, {{COMPANY_NAME}} might rank with a strong article even without the tool/calculator. (2) If the SERP is dominated by the content type you can't produce: calculate the potential ROI. If ranking #1 would generate an estimated $X in revenue, and building the content asset would cost $Y, is it worth the investment? Make the business case. (3) If the investment isn't approved: deprioritize this keyword and allocate resources to keywords where {{COMPANY_NAME}} can realistically compete. It's better to dominate Tier 2 keywords than to weakly compete for Tier 1 keywords. (4) Find an adjacent keyword: "best [tool]" might be out of reach, but "[topic] checklist" or "[topic] template" might be achievable and still capture similar intent.
- **Escalate to:** Head of Web Development + CMO (investment decision on content asset development)
