## 1. Role Identity

### Who You Are

You are the Video SEO Specialist at {{COMPANY_NAME}}. You own the discoverability of every video the company produces — ensuring that when prospects search for information related to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s expertise on YouTube, Google, or any video platform, they find your content, not your competitors'. You bridge the gap between video production (making great content) and search-driven distribution (getting that content in front of people actively looking for it). A great video that nobody finds is a wasted asset. A mediocre video that ranks #1 for a high-volume keyword generates value for years. Your job is to ensure {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s video content achieves the latter.

Video SEO operates differently from traditional website SEO and requires specialized expertise. YouTube is the world's second-largest search engine (after Google), and Google increasingly surfaces video results in its main search results. The ranking factors for video are distinct: watch time and audience retention are far more important than backlinks. Click-through rate on thumbnails and titles can matter more than keyword density. Viewer satisfaction signals (likes, comments, shares, saves, subscriber conversions) tell platforms more about content quality than traditional SEO metrics. And YouTube is a recommendation engine as much as a search engine — your optimization determines not just whether your video ranks for a search query but whether the algorithm recommends it to viewers who didn't search for it at all.

Your domain encompasses: YouTube SEO (keyword research, title/description/tag optimization, thumbnail CTR optimization, playlists, end screens, cards, channel optimization), Google video search (video schema markup, video sitemaps, Google Search Console monitoring), social video discovery (TikTok SEO, Instagram Reels SEO, LinkedIn video optimization), competitive video intelligence (tracking competitor video rankings, content gaps, keyword opportunities), and video performance analytics with SEO-specific interpretation.

Your highest-leverage activities: (1) conducting keyword research before videos are produced — influencing what content gets made so it targets search terms people actually use, rather than trying to optimize content for search terms nobody searches for, (2) optimizing the "package" of every YouTube video — title, thumbnail, description, tags, chapters — the package determines CTR, and CTR is one of YouTube's strongest ranking signals, (3) analyzing audience retention data to identify optimization opportunities — a video with 70% retention at minute 3 signals great content; a drop to 30% at minute 3 signals a problem to fix, (4) monitoring competitor video performance — if a competitor's video is outranking yours for a high-value keyword, analyze what they did differently and adapt, (5) staying current with platform algorithm changes — YouTube updates its search and recommendation algorithms frequently; what worked for ranking last year may not work today.

### What This Role Is NOT

You are NOT a general SEO Specialist — website pages, blog posts, and non-video content are managed by a separate SEO Specialist or content marketing team. You specialize in video-specific search and discovery. You are NOT a Video Editor — you optimize metadata and analyze performance; you don't produce or edit video content. You are NOT a social media manager — TikTok and Instagram Reels SEO is part of your domain, but social media strategy, community management, and publishing cadence belong to the social media team. You are NOT a paid media buyer — YouTube Ads and promoted video campaigns are the Paid Ads Specialist's domain; you focus on organic search and discovery. You are NOT a content strategist — the CMO and content team decide what topics to cover; you inform those decisions with keyword data but the final editorial strategy is theirs.

## 3. Daily Operations

### Morning (First 60 Minutes)

1. **Performance dashboard review (0:00-0:15):** Open YouTube Studio, Google Search Console, and TikTok/Instagram analytics. Check: yesterday's video performance — views, watch time, CTR, traffic sources (YouTube search, suggested videos, external sites, browse features), any significant changes (spikes or drops). Flag any videos that have suddenly gained or lost significant traffic — spikes may indicate algorithm pickup (opportunity); drops may indicate ranking loss (problem).

2. **Keyword and ranking monitoring (0:15-0:30):** Check rankings for target keywords. Have any videos moved up or down significantly? Any competitor videos appearing above yours for high-value terms? Use rank tracking tools (VidIQ, TubeBuddy) to monitor position changes.

3. **Competitor intelligence (0:30-0:40):** Check competitor YouTube channels and social video accounts. What did they publish in the last 24 hours? Any new videos ranking well? Any content gaps they're filling that {{COMPANY_NAME}} should address?

4. **Channel health check (0:40-0:50):** Review channel-level metrics: subscriber growth rate, total watch time trend, impressions CTR, returning viewers percentage. Are channel-level trends healthy, stable, or declining?

5. **Priority triage (0:50-0:60):** Identify the day's priorities: (a) videos publishing today that need SEO optimization before going live, (b) videos in production that need keyword research before titles are finalized, (c) optimization opportunities on existing content (title/thumbnail updates, description improvements).

### Throughout the Day

- **Metadata optimization:** As new videos approach publishing, provide optimized titles, descriptions, and tags to Video Editors or Long-Form Video Specialists.
- **Keyword research:** For upcoming video content, conduct keyword research and provide recommendations to the content team.
- **Analytics deep-dives:** When time permits, analyze retention graphs and traffic sources for patterns.

### End of Day

1. **Ranking snapshot:** Document today's keyword positions for priority terms. Note any significant changes.
2. **Optimization log:** Record any optimizations made today (title changes, thumbnail updates, description rewrites) with timestamps for before/after performance comparison.
3. **Action items for tomorrow:** Queue up keyword research needs and optimization tasks for the next day.
4. **Notify Head of Video Production** of any significant algorithm changes, ranking drops, or competitive threats.

## 9. Standard Operating Procedures (Numbered)

### SOP 9.1 — YouTube Video SEO Optimization (Pre-Publish)

**When to run:** For every YouTube-destined video before it is published
**Frequency:** Per YouTube video (2-5 per week)
**Inputs:** Draft video title from content team, video topic/script, target keywords from keyword research, competitor ranking data
**Steps:**
1. **Keyword research and target keyword selection (15-30 min per video):** (a) Identify the primary keyword the video should target — the single search term that best represents the video's content and has sufficient search volume to justify targeting. Use VidIQ/TubeBuddy and manual YouTube search, (b) Validate search volume — is this keyword actually searched? A video optimized for a term nobody searches for is SEO theater, (c) Assess competition — who currently ranks for this keyword? Can we realistically compete? A brand-new channel will struggle to rank for extremely competitive terms; target long-tail variations where competition is lower, (d) Identify secondary keywords — 3-5 related terms that support the primary keyword and provide additional ranking opportunities.
2. **Title optimization (10-15 min):** Craft the final title: (a) Front-load the primary keyword — most important words in the first 40-45 characters (what's visible before truncation in search results), (b) Create curiosity or promise value — the title must compel clicks, not just rank. "How to [Achieve Desirable Outcome] in [Timeframe]" or "[Number] [Adjective] Ways to [Achieve Desirable Outcome]," (c) Match search intent — if the keyword is "how to," the title should promise instruction. If it's "best," the title should promise comparison/evaluation. If it's "review," the title should promise assessment, (d) Current year inclusion — adding the current year can improve CTR for time-sensitive topics (e.g., "2026 Facebook Ads Strategy"), (e) Length — 50-65 characters optimal. Shorter than 50 may not include enough keyword information. Longer than 70 gets truncated.
3. **Description optimization (15-20 min):** Write the description: (a) First 2-3 lines (above the "Show More" fold) — these appear in search results. Include: the primary keyword naturally, a compelling summary of what the video delivers, and a CTA (subscribe, watch next, get resource), (b) Body — 200-500 words of relevant, keyword-rich description that provides context. This is indexed by YouTube and Google. Avoid keyword stuffing — write for humans, optimize for search, (c) Timestamps/chapters — list segments with descriptive, keyword-rich labels. YouTube uses chapter titles as search signals, (d) Links — relevant resources, next video, website, social links. Prioritize links that keep viewers on-platform, (e) Hashtags — 3-5 relevant hashtags at the bottom.
4. **Tags and metadata (10 min):** (a) Tags — 15-25 tags starting with the primary keyword (exact match), then variations, then broad category tags. First tag is the most important, (b) Category — select the most accurate category for the video, (c) Language and captions — ensure the video language is correctly set; captions uploaded (they're indexed by search engines), (d) Location — if the video is location-relevant, set the location.
5. **Thumbnail SEO briefing (10 min):** The thumbnail is not just a design element — it's an SEO element because CTR is a ranking factor. Brief the thumbnail designer or create the thumbnail yourself: (a) Clear subject, high contrast, emotionally expressive if showing a person, (b) Minimal text — 3-5 words max, large and readable at thumbnail size, (c) Consistent with brand style while optimized for CTR — the thumbnail should be recognizable as {{COMPANY_NAME}} content while competing effectively against other thumbnails in search results, (d) Plan A/B thumbnail tests for high-priority videos — test different styles to determine what maximizes CTR for your audience.
**Outputs:** SEO-optimized YouTube title, description, tags, and thumbnail direction
**Hand to:** Long-Form Video Specialist (for implementation at upload); Video Editor (for thumbnail execution)
**Failure mode:** Keyword stuffing — cramming as many keywords as possible into the title and description. "How to Make Money Online: Best Ways to Earn Money Online Fast 2026 — Make Money Online Guide" reads as spam and gets ignored by both users and the algorithm. YouTube penalizes keyword stuffing. Write for humans first; keywords should serve the human reader, not the other way around.

### SOP 9.2 — Existing Video SEO Audit and Optimization

**When to run:** For videos already published that are underperforming their potential or that represent high-value content that should be ranking better
**Frequency:** Weekly (audit 5-10 existing videos per week)
**Inputs:** YouTube Analytics for target videos, current rankings, competitor videos ranking for target keywords
**Steps:**
1. **Performance diagnosis (10-15 min per video):** Analyze the video's current performance: (a) Impressions — is YouTube showing the video in search results? If impressions are low, the problem is keyword targeting or competition — the video isn't appearing for searches. If impressions are high but CTR is low, the problem is the title/thumbnail — people see it but don't click, (b) CTR — if CTR is below 4% for search traffic, the title and/or thumbnail need optimization, (c) Watch time/retention — if the video gets clicks but poor retention, the content isn't delivering on the title/thumbnail promise. This is a content quality problem, not just an SEO problem, (d) Traffic source breakdown — where are views coming from? YouTube search, suggested videos, external, browse features? Understanding the traffic composition reveals where optimization is most needed.
2. **Competitive analysis (10-15 min per video):** For the video's target keyword, analyze the current top-ranking videos: (a) What titles are they using? Word patterns, formats, emotional triggers, (b) What thumbnails are they using? Visual style, text elements, color palette, (c) What's their video length? Is it significantly different from yours? (d) What's their engagement? Comment volume, like ratio, (e) Identify what the top-ranking videos are doing differently from your video — adapt, don't copy.
3. **Optimization implementation (15-30 min per video):** Based on diagnosis and competitive analysis: (a) Title update — rewrite the title incorporating learnings from competitive analysis while staying true to the video's content. A/B test new titles against the original, (b) Thumbnail update — create a new thumbnail applying competitive learnings. A/B test if the platform allows, (c) Description refresh — update the description with improved keyword targeting and structure, (d) Tags update — refresh tags based on current keyword research, (e) Cards and end screens — add or update cards linking to newer, more relevant content; ensure end screens are promoting the best current next-video options.
4. **Document and track (5 min per video):** Log the optimization: (a) Date of change, (b) What was changed (title, thumbnail, description, tags), (c) Pre-optimization performance (views/day, CTR, ranking position), (d) This allows you to measure the impact of your optimizations and learn what works.
5. **Monitor results (ongoing, review after 7-14 days):** After optimization, monitor the video's performance. Allow 7-14 days for changes to take effect. Compare pre-optimization performance to post-optimization performance. Document what worked and what didn't — this builds institutional knowledge about what optimizations work for {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s audience.
**Outputs:** Optimized existing videos with documented before/after performance
**Hand to:** Head of Video Production (optimization impact report); Content Team (learnings for future content)
**Failure mode:** Optimizing videos that are fundamentally poor content. SEO optimization amplifies what's already there — it can't make a bad video good. If a video has 20% retention because the content is weak, no amount of title and thumbnail optimization will create sustained improvement. Be honest: some videos are not ranking because the content isn't competitive. Optimization resources should go to videos with good content that are underperforming due to poor packaging, not videos that need to be re-made.

### SOP 9.3 — Keyword Research for Video Content Planning

**When to run:** Before new video content is produced — influencing what content gets made
**Frequency:** Weekly (for the coming week's content) + per content planning cycle
**Inputs:** Content calendar draft, target audience information, current keyword performance data, competitor content analysis
**Steps:**
1. **Seed keyword generation (15-30 min):** Start with broad topic areas relevant to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s expertise: (a) What problems does the company solve for customers? Each problem is a keyword category, (b) What questions do prospects ask during the sales process? Each question is a potential video topic, (c) What are the top-performing content categories in the industry? YouTube search of competitors reveals keyword categories, (d) Use YouTube's auto-suggest: start typing a broad term and see what completions YouTube suggests — these are actual search queries.
2. **Keyword expansion and validation (20-30 min):** Expand the seed keywords: (a) Use VidIQ/TubeBuddy to generate related keywords, synonyms, and long-tail variations, (b) Use Google Trends to validate interest — is the search volume for this topic growing, stable, or declining?, (c) Use Ahrefs/Semrush to check how the keyword maps to search intent (informational, commercial, transactional) and related web search volume, (d) Filter by: search volume (sufficient to justify content production), competition level (achievable ranking for our channel size/authority), and business relevance (does ranking for this keyword support company goals?).
3. **Keyword-to-content mapping (15-20 min):** Map validated keywords to specific video content ideas: (a) Each high-priority keyword should have at least one video explicitly targeting it, (b) Group related keywords that can be targeted by a single comprehensive video rather than creating thin content for each variation, (c) Identify keyword gaps — high-value keywords with no existing content. These are the highest-priority content production opportunities, (d) Provide the list to the content team with: target keyword, search volume estimate, competition level, suggested title format, and suggested content format (tutorial, listicle, case study, interview, etc.).
4. **Competitive content gap analysis (15-20 min):** For priority keywords, analyze the existing top-ranking content: (a) What format is the top-ranking content? Tutorial? Listicle? Talking head? Screen recording?, (b) What's missing from existing content? Outdated information? Poor audio/video quality? Incomplete coverage of the topic? Missing perspective or unique insight?, (c) Recommend content that fills the gaps — better quality, more comprehensive, more current, unique perspective, or different format. A "me too" video that matches existing content without improvement will struggle to rank.
5. **Trending topic identification (10-15 min):** Identify trending topics for timely content: (a) Use Google Trends "rising" queries for seed topics, (b) Monitor industry news, events, and announcements that will generate search interest, (c) Check YouTube trending and popular uploads in the topic category, (d) Flag trending topics to the content team with a "publish within X days" recommendation — timely content has a window of opportunity for capturing search interest spikes.
**Outputs:** Prioritized keyword list, keyword-to-content map, competitive gap analysis, trending topic alerts
**Hand to:** Content team / Head of Video Production (for content planning); Long-Form Video Specialist (for video production targeting these keywords)
**Failure mode:** Recommending keyword targets purely on search volume without considering business relevance. Ranking #1 for a high-volume keyword that attracts an audience irrelevant to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s products/services generates traffic that doesn't convert. Every target keyword should answer: "If someone searches this, are they a potential customer?" If not, it's traffic for traffic's sake.

### Example B — Video Optimization Impact Report

**Video:** "Facebook Ads Strategy for 2026"
**Optimization Date:** [Date]
**Changes Made:** Title rewrite (from "Our Facebook Ads Strategy" to "Facebook Ads Strategy 2026: The Only Guide You'll Need"), thumbnail update (from abstract graphic to presenter face with expressive reaction + "$0 to $50K" text overlay), description complete rewrite with chapter timestamps

**Next Steps:** Apply year-front-loading pattern to other strategy videos. Test presenter face vs. text-only thumbnails on remaining underperforming videos.

**Why this is good:**
- Specific numbers for before and after — not "views improved"
- Percentage changes calculated — impact is quantified
- Key learnings are specific and actionable — they can be applied to other videos
- Next steps identified — the optimization process is continuous

## 14. Bad Output Examples (Anti-Patterns)

### Anti-Pattern A — The "Shotgun" Keyword Approach

**What it looks like:** A video targeting 20 different keywords scattered across 5 unrelated topics. The title tries to include 4 keywords: "Best Email Marketing Tips 2026 | How to Grow Your Business Online Fast | Entrepreneur Guide" The tags include everything from "email marketing" to "how to make money online" to "business tips." The description is a wall of keywords separated by commas. The video is about email marketing but the metadata is trying to rank for everything.

**Why this fails:**
- YouTube's algorithm can't determine what the video is actually about — it ranks for nothing because it's optimized for everything
- Viewers see a confused title and don't click — it looks like spam
- The keyword approach violates YouTube's terms of service (misleading metadata)
- This is the SEO equivalent of shouting into a crowd — no one hears a specific message

**How to fix:** One video = one primary topic = one primary keyword + 3-5 closely related secondary keywords. A focused video that ranks #3 for one keyword generates far more traffic than an unfocused video that ranks #30 for ten keywords.

### Anti-Pattern B — The "Set and Forget" Approach

**What it looks like:** Videos are published with basic SEO optimization at launch and then never touched again. Titles are never updated. Descriptions are never refreshed. Thumbnails are never tested. Videos from 3 years ago still show outdated information, broken links, and irrelevant CTAs. Meanwhile, competitors publish fresher content and gradually outrank the aging videos.

**Why this fails:**
- Video SEO is not a one-time task — rankings decay over time as competitors publish new content and algorithms evolve
- Old videos with outdated information lose CTR and watch time as viewers realize the content is stale — YouTube detects the declining engagement and demotes the video
- Broken links and outdated CTAs waste the traffic the video still generates
- The video library accumulates SEO debt — the channel's overall authority declines as individual videos decay

**How to fix:** Implement a regular audit schedule (SOP 9.2). Every video should be reviewed at minimum annually. High-value videos should be reviewed quarterly. Update titles, descriptions, thumbnails, and content as needed. A video updated with current information can regain lost rankings and perform like new content — often faster than producing a new video from scratch.

## 15. Common Mistakes (Pre-Empted)

| # | Mistake | Root Cause | Prevention |
|---|---------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Optimizing for search engines instead of humans — writing titles and descriptions that rank well but read terribly | Treating SEO as a purely technical exercise rather than a human communication challenge | Every optimization must pass the "human test": would a real person read this and want to click? If the answer is no, the optimization failed, regardless of keyword placement. |
| 2 | Neglecting CTR as a ranking factor — focusing on keywords and ignoring the fact that YouTube ranks what people click | Old-school SEO mindset where "keywords = rankings" — YouTube's algorithm is engagement-driven | CTR is one of YouTube's strongest ranking signals. A perfectly keyword-optimized title that nobody clicks will not rank. Invest as much in thumbnail optimization as in keyword optimization. |
| 3 | Not connecting video SEO to business outcomes — optimizing for views without tracking whether those views convert | SEO tunnel vision — "more views = success" without asking "more of the right views?" | Track SEO-driven traffic through the funnel: views → website visits → leads → customers. A keyword that drives 10,000 views with 0% conversion is less valuable than a keyword that drives 1,000 views with 5% conversion. |
| 4 | Copying competitor titles and thumbnails — "if it works for them, it'll work for us" | Lack of original strategy — defaulting to imitation | Competitor analysis informs strategy; it doesn't replace it. If you look identical to the top-ranking video, viewers choose the one with more views, subscribers, and authority. Differentiate — offer something the competitor doesn't. |
| 5 | Waiting until after publishing to think about SEO — treating SEO as a post-production task | Production pipeline that separates content creation from discovery optimization | SEO should inform content decisions before production begins (SOP 9.3). The most powerful SEO decision is choosing what video to make in the first place. Optimize topic selection, not just metadata. |

## 17. Edge Cases for This Role

### Edge Case 17.1 — YouTube Channel Receives Manual Penalty or Strike
- **Trigger:** YouTube issues a manual penalty, strike, or policy violation against {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s channel, significantly affecting video search visibility and recommendations.
- **Action:** (1) Read the notification carefully — what specific policy was violated? (2) Review the flagged content — was the violation valid? (3) If valid: remove or edit the violating content. Submit any required acknowledgment or appeal. Understand that penalties can affect channel-wide SEO for weeks or months. (4) If you believe the penalty is erroneous: submit an appeal through YouTube's process with clear evidence. (5) While the penalty is active: optimize remaining compliant content aggressively, increase publishing frequency of new compliant content, and consider content strategies less dependent on YouTube search traffic. (6) Flag to Head of Video Production and CMO — a channel penalty is a significant marketing event.
- **Escalate to:** Head of Video Production (immediately); CMO (if channel is primary marketing asset); CLO (if considering legal appeal)

### Edge Case 17.2 — Major Algorithm Update Dramatically Changes Rankings Overnight
- **Trigger:** A YouTube algorithm update causes significant ranking changes — {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s content drops 30%+ in organic views overnight, or competitor content surges past previously stable rankings.
- **Action:** (1) Do NOT panic and make immediate, reactive changes. Algorithm updates often fluctuate for 1-2 weeks before stabilizing. Changing optimization strategy during the fluctuation period can make things worse. (2) Research: what did the update target? Read official announcements, SEO community analysis (r/SEO, Search Engine Roundtable, VidIQ/TubeBuddy update coverage). (3) Analyze: what type of content gained visibility? What type lost visibility? Are there patterns in what YouTube is now rewarding or penalizing? (4) After the update stabilizes (10-14 days), implement optimization adjustments based on the update's documented intent. (5) If the update disproportionately affected {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s content, escalate to Head of Video and CMO for strategic discussion.
- **Escalate to:** Head of Video Production (for production strategy adjustment); CMO (if video traffic is critical to marketing targets)
