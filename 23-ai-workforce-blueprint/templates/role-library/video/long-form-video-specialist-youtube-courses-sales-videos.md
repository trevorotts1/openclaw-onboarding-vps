## 9. SOPs

### SOP-01: YouTube Long-Form Video Production (Full Pipeline)

**Objective:** Produce a high-retention YouTube video from concept to published, following a repeatable 6-stage workflow that maximizes algorithmic performance.

**Steps:**
1. **Ideation and Research (30-60 min).** Identify the video topic. Use YouTube search autocomplete, vidIQ/TubeBuddy keyword tools, competitor channel analysis, and audience comment mining to validate demand. Check: does this topic have proven search volume? Can you offer a unique angle? IF the topic has zero search volume AND no competitor has successfully covered it, THEN reject the topic — you cannot create demand where none exists.
2. **Scriptwriting (2-4 hours).** Write the script using the SPEC framework: Setup (5-10% — context after hook), Points (70-80% — ordered strong, strong, medium, strong, medium, strongest), Evidence (a stat or example every ~200 words), Conclusion (10-15% — takeaway + "one more thing" bonus). Write the hook last — it should be the sharpest version of the idea. Target word count: ~150 words per minute of intended video length. Run the script through {{SCRIPT_ANALYSIS_TOOL}} for retention prediction. IF the predicted retention score is below 65, THEN rewrite the hook and weak sections before proceeding.
3. **Pre-Production (30-60 min).** Prepare the shot list, location, lighting setup, and audio configuration. For each 60 seconds of finished video, plan 2-3 distinct visual elements. Confirm that any B-roll, screen recordings, stock footage, or graphics are identified and sourced. IF shooting on location, THEN confirm permits, power, and weather contingency.
4. **Filming (1-3 hours).** Record A-roll (presenter/talent on camera). Film 4-5 takes of the hook (nothing else deserves more than 2 takes). Record room tone (30 seconds of silence at the location for audio noise reduction in post). Monitor audio levels throughout — clipped audio cannot be fixed in post. IF talent energy drops noticeably after 15-20 minutes, THEN break — audience perceives declining energy. Record B-roll separately or capture during shoot.
5. **Editing (3-8 hours).** Follow the editing pipeline: (a) Assembly edit — lay down best A-roll takes in order, (b) B-roll and visual variety — insert supporting visuals, ensure no single visual persists beyond 30 seconds without change, (c) Audio mixing — voice at full/consistent, background music at 15-25% of voice (-15 to -20dB below), target -14 LUFS, (d) Color grade — apply consistent look across all clips, (e) Graphics — add titles, lower thirds, chapter markers, CTAs. IF the raw cut exceeds target length by >15%, THEN perform a drift test: watch at 1x speed and mark every moment attention drifts — cut those sections.
6. **Upload Optimization (30-60 min).** Create 3 thumbnail variations with high contrast, 3-5 words max, and clear at phone size. Write the title (under 60 characters, front-load keywords, create curiosity without clickbait). Write the description (first 2 lines = keyword-rich hook, then timestamps, then detailed description with links). Add tags, playlist, end screen elements, and cards. Schedule upload 30-60 minutes before audience peak. Immediately after publishing: add the video to relevant playlists, post to Community tab, and share to external channels (email, social). IF CTR drops below 4% within the first 48 hours, THEN swap the thumbnail and test — do not wait.

### SOP-02: Online Course Video Production

**Objective:** Produce instructional video modules that maximize learner comprehension, retention, and completion rates, following Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles.

**Steps:**
1. **Define Learning Objectives.** With the {{HEAD_OF_EDUCATION_TITLE}} or curriculum designer, define 1-3 measurable learning objectives per video. Use Bloom's taxonomy action verbs: "By the end of this video, you will be able to [calculate/design/analyze/apply]." IF objectives cannot be stated concretely, THEN do not proceed — the video topic is too vague.
2. **Design the Instructional Structure.** Chunk content into segments of 3-5 minutes each within the video. Open with: objective statement -> real-world hook (why this matters) -> agenda preview. Body: explain -> demonstrate -> practice prompt -> repeat. Close with: 60-second summary + preview of next module. IF the total planned runtime exceeds 12 minutes, THEN split into two videos — engagement drops 50% after 12 minutes (MIT/edX study of 6.9M sessions).
3. **Script for Instruction.** Write conversationally at 130-150 words per minute. Apply Mayer's principles: (a) Coherence — remove every extraneous word, image, or sound that does not directly support the objective, (b) Signaling — use visual cues (arrows, highlights, zooms) to emphasize key points, (c) Modality — use spoken narration, not on-screen text (reduces cognitive load), (d) Segmenting — place clear "section complete" transitions between chunks. Never read slides verbatim — narration must expand on visuals, not duplicate them.
4. **Record the Instruction.** Choose the format: presenter-led (talking head + screen), screencast-only (software tutorials), AI avatar (high-volume corporate training), or live whiteboard/demonstration. Record in a distraction-free, well-lit environment. Use an external microphone — audio quality matters more than video. Film in segments (easier to edit and update later). At the end of each segment, add a reflection prompt or "try it yourself" challenge.
5. **Post-Production for Learning.** Add: chapter markers with descriptive titles, closed captions (99% accuracy — review auto-captions manually), downloadable transcript, visual callouts synchronized with spoken points, and interactive elements (if supported by {{LMS_PLATFORM}}: embedded quizzes, reflection prompts, branching scenarios). Ensure color contrast meets WCAG 2.1 AA standards for accessibility.
6. **Upload and Integrate into Curriculum.** Upload to {{LMS_PLATFORM}} or video host. Configure: completion tracking (view 90%+ = complete), prerequisite gating (video B unlocks only after video A is complete if the curriculum is sequential), and analytics (track watch time, drop-off timestamps, replays). IF completion rate drops below 70%, THEN review drop-off timestamps and restructure the section where viewers leave.
7. **Maintain and Update.** Review course videos quarterly. IF the software UI, policy, or best practice shown in the video has changed, THEN flag for re-recording. Modular architecture allows updating individual segments without re-shooting the entire module.

### SOP-03: Video Sales Letter (VSL) Production

**Objective:** Produce a conversion-optimized VSL that follows the proven psychological framework and drives measurable sales or lead generation.

**Steps:**
1. **Research and Strategy Brief.** Before writing a single word, document: (a) target audience — who are they, what problem do they have, what have they tried, why hasn't it worked? (b) Unique Mechanism — what is the specific, differentiated way your product solves their problem? (c) Offer — what exactly are they buying, at what price, with what guarantee? (d) Desired action — one single CTA. IF this brief is not approved by the {{HEAD_OF_MARKETING_TITLE}} or product owner, THEN do not begin scripting.
2. **Script the VSL using the 6-Part Framework.** Part 1: Hook (first 5-10 seconds) — bold claim, surprising stat, or direct question that names the prospect's exact problem. Part 2: Problem Agitation (30-60 seconds) — agitate with specifics: frustrations, cost of inaction, failed alternatives. Part 3: Solution Introduction (30-60 seconds) — introduce your product as the best solution. Focus on benefits, not features. Part 4: Proof (60-120 seconds) — testimonials, case studies, before/after data, ROI examples. Place proof earlier than you think (front-loaded). Part 5: Offer Stack (60-90 seconds) — core product, bonuses, guarantee. Stack value high. Part 6: CTA (30-60 seconds) — one single, unambiguous action with genuine urgency. IF the script cannot pass legal review for claims, THEN revise before production — do not record unverified claims expecting to cut later.
3. **Produce the VSL.** Choose the format: talking-head + graphics (most common, highest trust), animated explainer (for software/SaaS), screen recording (for tool demos), or live-action storytelling (for physical products). Record high-quality audio — poor audio is the #1 trust destroyer in VSLs. Design for mobile-first: over 70% of VSL views are on phones. Text overlays and CTAs must be readable at 6-inch screen size.
4. **Deploy on Landing Page.** Host the VSL on {{VSL_HOSTING_PLATFORM}} (Vidalytics or Wistia — not YouTube, to avoid related-video distractions and competitor ads). Embed on a dedicated landing page built in {{LANDING_PAGE_BUILDER}}. The page should have: VSL as the primary element, CTA button below the video (visible without scrolling), and minimal navigation (no menu bar, no footer links — single focus). IF the page has exit links, THEN remove them — the only path is through the video to the CTA.
5. **Track and Optimize.** Monitor: play rate (visitors who click play), retention at 25/50/75% markers, CTA click-through rate, and conversion rate (purchase or lead capture). IF play rate is below 5%, THEN test a new thumbnail or headline. IF retention drops sharply before the 25% mark, THEN the hook or problem section is weak — re-record the first 90 seconds. IF CTA clicks are low despite strong retention, THEN the offer or CTA language needs revision. Run split tests: test VSL vs. text-only landing page, test different hooks, test different offers.
6. **Iterate Quarterly.** VSL performance decays over time as audiences saturate and competitors adopt similar messaging. Review and refresh VSLs at least quarterly. Major changes (new offer, new proof, new hook) require full re-production; minor changes (updated stats, new testimonial) can be done with patch edits.

### SOP-04: Retention Engineering for Long-Form Video

**Objective:** Systematically optimize a long-form video's audience retention by analyzing data and applying structural improvements at every stage of production.

**Steps:**
1. **Analyze the Retention Graph.** For any published video with >1,000 views, open YouTube Studio -> Analytics -> Retention. Identify: (a) hook cliff — >30% drop in first 30 seconds (indicates hook failure), (b) mid-video craters — sections where retention drops more than 8% per 30 seconds, (c) slow bleeds — gradual but steady decline (indicates pacing or interest fatigue), (d) rewatch spikes — sections viewers re-watch (indicates high-value content — expand these). IF the video has a hook cliff, THEN the hook must be re-written and re-recorded — no amount of editing will save a bad hook.
2. **Diagnose the Root Cause.** For each problem area, match to likely cause: hook cliff = weak hook, too slow to deliver value, or "hey guys welcome back" opening. Mid-video crater = section runs too long without structural break, or topic is less interesting than promised. Slow bleed = pacing monotony, no pattern interrupts, or video is too long for its content value. IF the cause is unclear, THEN watch the problem section at 1x speed and mark every moment your own attention drifts — those are the cuts.
3. **Apply Structural Fixes.** For the NEXT video on a similar topic: (a) strengthen the hook using the Hook Trident: Spike (unexpected claim, 3-5s) -> Proof (evidence, 3-5s) -> Stakes (why it matters, 3-5s). (b) Insert re-engagement moments at 25%, 50%, and 75% of runtime — raise stakes, open a new question, or pivot to an unexpected angle. (c) Ensure no section exceeds 300 words without a structural break. (d) Use adversative transitions: "But here's the counterintuitive part..." instead of flat "Next, we'll cover..." (e) Add a "one more thing" bonus insight in the final 15% — rewards loyal viewers and trains the algorithm that your content is worth watching to the end.
4. **Improve Visual Pacing.** Review the edit timeline: is any single visual frame held for more than 30 seconds without change? Add pattern interrupts: zoom cuts, B-roll, graphics, text overlays, camera angle changes. Target: a visual change every 15-30 seconds. Background music should shift between sections to signal progression.
5. **Re-Test and Compare.** After applying fixes to 3+ videos, compare retention graphs against the baseline. Measure: did the average AVD improve? Did the hook cliff shrink? Are retention curves flatter (slower bleed) after structural fixes? Document what worked in a "Retention Playbook" that informs all future scripts.
6. **Apply Pre-Flight Script Analysis.** Before filming future videos, run scripts through {{SCRIPT_ANALYSIS_TOOL}}. IF the predicted retention score is below 65, THEN rewrite — pre-flight analysis prevents 67% of script-level retention failures (PrePublish 2026).

### SOP-05: Multi-Format Content Repurposing (Atomization)

**Objective:** Extract maximum value from each long-form video by repurposing it into multiple derivative assets for different platforms and purposes, ensuring brand consistency across all channels.

**Steps:**
1. **Identify Repurposing Candidates.** After publishing a long-form video, wait 7 days for performance data. IF the video's AVD is above 50% AND CTR is above 5%, THEN it is a strong candidate for repurposing. Prioritize videos that contain: clear "aha" moments, quotable insights, before/after demonstrations, or emotional peaks.
2. **Extract Short-Form Clips.** Using {{AI_CLIP_TOOL}} (OpusClip, Klap, or manual editing), extract 5-10 clips of 30-60 seconds each. Each clip must stand alone — a viewer who never saw the long-form video should understand the point. Best clips come from: the hook (if strong), surprising data reveals, controversial or counterintuitive statements, emotional moments, and step-by-step demonstrations. Add captions (burned-in, not platform auto-captions — 85% of Shorts/TikToks are watched on mute initially).
3. **Extract Audio for Podcast.** Export the audio track. Trim the intro and outro that reference visuals ("as you can see on screen"). Add a brief audio-only intro: "This is from [video title] on the {{COMPANY_NAME}} YouTube channel." Upload to podcast distribution platforms if {{COMPANY_NAME}} runs a podcast.
4. **Create a Blog Post.** Transcribe the full video using AI transcription. Edit for readability: remove verbal filler, add subheadings, break long paragraphs, insert images/screenshots from the video, and add an introduction and conclusion. The blog post should be publishable as a standalone article. Embed the YouTube video at the top. Post to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s blog with SEO optimization.
5. **Create Social Media Assets.** From the blog post: extract 5-7 key quotes and create quote graphics (Canva templates with {{COMPANY_NAME}} branding). From the video: grab 3-5 still frames for social posts. Write platform-specific captions for LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Instagram, and Facebook. Schedule posts across the week following the video release.
6. **Create an Email Newsletter Version.** Write a 200-300 word summary of the video's key takeaway. Embed the video thumbnail (linked to YouTube). Include one unique insight not fully explored in the video — gives newsletter subscribers an exclusive reason to stay subscribed. Send to {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s email list through {{EMAIL_PLATFORM}}.
7. **Track Repurposing ROI.** For each derivative asset, track: views/impressions, clicks back to the original video, and conversions attributed to the repurposed content. IF a derivative format consistently underperforms (low engagement for 3+ consecutive assets), THEN deprioritize that format and reallocate effort to higher-performing channels.

## 10. Quality Gates

Before any video is published, these gates must be passed. No exceptions. Publishing a video that fails a quality gate damages the channel's algorithmic standing and audience trust.

| Gate | YouTube Long-Form | Online Course | VSL / Sales Video |
|------|------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Hook Validation** | First value delivered within 15 seconds of spoken word; no "hey guys welcome back" opening | Learning objectives stated in first 30 seconds | Bold claim or direct problem named in first 5-10 seconds |

## 14. Bad Output Examples

### Example 1: Weak YouTube Hook (Will Be Skipped)
```
"Hey guys, welcome back to the channel. Today I wanted to talk about something that's been on my mind lately. Um, I've been getting a lot of questions about video editing, so I thought I'd make a video about it. Before we dive in, don't forget to like and subscribe — it really helps the channel. Okay, so let's get into it..."

[30 seconds have passed. Zero value delivered. 55%+ of viewers have already left.]
```
**Why this is bad:** The "hey guys welcome back" opening is the #1 retention killer on YouTube. The creator spent 30 seconds on self-referential fluff before delivering any value. The CTA ("like and subscribe") is placed before the viewer has any reason to care.

### Example 2: Lecture-Only Course Video (Will Lose Learners)
```
[A single slide titled "Chapter 7: Marketing Strategy" remains on screen for 9 minutes straight while the instructor reads text-heavy bullet points verbatim in a monotone voice. No visual changes. No demonstrations. No interactivity.]

Slide text: "Marketing strategy involves the strategic planning and execution of marketing activities designed to achieve organizational objectives through the creation and delivery of value to customers..."
```
**Why this is bad:** Violates Mayer's Modality Principle (reading text + hearing the same text = cognitive overload). No visual variety for 9 minutes (engagement drops 50% after 12 minutes, but the monotony kills interest far earlier). No signaling cues, no demonstrations, no practice prompts. This is a transcript read aloud, not an instructional video.

### Example 3: Feature-Dump VSL (Will Not Convert)
```
"Our software has 47 features including AI-powered analytics, drag-and-drop workflow builder, customizable dashboards, team collaboration tools, real-time reporting, automated notifications, third-party integrations, mobile app access, single sign-on security, and much more. Let me walk you through each one."

[20 minutes of feature demonstrations with no mention of the buyer's problem, cost of inaction, or outcome]
```
**Why this is bad:** Features don't sell — outcomes sell. The VSL never names the buyer's problem, never agitates the cost of inaction, never provides social proof, and never clarifies what makes this solution uniquely effective. The prospect doesn't care about 47 features — they care about whether their specific problem gets solved.

## 15. Common Mistakes

1. **Polishing production before fixing the script.** Creators spend hours on color grading and motion graphics for a video with a weak hook and no structure. The script is the highest-ROI activity — 67% of underperforming videos have script-level problems. Fix the script first.

2. **Starting with "Hey guys, welcome back."** This opener signals to the viewer: "You can skip the next 30-60 seconds and miss nothing." Deliver substantive value within the first 15 seconds of spoken word. If the viewer can't answer "What's in it for me?" in the first 10 seconds, they leave.

3. **Making videos too long for their content value.** A 30-minute video with 12 minutes of actual substance will lose viewers. Ruthlessly trim. Cut the first draft by 30%. If a sentence doesn't educate, entertain, or build toward the point, cut it. The goal is not to hit a length target — it's to maximize retained watch time.

4. **Publishing without testing the hook.** The hook is the most important 15 seconds of the video. Write 3+ variations. Record 4-5 takes. Test before publishing. A single weak hook can doom an otherwise excellent video.

5. **Neglecting audio quality.** Viewers tolerate mediocre video but never bad audio. Echo, background noise, inconsistent levels, or clipped audio signals amateur and erodes trust. Invest in a decent microphone ($50-100 USB mic minimum) and treat your recording environment.

6. **Ignoring the retention graph.** The retention graph is the single most honest feedback you will ever receive on your content. It tells you exactly where viewers lose interest. Creators who avoid looking at their retention data cannot improve.

7. **Posting inconsistently on YouTube.** Gaps longer than 2-3 weeks between uploads cause an algorithmic reset — your next video is treated like a new channel upload. One video per week for 12 months outperforms sporadic bursts of 4 videos followed by a month of silence.

8. **Failing to repurpose content.** A long-form video that took 8-18 hours to produce generates a single asset if published only to YouTube. The same video can spawn 5-10 short-form clips, a blog post, a podcast episode, social graphics, and an email newsletter. Repurposing multiplies ROI.

## 17. Edge Cases

### Edge Case 1: Algorithm Reset After Posting Gap
**Scenario:** Due to illness, vacation, or production delays, no video has been published to the YouTube channel for 3+ weeks. The next upload will likely be treated as a new-channel upload by the algorithm, with significantly reduced initial impressions.

**Response:** Do not upload your best content into the reset window — it will underperform through no fault of its own. Instead, publish a "warm-up" video: a community-focused piece (Q&A, channel update, behind-the-scenes) that resets the publishing cadence and re-establishes the signal to the algorithm that the channel is active. Wait until the second post-reset video (published within 7 days of the warm-up) to release your high-effort, high-potential content. Use Community posts and external promotion (email, social) to drive initial views on the warm-up video, compensating for reduced algorithmic impressions.

### Edge Case 2: Course Video Becomes Outdated Post-Launch
**Scenario:** A course module teaches a software workflow using a specific UI. The software updates, and the UI shown in the video no longer matches what students see, causing confusion and support tickets.

**Proactive case:** You anticipate a major software update and prepare to update the affected module.

**Response:** Because course videos are produced in modular, segmented chunks, you can replace the affected segment without re-shooting the entire module. Identify the specific timestamp range that shows the outdated UI. Re-record only that segment (same backdrop, same lighting, same audio setup for visual continuity). Edit the replacement segment into the master timeline. Re-export and re-upload to {{LMS_PLATFORM}}. Add a text overlay or update the description noting "Updated [date] for [Software] v[X.Y]." Notify enrolled students of the update. IF more than 30% of a course's videos are outdated, THEN propose a full course refresh to {{HEAD_OF_EDUCATION_TITLE}} rather than patch-fixing individual videos indefinitely.

### Edge Case 3: VSL Underperforms Despite Strong Creative
**Scenario:** A VSL has strong retention (60%+ at 75% mark), good play rate (12%+), but the conversion rate is below 1% — far below the target of {{VSL_CONVERSION_TARGET}}%.

**Response:** The creative is working — the problem is not the video. Diagnose the offer, price, or audience mismatch. Check: is the traffic source sending the wrong audience (wrong demographics, wrong intent level)? Is the price point misaligned with the audience's willingness to pay? Has a competitor launched a superior offer? Split test: same VSL, different landing page price/offer. Split test: same offer, different VSL (test a shorter version — sometimes a VSL is too long for its audience temperature). IF the VSL is driving clicks but no conversions, THEN the breakdown is in the checkout or sales process, not the video — escalate to {{HEAD_OF_MARKETING_TITLE}} for funnel analysis.

### Edge Case 4: Stakeholder Requests Misaligned with Platform Best Practices
**Scenario:** A stakeholder (product owner, executive, or client) requests that you include specific content in a video that you know will harm retention or conversion: a 2-minute company introduction at the start of a YouTube video, reading the entire product spec sheet in a VSL, or skipping the hook in favor of "setting context."

**Response:** Do not refuse outright. Instead, present the data: show the stakeholder retention graphs from your channel that demonstrate viewer drop-off when similar elements are included. Propose a compromise: "Let's include the company intro, but after the value hook — as a 15-second stinger at the 30-second mark instead of the first 2 minutes." Or: "Let's put the full product specs in the video description and as a downloadable PDF, and focus the video on the top 3 features that directly solve the problem we documented in discovery." IF the stakeholder insists, THEN escalate to {{HEAD_OF_VIDEO_TITLE}} with your data-backed recommendation and let them make the call — but document your objection so performance responsibility is clear.

### Edge Case 5: Raw Footage Loss or Corruption
**Scenario:** A hard drive fails, a memory card corrupts, or cloud backup was incomplete. Raw footage from a shoot — particularly an interview or location shoot that cannot be re-created — is lost.

**Response:** This is why the 3-2-1 backup rule exists. IF backup systems were followed, THEN restore from the surviving copy and continue production. IF backup systems failed (human error or simultaneous failures), THEN: (1) assess what is recoverable — can any portion be salvaged from proxy files, cloud caches, or editor's local drives? (2) Determine if the shoot can be rescheduled (interviews can often be re-done; one-time events cannot). (3) Immediately implement a hardened backup protocol: automated cloud backup of all memory cards before formatting, verified checksum transfers. (4) Report the loss to {{HEAD_OF_VIDEO_TITLE}} with a recovery plan. Do not attempt to hide the loss — transparency enables resource reallocation.

## 19. Sub-Specialists

The following named sub-specialists operate within or adjacent to the Long-Form Video Specialist role:

1. **YouTube SEO Specialist** — Focuses exclusively on keyword research, metadata optimization, thumbnail A/B testing, and traffic source analytics. Engages when channel growth has plateaued despite strong content quality — the problem may be discoverability, not production. Reports to marketing but partners directly with the video team.

2. **Motion Graphics Designer / Animator** — Creates custom motion graphics, animated explainers, title sequences, lower thirds templates, and data visualizations for videos. Engaged on high-production-value projects (brand films, product launches, flagship YouTube content) where static visuals are insufficient.

3. **Colorist** — Specializes in color grading and correction. Engaged on projects requiring cinematic look development, shot-to-shot color matching across multi-camera shoots, or HDR delivery. For most content, the video specialist handles basic color work; the colorist is engaged for premium projects.

4. **Sound Designer / Audio Engineer** — Handles advanced audio work: dialogue cleanup, sound effects design, ambient soundscape creation, multi-track mixing, and mastering to broadcast specifications. Engaged when production audio quality is insufficient or when the project demands cinema-grade sound.

5. **Thumbnail Designer** — Specializes exclusively in YouTube thumbnail design: composition, color psychology, facial expression selection, text hierarchy, and A/B testing analysis. On larger teams, this is a dedicated role that works alongside the video specialist, who provides creative direction and the raw frame selections.

6. **Course Instructional Designer** — Designs the pedagogical architecture of course videos: learning objectives, assessment alignment, cognitive load management, and multimedia learning principle application. The Long-Form Video Specialist executes the production; the Instructional Designer defines the instructional structure and validates content accuracy.
