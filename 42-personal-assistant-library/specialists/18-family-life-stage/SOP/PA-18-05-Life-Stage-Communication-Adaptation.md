## Purpose

To help {{OWNER_NAME}} adapt how they communicate with each family member as everyone moves through different life stages — so a conversation with a teenager lands differently than one with a kindergartner, an aging parent, or a stressed-out partner in a career pivot.

---

## Trigger

{{OWNER_NAME}} reports friction in a family relationship, a major life transition occurs (graduation, new school, empty nest, retirement, new baby, divorce conversation), or the quarterly family-communication audit fires in {{TASK_TOOL}}.

---

## Inputs

- Family member profiles (age, life stage, communication preferences, current stressors)
- Recent relationship notes from {{OWNER_NAME}}'s journal or check-in logs
- Family calendar for upcoming transitions and milestones
- {{OWNER_NAME}}'s own communication style and triggers (from IDENTITY.md)

---

## Define

The problem: We often communicate with the people we love the most on autopilot — using the same tone, the same expectations, and the same scripts year after year. But a 7-year-old needs different communication than a 14-year-old. A partner in a career crisis needs different support than one who's thriving. An aging parent who's losing independence needs dignity, not directives. When communication doesn't evolve with the person, relationships stall — and {{OWNER_NAME}} ends up confused about why the old approaches stopped working.

---

## Measure

Baseline: How often does {{OWNER_NAME}} walk away from a family conversation feeling heard and connected vs. frustrated and misunderstood? Track: instances of miscommunication, recurring conflict patterns, and each family member's expressed satisfaction with how they're being communicated with.

---

## Analyze

Communication breakdowns by life stage: with young children — over-explaining abstract concepts they're not developmentally ready for. With teens — lecturing when they need autonomy and questions. With adult children — defaulting to the parent role instead of the mentor role. With partners — making assumptions instead of asking. With aging parents — talking down to capable adults who are losing some independence but not their dignity.

---

## Improve

Apply these stage-aware adaptations:

1. **Young children (ages 0-9).** Use concrete language and emotional labeling ("I can see you're feeling frustrated"). Give two acceptable choices instead of open-ended questions. Get on their physical level — kneel, sit beside them. Connection before correction. The goal isn't the perfect explanation; it's that they feel seen.

2. **Pre-teens and teens (ages 10-18).** Lead with curiosity, not conclusions. Ask questions you don't already know the answer to. Respect their privacy while staying present. Side-by-side conversations (car rides, walks, cooking together) work better than face-to-face interrogations. Your job is shifting from manager to consultant.

3. **Adult children.** Ask permission before giving advice. Celebrate their competence. Let them initiate the depth of the relationship. Your pride in them should be louder than your worry about them. The relationship is optional now — earn it.

4. **Partner/spouse.** Never assume you know what they're thinking. The "we've been together so long I already know" trap is real. Conduct regular temperature checks outside of conflict. Speak your needs instead of expecting them to be decoded. The goal is understanding, not winning.

5. **Aging parents.** Preserve their autonomy wherever possible. Ask what they want before telling them what they need. Use their life wisdom as the anchor — "Mom, you've always been so good at planning ahead. What feels right to you about this decision?" Communicate about them, not at them. Let them lead until they can't.

---

## Control

- Quarterly: review relationship satisfaction across family members. Any relationship scoring below a 6 triggers a deeper check-in.
- After any significant family conflict: schedule a 24-hour cooldown, then revisit with stage-aware communication.
- Annually: update family-member profiles as kids age and life circumstances shift.

## Escalation — Named Partners

Escalate to {{OWNER_NAME}} when: a family relationship shows a sustained negative trend over two quarters, a communication pattern is causing observable distress in a child, a partner explicitly says they feel unheard, or a life-stage transition is approaching that requires a deliberate communication reset (child starting middle school, parent losing driving privileges, etc.). Present observations, not judgments. If a child's behavioral change signals deeper emotional needs, partner with **Specialist 09 (Emotional Support & Wellbeing)** and **Specialist 26 (Therapeutic Support)**. If partner communication breakdowns require structured mediation or relationship work, coordinate with **Specialist 12 (Relationships & Dates)**. If aging-parent communication requires elder-care expertise beyond the PA's scope, route to **Specialist 18 (Family & Life-Stage — PA-18-04)**.

---

## Definition of Done

This SOP is effective when {{OWNER_NAME}} feels fluent in each family member's current communication language — not perfect, but adaptive. When a teenager voluntarily shares something. When a partner says "thank you for really listening." When an aging parent feels respected, not managed.

---

## Tone Note

This isn't about scripting perfect conversations. It's about showing up as the right version of yourself for each person you love — the parent a 6-year-old needs, the parent a 16-year-old needs, the partner, the adult child, the caregiver. Different stages, different approaches, same heart. The goal is that everyone in {{OWNER_NAME}}'s family feels known in the way they need to be known right now.
