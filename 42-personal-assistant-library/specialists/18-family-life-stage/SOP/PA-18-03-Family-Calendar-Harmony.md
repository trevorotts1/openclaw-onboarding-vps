# PA-18-03 — Family Calendar Harmony

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **SOP ID** | PA-18-03 |
| **Title** | Family Calendar Harmony |
| **Version** | 1.0 |
| **Department** | Family & Life-Stage Concierge (Specialist 18) |

---

## Purpose

To maintain a single source of truth for every family member's commitments — work, school, activities, health, social — so {{OWNER_NAME}} can see the whole picture in one glance and prevent the chronic overcommitment that frays family life.

---

## Trigger

New event enters any family member's orbit, the weekly family sync fires (recommended: Sunday evening), or a scheduling conflict is detected by {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}.

---

## Inputs

- {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} (the family master calendar)
- Individual calendars for each family member (school, activities, work schedules)
- School and extracurricular calendars
- {{OWNER_NAME}}'s work calendar and travel schedule
- Partner's work calendar (if shared)

---

## Define

The problem: Most families don't have a calendar problem — they have a calendar-integration problem. Mom's work trips live in Outlook, the kids' soccer schedule is in TeamSnap, the school calendar is a PDF on the district website, and the family dinner plan lives in someone's head. The result: double-booked Saturdays, forgotten early-dismissal days, and the sinking feeling that the family is being run by whoever screamed loudest last. This SOP builds a single unified calendar that every family member's commitments flow into.

---

## Measure

Baseline: How many scheduling conflicts occurred last month? How many times did {{OWNER_NAME}} say "I forgot that was today"? Count conflicts, missed events, and last-minute scrambles. The goal is zero surprises.

---

## Analyze

Calendar-harmony killers: separate calendar ecosystems that don't talk to each other, "soft" commitments that were discussed but never written down, over-optimistic time estimates (20 minutes between soccer and violin across town), and a culture where anyone can add anything to the family calendar without a capacity check.

---

## Improve

Implement this structure:

1. **One family master calendar.** Choose {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} as the single system of record. Every family obligation — school events, practices, games, doctor appointments, birthday parties, travel, date nights, PTA meetings — lives here. No exceptions.

2. **Color-code by person.** Assign each family member a dedicated color. At a glance, {{OWNER_NAME}} can see who's where. {{OWNER_NAME}}'s events get their own color distinct from the kids and partner.

3. **Weekly family sync (30 minutes, Sunday evening).** Gather the family (or at minimum, {{OWNER_NAME}} and partner) to review the coming week. Walk through each day: who needs rides, who has late practice, which night is family dinner, where's the margin. Kids old enough to participate learn calendar literacy.

4. **Buffer blocks.** Add 30-minute travel buffers between commitments at different locations. Block "sacred" family time — recurring holds that don't get bumped for non-emergencies. Dinner together two nights a week is a real event on the calendar.

5. **Monthly capacity check.** Review the next 30 days. Is there at least one weekend with nothing scheduled? One evening per week with no commitments? If the calendar has zero white space, something needs to come off. Busy is not the same as full.

---

## Control

- Every Sunday: the upcoming week's calendar is reviewed and conflicts are resolved
- Every evening: a 2-minute next-day preview confirms tomorrow's plan
- Monthly: the capacity audit happens — if white space is below threshold, {{OWNER_NAME}} is alerted

---

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks

- [ ] Family master calendar contains all known commitments for the upcoming week by Sunday 8 PM
- [ ] No double-booked time slots exist for any family member
- [ ] At least one "sacred" family block is protected on the calendar this week
- [ ] All travel/sports buffers are calculated and reflected in drive times

---

## Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Scheduling conflicts per month | 0 |
| Weekly family sync completion | Every Sunday |
| White-space weekends per month | ≥ 2 |
| Last-minute scramble incidents | 0 |

---

## Escalation — Named Partners

Escalate to {{OWNER_NAME}} when: a scheduling conflict can't be resolved without their input, the monthly capacity check shows zero white-space weekends, a recurring commitment is consistently causing friction, or a family member is expressing distress about being overscheduled. Present options, not just problems. If calendar complexity requires deep scheduling redesign, partner with **Specialist 02 (Calendar & Scheduling Manager)**. If overscheduling is driven by {{OWNER_NAME}}'s difficulty saying no, route to **Specialist 17 (The Challenger)** for boundary work. If a family member's overcommitment is causing emotional distress, coordinate with **Specialist 09 (Emotional Support & Wellbeing)**.

---

## Definition of Done

Calendar harmony exists when {{OWNER_NAME}} can open one calendar and see the truth — every commitment, every person, every buffer — and when the family operates from a shared rhythm instead of competing individual schedules.

---

## Tone Note

The calendar isn't a prison — it's a permission slip. When it's right, it tells everyone what they can say yes to and what they can let go of. The goal isn't a perfectly optimized family. It's a family where no one feels like they're fighting the schedule to spend time together.
