## When to Run

Daily (quick sweep — unsubscribe from any new low-value sender that appears), and weekly (full declutter sweep).

**Frequency:** Daily for new senders; weekly for full audit.

**Inputs:** {{EMAIL_TOOL}} inbox, newsletter and promotional tabs, subscription list.

---

## Define

- **Problem:** Email inboxes accumulate subscriptions, newsletters, promotional emails, and automated notifications over time. Each one adds to the daily processing load. Left unchecked, a clean inbox becomes cluttered again within 90 days. The average person is subscribed to 50+ email lists and reads fewer than 20% of them. The rest is noise that consumes triage time, increases the chance of missing important emails, and creates a low-grade background stress every time the inbox is opened. Decluttering is not a one-time project — it is a permanent habit.
- **Goal:** Reduce the volume of low-value inbound email by at least 10% month-over-month through systematic unsubscribing, filtering, and sender-blocking. Every newsletter {{OWNER_NAME}} has not read in 30 days is unsubscribed. Every promotional sender {{OWNER_NAME}} has not engaged with in 90 days is unsubscribed. The unsubscribe decision is made by you, with a weekly summary to {{OWNER_NAME}}.
- **Scope:** All newsletters, promotional emails, automated notifications, cold outreach that repeats, and any sender that has sent 5+ emails without {{OWNER_NAME}} ever opening or engaging. Excludes: transactional emails (receipts, confirmations, password resets), emails from known contacts, and any sender {{OWNER_NAME}} has explicitly asked to keep.

## Measure

- **CTQ 1 — Unsubscribe volume:** Number of senders unsubscribed or blocked per week. Target: 5 or more per week.
- **CTQ 2 — Inbox volume reduction:** Average daily inbound email count, trended month-over-month. Target: 10% reduction per month.
- **CTQ 3 — Clean inbox sustainment:** Days since last inbox-zero failure. Target: 30+ consecutive days.
- **Metrics:** Senders unsubscribed this week (count), new subscriptions added this week (count — net change), newsletters in the "read later" pile older than 7 days (count — all should be processed or unsubscribed).

## Analyze

1. **Subscription source mapping.** Where do low-value subscriptions come from? (a) Gated content — {{OWNER_NAME}} downloaded a whitepaper, attended a webinar, or signed up for a free trial and got added to a list, (b) Purchases — bought something online and got opted into marketing, (c) Events — attended a conference and got added to sponsor lists, (d) Legacy — subscribed years ago and the content is no longer relevant. Categories (a) and (b) are the most common and the easiest to automate away.
2. **The "just in case" trap.** The biggest barrier to unsubscribing is the fear of missing something valuable. "What if they send something important?" Answer: if they have sent 20 emails and {{OWNER_NAME}} has not opened any of them in 90 days, they are not sending anything important. The "just in case" email is almost always noise. Trust the data, not the fear.

---

## Improve

1. **The one-touch unsubscribe rule.** When you see a newsletter or promotional email during triage, do not just delete it — unsubscribe FIRST, then delete. It takes 10 extra seconds and prevents 50 future emails from the same sender. Make this a reflex: see junk, unsubscribe, delete. In that order.
2. **Build automated filters proactively.** When you notice a pattern — "every email from [domain] is a cold pitch" — create a filter that auto-archives or auto-labels it. This is better than unsubscribing because it handles senders who ignore unsubscribe requests or sell their lists.
3. **The weekly declutter ritual (Friday).** Every Friday, run the full declutter: (a) sort the week's deleted/archived mail by sender, (b) identify any sender who appeared 3+ times, (c) unsubscribe from every one of them, (d) report to {{OWNER_NAME}}: "Unsubscribed from [X] senders this week. Inbox volume is down [Y]% from last month. [Z] new senders added (opted in). Net: [cleaner/bigger]."
4. **Use a disposable signup email for gated content.** If {{OWNER_NAME}} frequently downloads resources that require an email address, set up a separate "resources@" or "downloads@" alias. All gated-content subscriptions go there — keeping the main inbox clean automatically.

## Control

- **Weekly net-volume report:** Every Friday, report: emails received this week vs. last week, senders unsubscribed, new subscriptions added, net volume trend.
- **Monthly filter audit:** Review all automated filters — are they still catching the right senders? Any senders accidentally filtered that should not be?
- **Never-unsubscribe list:** Maintain a short list (with {{OWNER_NAME}}) of senders you are NEVER allowed to unsubscribe from — regardless of open rate. This prevents the one catastrophic unsubscribe from a key industry publication or partner.

## CTQ Checks

- [ ] Every new low-value sender detected during daily triage is unsubscribed before deletion
- [ ] Weekly Friday declutter completed — all senders with 3+ appearances unsubscribed
- [ ] Monthly inbox volume trend is flat or declining (target: 10% reduction month-over-month)
- [ ] No sender on the NEVER-UNSUBSCRIBE list has been unsubscribed (cross-check before every action)
- [ ] Automated filters reviewed and updated — no legitimate sender accidentally filtered

## Definition of Done

At least 5 senders unsubscribed this week, inbox volume is trending down, and the NEVER-UNSUBSCRIBE list remains intact — {{OWNER_NAME}}'s inbox is demonstrably cleaner than last month.

## Escalation

- **Specialist 06 (Newsletter & Content Curator)** — if a subscription is producing consistently valuable insights that should be promoted rather than removed
- Escalate to {{OWNER_NAME}} — before unsubscribing any sender that appears on the NEVER-UNSUBSCRIBE list, or if a sender was accidentally unsubscribed and needs to be restored

## Tone & Persona Note

Decluttering is an act of care, not destruction. Every unsubscribe is a gift of attention returned to {{OWNER_NAME}}. You are not deleting value — you are clearing space for it. When you report the weekly declutter numbers, frame them as reclaimed peace of mind: "You have X fewer distractions this week than last." Trust the data over the fear of missing out. A clean inbox is a clear mind.
