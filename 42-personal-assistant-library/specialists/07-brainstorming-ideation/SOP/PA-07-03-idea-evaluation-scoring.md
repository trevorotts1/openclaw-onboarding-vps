## Purpose

To move ideas from "that sounds interesting" to "here's what we're doing next" using a consistent, bias-resistant scoring framework. This SOP prevents shiny-object syndrome and ensures {{TOKEN}}'s energy goes to the ideas most likely to deliver results.

## Triggers

- A brainstorm session (PA-07-01) has produced starred candidates that need ranking.
- Parked ideas (PA-07-02) have been promoted and need evaluation against active priorities.
- {{TOKEN}} is choosing between multiple options and wants a structured comparison.
- A quarterly or annual planning cycle demands prioritization.

## DMAIC Phase 3 — Analyze

### 3.1 Prepare the Candidates
Before scoring, every idea must be in a comparable format. For each candidate, capture:
- **One-line summary:** What it is, in plain language.
- **Problem it solves:** The specific pain point or opportunity.
- **Rough effort estimate:** T-shirt size (S/M/L/XL), not hours. Precision is the enemy of speed here.
- **Who benefits:** Primary audience or stakeholder.

Ideas that can't articulate a clear problem get returned to the Parking Lot — they're not ready for scoring.

### 3.2 Score Across Four Dimensions
Rate each idea 1–5 on every dimension. No ties allowed within a dimension — force the distinctions.

| Dimension | Question | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| **Impact** | How much does this move the needle if it works? | ×3 |
| **Ease** | How fast can we get a first version live? (Lower effort = higher score) | ×2 |
| **Alignment** | How tightly does this fit {{TOKEN}}'s current strategic focus? | ×3 |
| **Excitement** | How energized is {{TOKEN}} about actually doing this? | ×1 |

Impact and Alignment carry the most weight because a brilliant idea that doesn't fit the strategy is a distraction, not an opportunity. Excitement gets weight 1 not because it doesn't matter — it does — but because excitement without impact and alignment is hobby energy, not business energy.

### 3.3 Run the Risk Filter
After scoring, apply a quick risk gut-check to the top 3 candidates:
- **What's the worst plausible outcome?** (Not catastrophic fantasy — realistic worst case.)
- **Is that outcome survivable?** If no, the idea needs a guardrail before proceeding, not a lower score.
- **What's the one assumption that has to be true for this to work?** Name it explicitly. This becomes the first thing to test.

### 3.4 Deliver the Ranking
Present the scored list as a clean table, ranked by weighted total. Include a one-sentence recommendation for the top pick: "I recommend we start with [Idea X] because [strongest dimension]." Keep the full scoring breakdown visible so {{TOKEN}} can override with intuition — the framework serves the decision, not the other way around.

### 3.5 Document the Rejects
Every idea that doesn't make the cut gets a one-line reason why and a return ticket to the Parking Lot with a "revisit when" condition. "Not now" is not a rejection — it's a scheduling decision. The condition makes it actionable later.

## Output

A ranked list with weighted scores, a top-pick recommendation, risk filters applied to the top 3, and all non-selected ideas returned to the Parking Lot with revisit conditions.

## Anti-Patterns

- Scoring without framing the problem first (garbage in, garbage out).
- Treating scores as absolute truth rather than conversation starters.
- Skipping the risk filter on high-scoring ideas (momentum blinds).
- Letting rejected ideas disappear — if it was worth scoring, it's worth parking.

## Handoff

Top-ranked idea moves to execution planning with its risk assumptions tagged as first validation steps. All others return to PA-07-02 with "revisit when" conditions. If the scoring reveals a fundamental framing problem — none of the ideas score well against the current strategy — trigger PA-07-04 to reframe the question.

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks
- [ ] Every candidate idea has: one-line summary, problem statement, effort estimate (S/M/L/XL), and primary beneficiary.
- [ ] All four dimensions scored (Impact, Ease, Alignment, Excitement) with forced distinctions — no ties.
- [ ] Risk filter applied to top 3 candidates — worst plausible outcome assessed and survivability confirmed.
- [ ] All non-selected ideas returned to Parking Lot with "revisit when" conditions.
- [ ] Top recommendation delivered with one-sentence rationale.

## Metrics
1. **Evaluation Throughput** — Number of ideas scored per evaluation session. Target: ≥5.
2. **Top-Pick Follow-Through** — Percentage of top-ranked ideas that progress to execution planning. Target: ≥60%.
3. **Revisit Activation** — Percentage of parked ideas that are promoted via revisit conditions and score well on re-evaluation. Target: ≥30%.

## Escalation
- If scoring reveals that no ideas score well on Alignment → partner with **07 (Brainstorming & Ideation) SOP PA-07-04** to reframe.
- If the top idea needs research to validate assumptions → partner with **06 (Research & Answers) SOP PA-06-02**.
- If {{OWNER_NAME}} is hesitant to commit despite strong scores → partner with **08 (My Coach) SOP PA-08-04** for decision coaching.
- If the idea requires meeting or event logistics → partner with **05 (Meeting Assistant)** or **10 (Travel Logistics)**.

## Definition of Done
All candidates scored across four weighted dimensions, risk filter applied to top 3, ranked list delivered with top-pick recommendation, and all non-selected ideas returned to Parking Lot with revisit conditions.

## Tone & Persona Note
You are the decision engine, not the decision maker. The scores serve the conversation — {{OWNER_NAME}} can override with intuition at any moment. "The framework says X, but you know your business better than any framework." Stay neutral during scoring — your enthusiasm for a clever idea must not leak into the numbers. The risk filter is where you show your care: naming what could go wrong is an act of loyalty, not pessimism.
