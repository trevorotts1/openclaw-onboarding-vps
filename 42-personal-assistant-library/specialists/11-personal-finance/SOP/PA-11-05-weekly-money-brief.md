## Purpose

To deliver a concise, emotionally intelligent weekly financial snapshot to {{TOKEN}} — one that informs without overwhelming, celebrates wins, flags concerns early, and transforms money from a source of background anxiety into a clear, managed system.

---

## DMAIC Section 3 — Analyze (Why Most Money Reviews Fail)

### 3.1 The Avoidance-Overwhelm Cycle

I've studied why people avoid looking at their finances even when they know they should. The pattern is consistent:

| Psychological Barrier | What Happens | The Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **The "It's Probably Bad" Dread** | {{TOKEN}} avoids opening the numbers because they anticipate bad news. The avoidance creates more bad news. | Lead with wins. Every brief opens with something positive — even if it's just "you stayed under budget in 2 categories." |
| **Information Overload** | A dump of raw transactions, balances, and categories with no narrative. The brain shuts down. | Maximum 5 numbers that matter. Everything else is available on request. |
| **Judgment-Freeze** | {{TOKEN}} spent more than planned on something. The shame spiral prevents engagement. | Zero judgment language. "Spending was higher in Dining this week" — not "You overspent." Data, not verdict. |
| **No Action Connection** | The review tells {{TOKEN}} what happened but gives no clear next step. It feels pointless. | Every brief ends with one small, concrete action. "This week, check if the $14.99 charge from 'AMS*SOMETHING' on June 4 is legitimate." |
| **Inconsistent Cadence** | Reviews happen "when I get around to it" — which is never. | Same time, same format, every Sunday evening. Predictability builds trust. |

### 3.2 The "3-Number Rule"

The most effective financial communication I've observed follows a simple principle: present no more than three headline numbers in the initial view. These three anchor the conversation. Everything else is drill-down detail available if {{TOKEN}} wants it.

The three numbers that matter most week to week:
1. **Spent this week** — actual discretionary outflow
2. **Upcoming in 7 days** — bills and known expenses hitting before next Sunday
3. **One flag** — anything unusual, concerning, or worth celebrating

## Trigger Conditions

- Every Sunday at 7:00 PM local time (automatic)
- Before major spending decisions (large purchase, trip, investment)
- On {{TOKEN}}'s request: "How are we looking this week?"
- End of month: expanded Monthly Money Brief with category trends

---

## Procedure

### Step 1: Data Collection (Sunday Afternoon)

Pull data from all sources before drafting:

1. **PA-11-03 (Expense Capture)** — total spending by category for the week
2. **PA-11-01 (Bill Calendar)** — upcoming 7-day bill forecast
3. **PA-11-02 (Subscription Audit)** — any new subscription flags from the past week
4. **Bank balances** — snapshot of all checking and savings accounts
5. **Credit card running balances** — current totals vs. typical month-to-date

### Step 2: Build the 3-Number Brief

The brief follows a strict structure designed for 90-second readability:

---

**📊 Weekly Money Brief — [Date Range]**

**💰 Spent This Week: $[total discretionary]**
- Top category: [category] at $[amount] ([↑↓→] vs. average)
- [One sentence of context, no judgment]

**📅 Due in the Next 7 Days: $[total upcoming]**
- [Bill 1] — $[amount] on [day]
- [Bill 2] — $[amount] on [day]
- [Bill 3 if applicable]

**🔍 One Thing to Know:**
- [A flag — unusual charge, upcoming large expense, savings milestone, subscription about to renew, reimbursement still outstanding]

---

### Step 3: Add the "Worth Celebrating" Line

Every brief includes one positive observation. This is not fluff — it's the antidote to avoidance. Examples:
- "You're on track to come in under budget for the third straight week."
- "The subscription audit you did last month is saving you $47/month — that's $564/year."
- "No late fees this month. That's the system working."

### Step 4: Deliver

Send the brief via {{TOKEN}}'s preferred channel (Telegram message, email, or spoken summary). Keep it to one screen — no scrolling required. End with one optional prompt: "Want me to dive deeper into anything?"

If {{TOKEN}} replies with a category or line item, I expand on just that item in the same format: context, comparison, recommendation.

### Step 5: Monthly Deep Dive (First Sunday of Each Month)

The Monthly Money Brief adds three elements to the weekly format:

1. **Category trend chart** — top 5 spending categories, month-over-month with direction arrows
2. **Subscription total** — what's currently being paid monthly in subscriptions (from PA-11-02 audit data)
3. **Savings rate** — (income minus expenses) / income, expressed as a percentage. Tracked month-over-month.

## Escalation

- Savings rate drops below 10% for 2+ consecutive months → flag in Monthly Brief with "Let's look at where we can adjust"

## Definition of Done

{{TOKEN}} opens a single Sunday-evening message and knows exactly where her money stands — what was spent, what's coming due, and the one thing worth paying attention to — without ever feeling judged or overwhelmed.

## Tone & Persona Note

Money reviews trigger dread for most people. Your job is to make them feel like checking the weather — informative, neutral, occasionally sunny. Lead with wins. Never use "overspent," "blew through," or any shame-adjacent language. For Black women professionals navigating the "strong Black woman" expectation, financial check-ins should feel like a teammate has your back, not like a parent checking your homework. Warm. Brief. Useful. Always end with a door open: "Want me to dive deeper into anything?"

## Escalation

- Bank balance dropped below safety threshold → immediate alert to {{TOKEN}}: "Your checking account is at $X. Upcoming bills this week total $Y. Let's discuss." Cc Specialist 09 (Daily Check-In)

- Overdraft or returned payment → immediate notification with remediation plan; escalate to Specialist 11's PA-11-01 (Bill Calendar) for calendar review
- Savings rate drops below 10% for 2+ consecutive months → flag in Monthly Brief; offer to connect with Specialist 11's PA-11-02 (Subscription Audit) for cost-reduction sweep
