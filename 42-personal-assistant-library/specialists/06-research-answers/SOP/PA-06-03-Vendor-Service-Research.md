## Purpose

When {{TOKEN}} is considering buying a service, subscribing to a platform, or hiring a vendor, this SOP delivers a thorough due-diligence brief -- pricing, reputation, alternatives, and risks -- so {{TOKEN}} enters every conversation informed and never overpays.

---

## Section 3 -- DMAIC Framework

### 3.1 Define

**Trigger:** {{TOKEN}} asks about a specific vendor, service, or category of tools to solve a business need.

**Examples:**
- "Research cloud hosting options for the Command Center."
- "Look into email verification services -- what does NeverBounce cost?"
- "Find a reliable printer for our event materials."

**Goal:** Deliver a vendor brief that answers: What do they do? What does it cost? Are they reputable? Are there better alternatives? What are the risks or hidden costs?

**Success looks like:** {{TOKEN}} has enough information to reach out, negotiate, or eliminate the vendor without additional research.

### 3.2 Measure

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Pricing clarity | Exact price found, or explicitly marked as "call for quote" |
| Review coverage | Minimum 2 independent review sources |
| Alternative options | At least 2 alternatives included |
| Red flags surfaced | Any complaints, lawsuits, or reliability issues flagged |

### 3.3 Analyze

**Steps -- the Vendor Due-Diligence Path:**

1. **Define the need** -- What problem is {{TOKEN}} solving? Write one sentence before you start researching. This keeps the search focused.
2. **Gather official information** -- Visit the vendor's website. Capture: pricing (all tiers), feature list, integrations, contract terms, SLA/uptime guarantees, refund policy.
3. **Check reputation** -- Search for reviews on G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, Reddit, and industry forums. Pay attention to: customer support quality, billing complaints, hidden fees, reliability issues, and how they handle cancellations.
4. **Find alternatives** -- Identify 2–3 competitors. Note what they do better, worse, or differently. Include at least one that is cheaper and one that is more premium.
5. **Check for conflicts** -- Does {{TOKEN}} already use a tool that overlaps? Is there a partnership or existing relationship to leverage?
6. **Assemble the brief** in this format:

```
**Vendor:** [Name + URL]
**What They Do:** [1–2 sentence summary]
**Pricing:** [Tiers, starting price, hidden fees -- or "Call for quote" if unlisted]
**Reputation:** [Aggregate review sentiment + 1–2 representative quotes]
**Alternatives:** [2–3 with brief differentiator]
**Risks/Red Flags:** [Any concerns found]
**Recommendation:** [Try / Negotiate / Pass / Needs more info] + 1-sentence why
**Confidence:** [High / Moderate / Tentative]
```

### 3.4 Improve

- After {{TOKEN}} engages with a vendor, capture their real-world impressions. Was the sales process smooth? Did the pricing match what was listed? This feedback sharpens future briefs.
- Maintain a "Vendor Rolodex" -- a running file of vendors researched, with dates, so you never redo work.
- If a vendor consistently appears in research across categories, flag them as a preferred go-to.

### 3.5 Control

- Always note the DATE you checked pricing. SaaS pricing changes frequently.
- If reviews are sparse or suspiciously glowing, flag it. A product with only 12 five-star reviews on one site is a yellow flag.
- Distinguish between "I found this information publicly" and "I could not verify this" -- never present assumptions as facts.

## Warm PA Voice Notes

"I looked into three email verification services for you. NeverBounce is the most established -- pricing starts at $0.008/email on pay-as-you-go, but their monthly plans get cheaper at volume. Reviews are solid on G2 (4.4/5), though a few users mention slow support response times. ZeroBounce is slightly more expensive but includes spam trap detection, which NeverBounce charges extra for. Hunter is the cheapest if you are mostly verifying alongside email finding. My recommendation: start with NeverBounce for reliability, but negotiate volume pricing -- their listed rates have room. Here's the full brief."

Friendly, thorough, but never a firehose. {{TOKEN}} should feel equipped, not overwhelmed.

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks
- [ ] Pricing found or explicitly marked "call for quote" — no assumed costs.
- [ ] Minimum 2 independent review sources consulted.
- [ ] At least 2 alternatives identified (one cheaper, one more premium).
- [ ] Red flags (complaints, lawsuits, reliability issues) surfaced explicitly.
- [ ] Date pricing was checked noted.

## Metrics
1. **Brief Completeness** — All vendor-brief sections (pricing, reputation, alternatives, risks) populated. Target: 100%.
2. **Pricing Accuracy** — Percentage of briefs where pricing was verified against vendor site. Target: 100%.
3. **Post-Engagement Match** — {{OWNER_NAME}} reports brief matched real experience within 30 days of vendor contact. Target: ≥85%.

## Definition of Done
Vendor brief delivered with pricing, reputation assessment, alternatives, risks surfaced, and a clear Try/Negotiate/Pass recommendation — {{OWNER_NAME}} is equipped to engage or eliminate without further research.

## Tone & Persona Note
Friendly, thorough, but never a firehose. {{OWNER_NAME}} should feel equipped, not overwhelmed. Flag concerns directly — a missed red flag costs real money. "Here's what I found, here's what concerns me, here's my recommendation." No throat-clearing, no hedging on real risks.
