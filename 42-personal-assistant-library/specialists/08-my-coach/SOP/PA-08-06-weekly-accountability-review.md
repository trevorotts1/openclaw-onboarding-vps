# PA-08-06 — Weekly Accountability Review

## Purpose
Review {{OWNER_NAME}}'s week against their commitments — celebrating what got done, understanding what didn't, and setting next week's priorities with clarity and renewed ownership. This is a progress checkpoint, not a performance review.

## DMAIC Framework

### Define
The Weekly Accountability Review is the rhythm that keeps goals alive. Without it, commitments fade into the noise of busy weeks. With it, every week becomes a deliberate step toward the vision. The tone is warm but direct: "Let's look at what happened, learn from it, and set up next week to win."

### Measure
Review three categories: Completed Actions (what got done), Deferred Actions (what didn't — and why), and Unplanned Wins (good things that happened that weren't on the list). Score the week on Completion Rate (completed / total committed) and Alignment (did this week's actions move the needle on top goals?).

### Analyze
For every deferred action, categorize: Capacity (too much), Priority (something mattered more), Avoidance (resistance/fear), or Clarity (not specific enough to execute). Fix the cause, not the symptom — "too busy" needs a different solution than "I avoided it."

### Improve
1. Start with wins: "Before we look at what didn't happen — what went well this week?" Anchor in progress first.
2. Review last week's commitments one by one: Done? Not done? Partially? Be specific.
3. For incomplete items: "What got in the way?" Listen without judgment — understanding, not blame.
4. Extract the lesson: "What did this week teach you about how you work best?"
5. Prune or recommit: Carry forward, adjust, or drop. Not everything deserves to carry forward.
6. Set next week's 3–5 priority actions: specific, achievable, tied to a larger goal.
7. Rate confidence (1–10). If below 7, reduce scope until confidence rises.

### Control
Log the weekly review summary — completed, deferred, lessons, next week's commitments, and confidence score — to {{TASK_TOOL}} with tag `weekly-review`. Schedule next week's review at the end of this session. Track completion rate trends monthly.

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks
- [ ] Wins acknowledged before reviewing gaps.
- [ ] Every deferred action categorized with a reason (capacity / priority / avoidance / clarity).
- [ ] Lesson extracted from the week's pattern.
- [ ] Next week's commitments are 3–5 specific actions, each tied to a goal.
- [ ] Confidence score is ≥7/10.
- [ ] Next review scheduled in {{TASK_TOOL}}.

## Metrics
1. **Weekly Completion Rate** — Percentage of committed actions completed each week. Target: ≥70% (life happens — 100% isn't the goal, honest tracking is).
2. **Deferred-Action Resolution** — Percentage of deferred actions that are either recommitted with a new plan or intentionally dropped (not carried forward indefinitely). Target: 100%.
3. **Confidence Score** — Average weekly confidence rating. Target: ≥7.5.

## Escalation
If completion rate drops below 40% for three consecutive weeks, or if {{OWNER_NAME}} consistently rates confidence below 5 despite scoping down, escalate to {{OWNER_EMAIL}} with the pattern summary. Something structural may need attention — workload, energy, or goal alignment. If the accountability pattern reveals avoidance rooted in fear → partner with **08 (My Coach) SOP PA-08-05** for a confidence reset. If {{OWNER_NAME}}'s commitments consistently involve tasks that need ideation or creative work → partner with **07 (Brainstorming & Ideation) SOP PA-07-01**. If the review reveals calendar overload → partner with **02 (Calendar Management)**.

## Definition of Done
Week reviewed with wins celebrated, every commitment accounted for (done/deferred with reason), lesson captured, next week's 3–5 actions set with ≥7/10 confidence, and all logged to {{TASK_TOOL}} with next review scheduled.

## Tone Note
This is not a report card. You're not grading {{OWNER_NAME}} — you're helping them see their own patterns. "You had a full week and still got three of five done — that's solid. Let's talk about the two that slipped." Curiosity over criticism, always.
