# PA-08-05 — Confidence & Fear Reset

## Purpose
Coach {{OWNER_NAME}} through a moment of shaken confidence, impostor syndrome, or fear-based paralysis — using evidence, reframing, and one courageous action to rebuild self-trust and forward momentum.

## DMAIC Framework

### Define
A confidence reset is not a pep talk. It's a structured process that separates feelings from facts, acknowledges the fear without letting it drive, and produces one concrete action that proves the fear wrong. The goal is not to eliminate fear — it's to act in its presence.

### Measure
Assess the client's current state on three dimensions: Fear Intensity (1–10, where 10 is paralyzed), Evidence Awareness (does the client recall their own track record?), and Action Readiness (1–10, willingness to take one step despite fear).

### Analyze
Fear tells a story. Identify the story and check it against reality: "What exactly are you afraid will happen? Has that happened before? What actually happened instead?" Most fear narratives collapse under gentle, evidence-based scrutiny. Also check: Is this fear rational (real risk) or emotional (impostor syndrome, perfectionism)?

### Improve
1. Let the client name the fear — out loud, specifically. "I'm afraid of [X] because [Y]." Naming it reduces its power.
2. Build the Evidence Counter-Sheet: List past wins, hard things done, skills built, and similar successes. Visible evidence beats internal narrative.
3. Separate feelings from facts: "The feeling is 'I'm not ready.' The fact is you've done [X, Y, Z]. Which gets the vote?"
4. Reframe the fear: "What if this isn't a stop sign — just excitement wearing a scary mask?"
5. Design the Courage-Action: One small, specific, observable action this week that proves the fear wrong.
6. Pre-solve resistance: "What will your brain say right before you act? What will you say back?"
7. Close with commitment: "I am choosing courage over comfort. I will [action] by [date]."

### Control
Log the fear statement, evidence counter-sheet, courage-action, and commitment to {{TASK_TOOL}}. Schedule a 1-week courage check-in. Track fear intensity scores across sessions — persistent high scores despite intervention may signal a deeper pattern.

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks
- [ ] Fear named specifically — not "I'm scared" but "I'm afraid of [specific outcome]."
- [ ] Evidence Counter-Sheet written with ≥3 past wins or capabilities.
- [ ] Feelings-vs-facts separation completed.
- [ ] Courage-Action is specific, observable, and scheduled with a deadline.
- [ ] Resistance pre-solved with a planned counter-response.

## Metrics
1. **Fear Intensity Shift** — Fear score pre- vs. post-session. Target: decrease by ≥3 points.
2. **Courage-Action Completion** — Percentage of courage-actions completed within the committed timeframe. Target: ≥85%.
3. **Confidence Rebound** — Client self-rated confidence score 2 weeks post-session. Target: return to pre-dip baseline or higher.

## Escalation
If {{OWNER_NAME}} reports fear intensity at 9–10 across three consecutive sessions, or if fear is preventing basic functioning (not leaving the house, not opening email, not making calls), escalate to {{OWNER_EMAIL}}. This may exceed coaching scope and require professional therapeutic support. If the fear is rooted in imposter syndrome specifically → partner with **25 (Imposter Syndrome)** for specialized support. If {{OWNER_NAME}} needs emotional decompression alongside confidence work → partner with **09 (Emotional Support & Wellbeing) SOP PA-09-06** or **PA-09-07**. If the confidence dip relates to a specific upcoming event → partner with **05 (Meeting Assistant)** for preparation support.

## Definition of Done
Fear named specifically, evidence counter-sheet written, feelings separated from facts, courage-action committed with deadline and pre-solved resistance, all logged to {{TASK_TOOL}} with 1-week check-in scheduled.

## Tone Note
Never dismiss the fear. "That's not a big deal" is the fastest way to lose trust. Instead: "I hear how scary this feels. AND I've watched you do [X]. Let's look at the evidence together." Acknowledge first, reframe second. The fear is real — and so is their strength.
