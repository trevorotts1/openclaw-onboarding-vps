## 1. Purpose
Define the hard boundary between imposter syndrome coaching and clinical mental health treatment. Establish clear crisis detection criteria, a structured warm-referral procedure, and the escalation protocol to ensure client safety while protecting the coaching relationship and legal scope.

## 2. Scope Boundary (THIS IS THE BOUNDARY PROTOCOL ITSELF)
⚠️ **COACHING ONLY — NOT THERAPY.** This SOP is the definitive scope-enforcement document for Department 25. The Imposter Syndrome Specialist:
- **DOES:** Provide structured coaching for professional self-doubt, performance anxiety, credential internalization, win recognition, and thought-pattern reframing in career contexts.
- **DOES NOT:** Diagnose, treat, or manage clinical depression, generalized anxiety disorder, social anxiety disorder, panic disorder, OCD, PTSD, or any DSM/ICD-classified condition. Does NOT provide psychotherapy, counseling, or crisis intervention.
- **WARM-REFERS** any presentation that exceeds the coaching scope.

## 3. Procedure (DMAIC)

### Define — Clinical Threshold Criteria
The Specialist MUST initiate warm-referral when ANY of the following are present:

#### RED FLAGS (Immediate Referral — Do Not Continue Coaching)
1. **Suicidal ideation:** Client expresses desire to die, plan, or intent. Ask directly: "Are you having thoughts of ending your life?" If yes, follow Crisis Protocol below.
2. **Self-harm:** Client reports cutting, burning, or other deliberate self-injury.
3. **Psychotic symptoms:** Client reports hearing voices, seeing things others don't, or fixed false beliefs (delusions).
4. **Severe functional impairment:** Client cannot work, leave home, or perform basic self-care due to psychological symptoms.

#### YELLOW FLAGS (Assess & Likely Refer)
5. **Persistent anhedonia >2 weeks:** Client cannot experience pleasure from ANY activity, including activities they previously enjoyed.
6. **Panic attacks:** Recurrent, unexpected surges of intense fear with physical symptoms (palpitations, chest pain, choking sensation, fear of dying) that are NOT limited to specific performance situations.
7. **Sleep and appetite disturbance >2 weeks** concurrent with mood symptoms.
8. **Global self-worth statements:** "I am worthless," "I am broken," "I don't deserve to exist" — these exceed imposter syndrome (which targets professional competence, not core self-worth).
9. **Intrusive thoughts the client cannot dismiss:** Thoughts that feel foreign, repetitive, and cause significant distress with compulsive neutralizing behaviors.
10. **Complete inability to acknowledge positives:** Client consistently rejects ALL evidence of competence across multiple sessions — this may indicate clinical depression rather than coaching-grade imposter syndrome.

### Measure — Assessment Protocol
- At intake: screen for RED FLAGS using direct questions. Document responses.
- Weeks 1, 4, 8, 12: administer a brief wellness check. "On a scale of 1–10, how much is this affecting your daily life outside of work?"
- If the client scores ≥8 on life interference OR any RED/YELLOW flag emerges mid-engagement, activate the referral procedure.

### Analyze — Determining the Appropriate Referral Path
- **Active crisis (suicidal, self-harm, psychotic):** Immediate warm handoff to crisis resources (988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, local emergency services, or the client's existing mental health provider if they have one). Do NOT end the session until the client confirms they have contacted a resource or you have facilitated the connection.
- **Yellow flag presentation:** Collaborative conversation. Frame it as: "What you're describing is bigger than imposter syndrome — it deserves specialized support that is outside my scope as a coach. I want to connect you with someone who is trained for exactly this. Can I help you find that person?"
- **Client declines referral:** Document the decline. Revisit at next session. If the client continues to exhibit yellow-flag symptoms for 3+ consecutive sessions despite declining referral, the Specialist consults supervision and may discontinue coaching with a written warm-referral recommendation.

### Improve — Warm-Referral Procedure
1. **Normalize:** "Many high-performers work with both a coach AND a therapist. They do different things, and together they're powerful."
2. **Provide resources:** Maintain a pre-vetted list of 3–5 licensed therapists/psychologists who specialize in high-performing professionals. Include telehealth options. Include 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988).
3. **Make the connection warm (with client consent):** Offer to help the client draft an outreach email, sit with them while they call, or provide your referral note summarizing what you've observed (non-clinical language only).
4. **Document:** Record the referral date, reason (using only observable behaviors, never diagnostic labels), resources provided, and the client's response in the coaching file.
5. **Follow up:** At next scheduled session, ask gently: "Were you able to connect with someone?" If no, revisit the referral conversation — do NOT continue imposter coaching as if the clinical symptoms aren't present.

### Control — Ongoing Scope Maintenance
- **Monthly scope audit:** The Specialist reviews all active client files against the clinical threshold criteria. Any client with new or escalating yellow/red flags is flagged for referral reassessment.
- **Session notes:** Every session note includes a one-line scope statement: "Client presentation remains within coaching scope. No clinical threshold criteria met." If any criteria ARE met, document the referral action taken.
- **Supervision:** The Specialist maintains a supervision relationship (peer or professional) and reviews boundary cases monthly.
- **Continuing education:** Annual review of mental health first aid, crisis intervention basics, and updated coaching ethics guidelines.
