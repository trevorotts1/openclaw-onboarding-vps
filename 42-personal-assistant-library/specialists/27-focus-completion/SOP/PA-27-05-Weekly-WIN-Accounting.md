## Objective

Run a 15-minute weekly review that surfaces every WIN, quantifies completion velocity, identifies attention-leak patterns, and sets next week's ONE focus target -- so no completed work goes unacknowledged and no inefficiency repeats unnoticed.

## Why This Exists

The brain remembers interruptions and forgets completions. Without deliberate accounting, you end the week feeling busy but unable to name what you finished. WIN Accounting converts data from PA-27-01, PA-27-02, and PA-27-03 into behavioral adjustments. Rubinstein et al. (2001) gave us switch-cost math; this SOP closes the loop by turning that data into action.

## DMAIC

### Define

WIN Accounting is the weekly end-of-week ritual that reviews what was accomplished, what was not, and why. WIN stands for What I Noticed: every task marked COMPLETE in PA-27-02 during the review week is harvested by name and counted. The review also surfaces tasks that remained PARTIAL, quantifies the gap between intention and completion, calculates the attention tax from PA-27-03 switch data, and identifies the single most impactful pattern driving results -- or blocking them. It is not a performance review; it is a harvest ritual that makes completed work visible so the brain registers it as real.

### Measure

Four metrics drive the review. Completion rate: total COMPLETE tasks divided by total tasks seeded for the week. Focus block adherence: percentage of scheduled focus blocks that ran to completion without interruption per PA-27-01 data. Switch count: total switch events from PA-27-03 for the week, compared against the prior week. WIN score: a composite of completion rate, block adherence, and switch-count trend direction, summarized as a single qualitative assessment (Strong / Steady / Fragmented). These four numbers provide a complete picture of the week's output quality.

### Analyze

Pattern detection drives the analysis. Which days and times were most productive? Cross-reference PA-27-01 block completion data by day and time slot -- the highest-depth-score blocks reveal the operator's peak windows. Which task types stall repeatedly? Tasks carried forward as PARTIAL for 3+ days signal a structural issue (scope too large, wrong task, skill gap, or avoidance). What is the gap between intention and completion? Compare the total estimated minutes for the week's task seeds against actual completed minutes -- a gap >40% signals chronic underestimation or overcommitment. Identify the single pattern that cost the most output this week and name it in one sentence.

### Improve

Based on the pattern identified in Analyze, set exactly ONE target for next week. Not three. Not a prioritized list. ONE specific, measurable behavior change. Format: "Next week, I will [specific behavior], so that [measurable outcome]." Examples: "Next week, I will move my phone to another room before every morning block, so that my morning switch count drops below 2 per day." Or: "Next week, I will cap task seeds at 3 per day and kill or delegate anything beyond that, so that my carry-forward count drops from 4 to 0." One focus improvement per week compounds faster than three half-hearted ones -- you can measure whether you did the ONE thing, and whether it worked.

### Control

Weekly review cadence is non-negotiable: every Friday, 15 minutes, same time. Streak tracking: maintain a running count of consecutive weeks with completed WIN Accounting. If the streak breaks, the data feedback loop breaks within 7 days -- reschedule immediately. Escalation triggers: if WIN count declines for 2+ consecutive weeks, escalate to Specialist 16 (Motivation & Momentum) for engagement and goal-alignment assessment. If attention tax is rising despite PA-27-04 countermeasures, escalate to Specialist 09 (Emotional Support & Wellbeing) -- distraction may be a symptom of depletion, not discipline. If zero WINs for 2+ consecutive weeks, notify Master Orchestrator for structural review of the operator's workload, environment, or task-selection process.

## Procedure (15 minutes, every Friday)

### Step 1  --  Harvest Wins (3 min)
1. Filter PA-27-02 tracker to this week. Count all COMPLETE rows -- that is your WIN count.
2. List each WIN by name. Verbalize or write them -- this anchors completion in memory.
3. Flag any PARTIAL tasks carried forward from last week that remain partial. These are your stuck tasks.

### Step 2  --  Calculate Velocity (3 min)
1. Sum Actual Min for all COMPLETE tasks = Total Completed Minutes.
2. Sum all focus block minutes from PA-27-01 = Total Block Minutes.
3. Focus Efficiency = Completed / Block. Above 85% = excellent. Below 60% = investigate with PA-27-03.

### Step 3  --  Calculate Attention Tax (3 min)
1. Sum total switch events × 20 min (Rubinstein et al., 2001) = Attention Tax in minutes.
2. Compare vs. last week. Direction matters more than absolute number.
3. If rising: which category grew (Self / External / Environmental)? That is your target.

### Step 4  --  Pattern Recognition (3 min)
Answer in one sentence each:
1. Highest-quality WIN this week -- and why did it flow?
2. What task kept appearing as PARTIAL?
3. When did your best focus block happen (day + time) and what conditions were present?
4. What pattern cost the most output?

### Step 5  --  Set ONE Target (3 min)
Format: "Next week, I will [specific behavior], so that [measurable outcome]."
One target only. This department does not multitask. One focus improvement per week compounds faster than three half-hearted ones.

## Edge Cases
- **Zero WINs:** Structural blocker -- scope too large, blocks not protected, wrong task selection. Escalate if it persists 2+ weeks.
- **20+ WINs:** Check for task inflation -- breaking one task into micro-tasks for dopamine hits. A WIN must be meaningful.
- **Holiday/partial week:** Skip velocity comparison. Still count WINs and set the ONE target.
