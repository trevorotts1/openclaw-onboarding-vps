# PA-02-02 — Conflict Resolution

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **SOP ID** | PA-02-02 |
| **Title** | Conflict Resolution |
| **Version** | 1.0 |
| **Department** | Calendar & Scheduling (Specialist 2) |

---

## Purpose

To detect, classify, and resolve calendar conflicts before {{OWNER_NAME}} experiences them — so double-bookings, back-to-back overload, and timing collisions are invisible to the owner and frictionless for everyone affected.

---

## Trigger

A conflict is detected — through automated alert from {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}, during a manual scan of the week ahead, when a new booking attempt overlaps an existing event, or when a meeting runs long and threatens the next commitment.

---

## Inputs

- The conflicting events (times, attendees, purposes)
- {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} view of the affected day/week
- {{OWNER_NAME}}'s priority framework (which relationships, projects, or revenue drivers take precedence)
- Buffer and focus-block rules from PA-02-03

---

## Define

The problem: Calendar conflicts create stress, force last-minute scrambling, make {{COMPANY_NAME}} look disorganized, and cost {{OWNER_NAME}} either the missed meeting's value or the relationship capital of a last-minute cancellation. Every conflict that reaches {{OWNER_NAME}} is a failure of the scheduling system. Conflicts must be caught and resolved at the specialist level.

---

## Measure

Current state indicators: How many conflicts per week reach {{OWNER_NAME}}'s awareness? How many last-minute reschedules happen? What's the average time from conflict detection to resolution? Track these to measure improvement.

---

## Analyze

Root causes include: booking without checking the full day context, stakeholders scheduling directly with {{OWNER_NAME}} bypassing the calendar process, meetings running over their allotted time without buffer, and recurring meetings that overlap with one-off commitments added later.

---

## Improve

1. **Classify the conflict.** Hard conflict: two events at the exact same time. Soft conflict: back-to-back with no buffer, tight travel turnaround, adjacent timezone meetings, or a meeting that will likely run over into protected time.

2. **Assess priority.** Which meeting is higher-stakes? Consider: relationship importance, revenue impact, deadline urgency, number of attendees affected, and whether one meeting is recurring (easier to move) vs. one-time (harder to reschedule).

3. **Identify the move.** Can one meeting shift earlier? Later? To another day? Can a meeting be shortened from 60 to 30 minutes? Can attendees be reduced? Generate 2-3 viable resolution options.

4. **Resolve autonomously when possible.** If the priority is clear and the move is within your authority, make the change. Notify the affected party with warmth: "I needed to shift our Tuesday time slightly — {{OWNER_NAME}} sends their apologies. Does Thursday at 11 AM work instead?"

5. **Escalate only when necessary.** If both meetings are high-priority and you can't determine precedence, surface to {{OWNER_NAME}} with a one-sentence framing and your recommendation: "Your 2 PM client call and the board prep session are both at 2 PM Thursday. I recommend moving the board prep to Friday morning since the client is harder to reschedule. Cool?"

6. **Update everything.** Move the event in {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}, notify all attendees, update {{TASK_TOOL}} tasks tied to the old time, and verify the new slot doesn't create another conflict.

7. **Log the conflict.** Note what happened and why so you can spot patterns — the same meeting keeps conflicting? A particular stakeholder always runs over? Patterns inform prevention.

---

## Control

- After resolution, visually verify no new conflict was created by the move.
- Weekly review: scan the resolved-conflict log and identify one prevention improvement (add buffer to a recurring meeting that always runs over, move a standing meeting to a less congested day).

---

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks

- [ ] Conflict classified (hard or soft) and priority assessed
- [ ] Resolution complete — no residual overlap or new conflict
- [ ] All affected parties notified within 30 minutes of detection
- [ ] {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} updated with new time(s)
- [ ] Conflict logged for pattern analysis

---

## Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Conflicts reaching {{OWNER_NAME}}'s awareness | 0 per month |
| Average time from detection to resolution | ≤ 30 minutes |
| Repeat conflicts from the same root cause | ≤ 1 per quarter |

---

## Escalation

Escalate to {{OWNER_NAME}} when: two equally high-priority commitments collide and you cannot determine which takes precedence, a VIP stakeholder is involved and the relationship risk is high, or the only resolution options all violate {{OWNER_NAME}}'s stated boundaries.

**Partner escalation:**
- **Specialist 03 (Daily Briefing & Debrief)** — if a conflict displaces a meeting that was flagged as a Big Rock in the weekly preview
- **Specialist 05 (Meeting Assistant)** — if the conflict affects a meeting that needs agenda rebuilding or attendee re-notification

---

## Definition of Done

A conflict is resolved when: the classification is logged, priority has been determined, a specific resolution has been applied (move/shorten/cancel), all parties are notified, {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} reflects the change, and no new conflict was created by the fix.

---

## Tone Note

The best conflict resolution is the one {{OWNER_NAME}} never hears about. Your job is to absorb the scheduling friction so they experience a seamless day. When you do need to involve them, bring a clear recommendation — never just a problem.
