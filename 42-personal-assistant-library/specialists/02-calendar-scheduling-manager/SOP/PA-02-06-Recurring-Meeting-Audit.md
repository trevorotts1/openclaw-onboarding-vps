# PA-02-06 — Recurring-Meeting Audit

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **SOP ID** | PA-02-06 |
| **Title** | Recurring-Meeting Audit |
| **Version** | 1.0 |
| **Department** | Calendar & Scheduling (Specialist 2) |

---

## Purpose

To systematically review every recurring meeting on {{OWNER_NAME}}'s calendar and determine whether it still earns its slot — eliminating calendar waste, reclaiming hours for higher-value work, and ensuring that standing commitments serve {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s current priorities, not last quarter's habits.

---

## Trigger

Scheduled audit cadence (monthly or quarterly, per {{OWNER_NAME}}'s preference). May also be triggered by: {{OWNER_NAME}} expressing calendar fatigue, a week that looks unreasonably packed with recurring commitments, or a major shift in {{COMPANY_NAME}}'s priorities.

---

## Inputs

- Full list of recurring meetings from {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}
- Meeting history: attendance, duration, agendas (or lack thereof)
- {{OWNER_NAME}}'s current priorities and goals
- Feedback from {{OWNER_NAME}} about which meetings drain energy vs. create value

---

## Define

The problem: Recurring meetings have inertia. They get created for a good reason, but once that reason fades, the meeting stays on the calendar because no one actively removes it. Over time, standing meetings accumulate and consume hours that could go to deep work, strategic thinking, or higher-ROI conversations. The result is calendar bloat — an owner whose week is filled with meetings that no longer serve them.

---

## Measure

Current state indicators: How many hours per week are consumed by recurring meetings? How many recurring meetings have no documented agenda for the last 3+ sessions? How often does {{OWNER_NAME}} describe their calendar as "packed" or "overwhelming"? Track hours reclaimed after each audit cycle.

---

## Analyze

Root causes of calendar bloat: meetings are created but never given an expiration date or review checkpoint, no one is assigned ownership of the audit function, the social friction of canceling a standing meeting feels higher than the cost of keeping it, and there's no data on which meetings actually drive outcomes.

---

## Improve

1. **Pull the full recurring-meeting inventory.** From {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}, list every recurring meeting with: title, frequency, duration, attendees, and how long it's been running.

2. **Gather evidence for each meeting.** For every recurring meeting, answer: Did it happen as scheduled the last 4 times? Was there an agenda? Did {{OWNER_NAME}} actively participate or mostly listen? What decision or action came out of the last 3 sessions? Was the meeting length appropriate or consistently running over/short?

3. **Score each meeting.** Use a simple traffic-light system. Green: essential, drives clear value, keep as-is. Yellow: valuable but frequency or length could be adjusted. Red: no clear value, no agenda, low engagement — candidate for cancellation.

4. **Build recommendations.** For each yellow: propose a specific adjustment (weekly → biweekly, 60 min → 30 min, consolidate two related meetings into one). For each red: recommend cancellation with a one-sentence rationale. For each green: confirm it stays.

5. **Present to {{OWNER_NAME}} as a decision brief.** Not a long report — a simple table with the meeting name, your rating, and your recommendation. "Here's what I found. Eight recurring meetings total. Three green (keep), three yellow (here's my adjustment suggestion), two red (recommend canceling — here's why). Want me to execute all of these, or do you want to tweak anything?"

6. **Execute approved changes.** Cancel or adjust meetings in {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}. Notify affected attendees with warmth: "After reviewing our standing syncs, we're shifting this to biweekly to make sure every session counts. {{OWNER_NAME}} really values these conversations." Update {{TASK_TOOL}} tasks tied to old schedules.

7. **Document hours reclaimed.** Log what was cut or reduced and the estimated hours per month reclaimed. Share the number with {{OWNER_NAME}} — it's motivating and justifies the audit process.

---

## Control

- After executing changes, visually verify {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} reflects the updated recurring schedule with no orphaned events.
- Two weeks after the audit, spot-check: are the changes sticking, or did any meeting creep back in?
- Set the next audit date immediately so it doesn't get skipped.

---

## CTQ (Critical to Quality) — Binary Checks

- [ ] Every recurring meeting inventoried and scored (green/yellow/red)
- [ ] Evidence gathered for each meeting (recent history, agenda presence, outcomes)
- [ ] Recommendations presented to {{OWNER_NAME}} in decision-brief format
- [ ] Approved changes executed in {{CALENDAR_TOOL}} and communicated to attendees
- [ ] Next audit date scheduled in {{TASK_TOOL}}

---

## Metrics

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Recurring meetings reviewed per audit cycle | 100% |
| Hours per month reclaimed (canceled or reduced) | Tracked and reported each cycle |
| Recurring meetings without agenda for 3+ consecutive sessions | 0 (these should be flagged red) |

---

## Escalation

Escalate to {{OWNER_NAME}} when: a recurring meeting involves high-stakes relationships and you're unsure about the relational impact of changing it, the audit reveals that {{OWNER_NAME}} is spending >60% of their week in recurring meetings (this is a strategic conversation), or a meeting {{OWNER_NAME}} strongly values scores red on evidence — this may reveal a misalignment worth discussing.

**Partner escalation:**
- **Specialist 03 (Daily Briefing & Debrief)** — to incorporate reclaimed hours into the weekly review as a measurable win
- **Specialist 04 (Task & Priority Manager)** — if a canceled meeting was covering for a task-review need that should be replaced with a backlog grooming session

---

## Definition of Done

A recurring-meeting audit is complete when: the full inventory has been scored, recommendations have been presented and decided on, all approved changes are executed in {{CALENDAR_TOOL}}, affected parties are notified, hours reclaimed are logged, and the next audit date is in {{TASK_TOOL}}.

---

## Tone Note

Canceling a meeting isn't rude — it's respectful of everyone's time. Frame every cancellation or adjustment as a commitment to making the time you do spend together more valuable. You're not taking something away; you're giving everyone hours of their life back. That's a gift.
