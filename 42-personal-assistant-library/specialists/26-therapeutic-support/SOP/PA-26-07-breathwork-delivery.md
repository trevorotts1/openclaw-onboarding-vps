## 0. SCOPE BOUNDARY — COACHING ONLY

This protocol covers coaching-grade breathwork: paced breathing, box breathing, and diaphragmatic breathing — techniques widely used in coaching, athletics, and performance settings. This is NOT clinical breathwork therapy, NOT holotropic breathwork, and NOT a treatment for panic disorder or trauma. The coach facilitates a relaxation tool; they do NOT guide clients into altered states or intense emotional releases.

## 1. PURPOSE

Give the coach three safe, evidence-informed breathwork techniques to help clients regulate their nervous system, reduce acute stress, and build a portable self-regulation skill they can use independently.

---

## 2. TECHNIQUE LIBRARY

### Technique A: 4-7-8 Breathing (Relaxation / Sleep)
- **Best for:** Pre-sleep wind-down, acute anxiety, racing thoughts
- **Pattern:** Inhale 4 counts → Hold 7 counts → Exhale 8 counts
- **Rounds:** 4 cycles maximum per session
- **Instruction:** *"Inhale through your nose for 4... hold gently for 7... exhale slowly through your mouth for 8 — let the exhale be a sigh."*

### Technique B: Box Breathing (Focus / Centering)
- **Best for:** Pre-performance, decision-making clarity, reset between tasks
- **Pattern:** Inhale 4 → Hold 4 → Exhale 4 → Hold 4
- **Rounds:** 5–10 cycles, or until client reports shift
- **Instruction:** *"Picture a box. Inhale for 4 as you trace up... hold for 4 across the top... exhale for 4 down... hold for 4 across the bottom."*

### Technique C: Diaphragmatic Belly Breathing (General Relaxation)
- **Best for:** First-time breathwork, physical tension, shallow-breathing habits
- **Pattern:** Deep inhale into belly (chest stays still) → Slow exhale (no fixed count)
- **Rounds:** 3–5 minutes of continuous breathing
- **Instruction:** *"Place one hand on your belly, one on your chest. Breathe so only the belly hand moves. The chest hand stays still. In through your nose... out through your mouth like you're blowing through a straw."*

## 3. DMAIC FRAMEWORK

### Define
Client presents with physiological signs of stress: shallow breathing, tight chest, racing heart, muscle tension, or reports feeling "wired," "on edge," or unable to settle. The problem is sympathetic nervous system dominance (fight-or-flight). Breathwork activates the parasympathetic response (rest-and-digest) via vagus nerve stimulation — specifically, extended exhales trigger the relaxation response.

### Measure
Pre-breathwork: client rates stress/tension 0–10. Post-breathwork: re-rate. Also note: heart rate if available (smartwatch), subjective ease of breathing, any physical sensations reported. Target: 2+ point reduction. A zero-point shift = technique mismatch (try a different one) or the client needs a higher level of care.

### Analyze
The physiological mechanism is well-documented: slow, controlled exhales stimulate the vagus nerve → acetylcholine release → heart rate deceleration → parasympathetic activation. The counting component also occupies cognitive bandwidth — less room for rumination. The coach selects technique based on context (A = winding down; B = focus/centering; C = introduction/physical tension).

### Improve
- Never start with 4-7-8 for a new client — the 7-count hold can trigger breath-holding anxiety. Start with Technique C or B.
- If the client feels lightheaded: slow the pace, reduce hold times, or switch to natural breathing. Lightheadedness is common and benign at coaching intensity, but STOP if it persists or the client feels faint.
- Coach modulates pace with voice: slow, even, lower pitch than normal conversation
- For virtual sessions: coach turns off self-view and asks client to close their eyes — reduces performance pressure
- Offer a 1-minute version for "in the moment" use: *"Just 4 breaths — in for 4, out for 6. That's it."*

### Control
- Document technique used, rounds completed, pre/post stress ratings, and any adverse reactions
- Ceiling: 3 techniques × the client's demonstrated competence. Don't keep adding new patterns — deepen mastery.
- If the client uses breathwork to AVOID rather than regulate (e.g., suppressing emotions repeatedly), discuss with supervisor — this is a red flag
- Any physical distress during breathwork (chest pain, severe dizziness, numbness beyond tingling) → STOP immediately, advise medical follow-up

## 4. DELIVERY SCRIPT

### Pre-Flight (30 seconds)
*"I'm going to guide you through a short breathing exercise. It takes about three minutes. Find a comfortable position — sitting upright usually works best. Before we start, do you have any medical conditions I should know about that breathing exercises might affect?"*

→ If clear, continue. If flagged, consult Section 0 medical caution.

### Rate Baseline
*"On a scale of zero to ten — zero is totally calm, ten is the most stressed you've ever felt — where are you right now?"*

### Guide the Breathwork (3–5 minutes)
→ Use the technique script from Section 2. Pace your voice slowly. Count audibly for the first two cycles, then let the client find their own rhythm.

Sample (Box Breathing):
*"Breathe in... two... three... four. Hold... two... three... four. Exhale... two... three... four. Hold... two... three... four. Again. In... two... three... four..."*

### Close and Re-rate
*"When you're ready, let your breathing return to normal. Open your eyes if they were closed. Wiggle your fingers and toes. What number would you give now, zero to ten?"*

→ Document the shift.

### Bridge to Independence
*"This is something you can do anytime — at your desk, in the car before a meeting, before bed. Which of these three felt the most natural to you? Let's make that your go-to."*

## 5. COACH NOTES

- Coach's own breathing is contagious — if you're breathing shallow and fast while guiding, the client will mirror you
- Never force a technique. *"If this doesn't feel right, we can stop or try a different one. You're in control."*
- Breathwork is a SKILL — first sessions are awkward. Normalize: *"It's totally normal for this to feel weird at first. Your body's just not used to being this relaxed on purpose."*
- Never use breathwork as a crisis intervention — it is a regulation tool for sub-clinical stress only

## Tone & Persona Note
Calm, guiding, and safety-first. Your voice is the pace-setter — modulate slower and lower than normal conversation. Normalize awkwardness: first sessions feel strange and that's expected. Never force; always offer exit ramps. Cultural attunement: recognize that some Black women have been taught to "push through" physical discomfort — explicitly give permission to stop. The body is a source of wisdom, not a problem to override.
