## 0. SCOPE BOUNDARY — COACHING ONLY

This script is a coaching tool for moments when a client becomes visibly overwhelmed, tearful, or dysregulated during a coaching session. It provides a structured pathway to help the client return to a regulated state so the session can either continue safely or be rescheduled. This is NOT crisis counseling, NOT psychological first aid, and NOT a substitute for emergency intervention.

**Crisis Resources:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — Call or text 988
- **Therapy for Black Girls** — https://therapyforblackgirls.com
- **NAMI Helpline** — 1-800-950-NAMI (6264) or text "NAMI" to 741741

**Hard Stop Triggers:** If the client (a) expresses intent to harm self or others, (b) describes active abuse or violence, (c) appears to be experiencing a panic attack with chest pain or breathing difficulty, or (d) becomes non-responsive — STOP the de-escalation script and immediately route to crisis resources. If in-person, stay with the client while calling 988. If virtual, stay on the line and help them connect to emergency services.

---

## 1. PURPOSE

Provide the coach with a calm, scripted, step-by-step de-escalation pathway to use when a session takes an emotional turn that exceeds the client's current coping capacity. The goal is regulation, NOT resolution — return the client to baseline so they can think clearly. The original issue can wait.

---

## 2. OVERWHELM RECOGNITION — COACH CUES

| Sign | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Verbal Flooding | Rapid, fragmented speech; jumping between topics; can't finish sentences |
| Shut Down | Goes quiet; looks away or down; gives one-word answers after being engaged |
| Tearfulness | Crying that escalates rather than releases; can't speak through tears |
| Physical Agitation | Shaking, fidgeting, shallow rapid breathing, hand-wringing |
| Cognitive Collapse | "I don't know" to every question; confusion; can't recall what was just said |

ONE of these is enough to activate the de-escalation path. The coach does not need consensus across cues.

---

## 3. DMAIC FRAMEWORK

### Define
The session has moved beyond productive coaching depth into overwhelm. The client's sympathetic nervous system is activated to the point that executive function (reasoning, decision-making, perspective-taking) is offline. Continuing the coaching conversation at this point is counterproductive and potentially harmful. The immediate goal is nervous system regulation — not processing the trigger.

### Measure
Pre-de-escalation: note the overwhelm cue observed and the trigger (what topic preceded it). Post-de-escalation: client self-reports readiness 1–5 (1 = still overwhelmed, 5 = back to baseline). Target: 4+. If below 3 after full protocol, the session should end — offer to reschedule and route supportive resources.

### Analyze
Overwhelm in coaching is a signal of mismatch: the depth of the material exceeds the client's current window of tolerance, OR the pace is too fast, OR the client entered the session already dysregulated. De-escalation is not about the CONTENT — it's about the NERVOUS SYSTEM. The coach's primary tool is their own regulated presence. A dysregulated coach cannot de-escalate a dysregulated client.

### Improve
- Coach monitors their own physiology: slow your breathing before you ask the client to slow theirs
- After de-escalation, never return to the triggering topic in the same session — that's a boundary violation
- For clients who overwhelm frequently (3+ sessions), the coach should initiate a conversation about adjusting session depth, frequency, or adding therapeutic support
- De-escalation is a success, not a failure — frame it as skill-building: *"What you just did — coming back from overwhelm — that IS the work."*

### Control
- Document every de-escalation event: date, trigger topic, overwhelm cues, technique used, post-rating, and session disposition (continued or rescheduled)
- If de-escalation occurs in 3 of 5 consecutive sessions, this client may need therapy-level support rather than coaching — flag for referral discussion
- Coaches must have their own support system — secondary stress is real
- Never use de-escalation to push through resistance. Regulation first, ALWAYS

## 4. DE-ESCALATION SCRIPT — STEP BY STEP

### Step 1: PAUSE AND NAME (30 seconds)

Coach internally recognizes: *"This is overwhelm. I'm switching to de-escalation mode."*

Say aloud, calmly:
*"I notice this is feeling like a lot right now. Let's pause the topic we were on. It's not going anywhere — we can come back to it another time. Right now I just want to make sure you're okay. Take a breath with me."*

→ Coach takes one audible, slow breath. Models regulation.

### Step 2: ANCHOR IN THE BODY (1–2 minutes)

*"Can you feel your feet on the floor right now? Just notice that. Press them down a little if that helps. Now your back against the chair — feel that support. You're safe in this room right now. The conversation can wait."*

→ If virtual: *"Look around the room you're in. Name one thing you can see — anything."*

### Step 3: OFFER CHOICE (30 seconds)

*"Would it feel better to take a few quiet breaths together, or would you rather I just sit here quietly with you for a minute? Either is fine. You pick."*

→ HONOR THE CHOICE. If they want silence, be silent. Do not fill the space with talking.

### Step 4: LIGHT RE-ENGAGE (1 minute)

After the chosen intervention, ask a non-cognitive question — something the body can answer:
*"What does your body need right now? Water? A stretch? A tissue? To step away for two minutes?"*

→ Meet the physical need before any verbal processing.

### Step 5: ASSESS AND DISPOSITION (1 minute)

*"On a scale of one to five — one is still really overwhelmed, five is back to feeling like yourself — where are you?"*

| Rating | Action |
|---|---|
| 4–5 | *"I'm glad you're feeling steadier. Let's wrap here for today — I want to honor what came up and give it space. Are you okay to end here?"* |
| 3 | *"Thank you for being honest. Let's take a few more slow breaths together, and then we'll wrap for today. Is that okay?"* |
| 1–2 | Stay. Repeat Step 2 or Step 3. If no improvement after 10 minutes total, call the client's emergency contact (if on file and pre-authorized) or route to 988. |

### Session Close
*"I really appreciate you letting me know — through your body — that we hit a tender spot. That's valuable information. We'll pick up gently next time. For now, what's one small, kind thing you can do for yourself after we hang up?"*

→ Get a specific, realistic answer. Document it. Check on it next session.

---

## 5. POST-SESSION

- Write a brief, factual de-escalation note in the session record: date, trigger topic, cues, intervention, outcome rating, disposition
- Do NOT process what happened with the client over text/email between sessions — that blurs the coaching boundary
- At the NEXT session, open gently: *"Before we dive in — how have you been since last time? Anything linger from what came up?"*
- If the client wants to revisit the trigger: *"I'm open to that. Let's go slowly and check in as we go. You're in charge of the pace."*

## CTQ Checks (Critical-to-Quality)
- [ ] Overwhelm cue recognized and documented (at least one of: verbal flooding, shut down, tearfulness, physical agitation, cognitive collapse)
- [ ] All five de-escalation steps completed (Pause & Name, Anchor in Body, Offer Choice, Light Re-engage, Assess & Disposition)
- [ ] Post-de-escalation readiness rating (1–5) documented with target of 4+ OR session safely ended with reschedule
- [ ] Trigger topic NOT re-engaged in same session after de-escalation
- [ ] De-escalation event documented: date, trigger topic, cues, intervention, outcome rating, and session disposition

## Tone & Persona Note
Calm, grounded, and compassionate — your regulation is the intervention. Never alarmed, never rushed, never dismissive. De-escalation is a success, not a failure: frame it as skill-building, not as "losing control." Cultural attunement: Black women are often expected to be the "strong one" who holds everyone else together. Explicitly release {{OWNER_NAME}} from that expectation in this space. "You don't have to hold it together here — that's the whole point."
