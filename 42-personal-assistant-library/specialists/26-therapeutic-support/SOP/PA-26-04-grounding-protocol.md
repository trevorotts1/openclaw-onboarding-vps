## 0. SCOPE BOUNDARY — COACHING ONLY

This protocol delivers a coaching-grade grounding exercise. It is NOT therapy, NOT a clinical intervention, and NOT a substitute for licensed mental health treatment. The practitioner is a coach — not a therapist, counselor, or crisis clinician. If at any point the client discloses suicidal ideation, self-harm, recent trauma, or severe distress beyond the scope of coaching, STOP the exercise immediately and route to crisis resources.

## 1. PURPOSE

Equip the coach with a structured, repeatable 5-4-3-2-1 sensory-grounding exercise to help clients interrupt spiraling thoughts, anxiety spikes, or emotional flooding and return to present-moment awareness. Suitable for virtual and in-person delivery.

---

## 2. PREREQUISITES

- Client is in a safe physical environment (not driving, not operating machinery)
- Coach has confirmed the client is NOT in crisis (see Section 0)
- A quiet 3–5 minute window is available

---

## 3. DMAIC FRAMEWORK

### Define
Client presents with racing thoughts, panic sensations, overwhelm, or dissociation. The problem is disconnection from the present moment — the mind is time-traveling (past rumination or future catastrophizing). Goal: re-anchor in the here-and-now within 3–5 minutes.

### Measure
Pre-exercise: ask client to rate current distress 0–10 (0 = calm, 10 = peak overwhelm). Document the number. Post-exercise: re-rate. Target reduction of 2+ points. If no shift after two rounds, consider alternative modalities (PA-26-07 Breathwork or PA-26-08 De-escalation).

### Analyze
The 5-4-3-2-1 sequence forces the brain to engage the sensory cortex — pulling processing power AWAY from the amygdala's threat loop. It works because the brain CANNOT simultaneously process sensory detail and maintain an anxiety spiral at full intensity. The counting structure provides a cognitive "handle" when executive function is offline.

### Improve
- If the client struggles to name sensory items, coach models first: *"I'll go first — I see the blue lamp on my desk. Your turn."*
- For virtual sessions: ask client to hold objects up to camera or describe their immediate surroundings
- Variation for highly activated clients: whisper the sequence slowly; use 3-2-1 abbreviated version
- Always close with one self-regulation resource (e.g., "Keep a cold water bottle nearby — sipping cold water triggers the dive reflex and calms the nervous system.")

### Control
- Document the pre/post distress scores in the session log
- Note which senses elicited the strongest response (pattern recognition for future sessions)
- If grounding is used in 3+ consecutive sessions, flag for coach supervision — the client may need a higher level of care
- Never exceed two full rounds in one session; repeated grounding without progress = escalate to Section 0 resources

## 4. PROCEDURE — 5-4-3-2-1 DELIVERY SCRIPT

### Coach Introduction (30 seconds)
*"I'd like to walk you through something called grounding. It takes about three minutes. All you need to do is follow my voice. There are no wrong answers. Ready?"*

### Phase 1: FIVE things you can SEE (45 seconds)
*"Without moving your head, look around and silently name FIVE things you can see. Take your time. Notice colors, shapes, textures — things you might normally overlook. When you have five, give me a small nod or say 'got it.'"*

→ Wait for acknowledgment. If struggling, coach contributes one: *"I see the corner of my desk. What's one thing near you?"*

### Phase 2: FOUR things you can TOUCH (45 seconds)
*"Now bring your attention to FOUR things you can physically feel. The texture of your clothing against your skin. Your feet on the floor. The chair beneath you. The temperature of the air. Name four sensations silently."*

→ Wait for acknowledgment.

### Phase 3: THREE things you can HEAR (30 seconds)
*"Close your eyes if that feels comfortable. Tune in to THREE sounds. Maybe my voice. Maybe a hum in the room. Maybe something distant — traffic, birds, the wind. Just notice three distinct sounds."*

→ Wait for acknowledgment.

### Phase 4: TWO things you can SMELL (30 seconds)
*"Now notice TWO things you can smell. It might be subtle — the scent of your soap, coffee, fresh air, the fabric of your sleeve. If nothing comes, think of your two favorite scents and imagine them."*

→ Wait for acknowledgment.

### Phase 5: ONE thing you can TASTE (30 seconds)
*"Finally — ONE thing you can taste. The lingering taste in your mouth. A sip of water. Or think of one taste you enjoy. Let it sit there."*

→ Wait for acknowledgment.

### Close and Re-rate
*"Take one deep breath in... and out. What number would you give your distress now, zero to ten?"*

→ Document. If distress remains at 7+, offer PA-26-07 (Breathwork) or gently discuss additional support resources from Section 0.

## 5. POST-DELIVERY NOTES

- **Do NOT** ask the client to "explain what happened" during the exercise — the point is somatic, not analytical
- **DO** normalize: *"It's completely normal for the mind to wander during this. You just bring it back — that IS the exercise."*
- File the distress scores in the client's session record under `grounding_log`

## Tone & Persona Note
Calm, grounding, and patient. Speak at a slightly slower pace than normal conversation — your voice is the anchor. Trauma-informed: never pressure, never rush, never demand answers. Normalize the wandering mind and affirm every small attempt as success. Cultural attunement: recognize that "feeling safe" may mean different things for Black women in different environments — the grounding space is explicitly named as safe.
