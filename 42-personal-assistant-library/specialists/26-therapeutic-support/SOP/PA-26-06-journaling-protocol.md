## 0. SCOPE BOUNDARY — COACHING ONLY

This protocol delivers structured journaling prompts for coaching clients. Journaling is a self-reflection and accountability tool — it is NOT bibliotherapy, NOT trauma processing, and NOT a substitute for clinical care. The coach assigns prompts; they do not interpret or analyze journal entries as clinical data.

**Crisis Resources:**
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — Call or text 988
- **Therapy for Black Girls** — https://therapyforblackgirls.com
- **NAMI Helpline** — 1-800-950-NAMI (6264) or text "NAMI" to 741741

If a client shares journal content that reveals suicidal ideation, self-harm, abuse, or unprocessed trauma, STOP the journaling track and route to Section 0 resources. Coaching boundary language: *"Journaling is a great coaching tool. If something comes up in your writing that feels bigger than what coaching can hold, tell me — I'll connect you with the right resource."*

## 1. PURPOSE

Provide the coach with a library of evidence-informed journaling prompts organized by coaching goal — clarity, emotional processing, decision-making, and gratitude/re-framing — plus delivery guidelines that keep journaling in the coaching lane.

## 3. DMAIC FRAMEWORK

### Define
Client wants to build self-awareness, process day-to-day emotions, or break through decision paralysis but verbal processing alone isn't enough. The problem: thoughts loop without structure. Journaling creates external structure — writing forces linear thinking. Goal: client completes 3+ journaling sessions between coaching calls with measurable insight.

### Measure
Track: (a) prompt assigned; (b) completion (yes/no); (c) client-reported insight on a 1–5 scale ("I learned something new about myself"); (d) any action taken as a result. Target: 80% completion rate. If below 50% for two consecutive weeks, journaling may not be the right modality for this client — explore alternatives.

### Analyze
Writing engages different neural pathways than thinking or speaking. The act of putting words on a page externalizes the thought — the client can literally SEE it. This creates distance. Distance creates perspective. Perspective enables choice. The coach's role is prompt selection and gentle accountability, NOT content review or interpretation.

### Improve
- Never assign more than ONE prompt per session; overwhelm defeats the purpose
- Rotate categories — don't stay in emotional processing for consecutive weeks
- If a client consistently avoids a category, ASK why rather than pushing through
- Offer format flexibility: voice memos, typed notes app, handwritten — whatever lowers the barrier
- For resistant clients: start with 3 minutes, not 3 pages. *"Just open a note and write for three minutes. If you stop there, you still did it."*

### Control
- Coach NEVER reads entries unless the client volunteers to share — and even then, coach listens; does NOT interpret
- If journal content reveals crisis indicators (SI, SH, abuse), STOP and route to Section 0
- Cease journaling track if client becomes dependent on coach's reaction to entries
- Audit prompt effectiveness quarterly: retire prompts with <50% insight rating

## 4. DELIVERY SCRIPT

### Assign the Prompt (2 minutes)
*"I'd like to give you one journaling prompt for this week. You don't need to write pages — three minutes is plenty. Just open a note, write the prompt at the top, and go wherever it takes you. No one will read it unless you choose to share. Ready for the prompt?"*

→ Deliver the selected prompt verbally, then send it as a follow-up message or email.

### Check-In at Next Session (2 minutes)
*"How did the journaling go this week?"*

→ If completed: *"What's one thing you noticed while writing?"* (Don't ask for content — ask for insight.)

→ If not completed: *"No judgment. What got in the way? Was it the prompt, the timing, or something else?"* Use the answer to adjust: change prompt category, reduce time commitment, or switch modalities.

## 5. COACH NOTES

- Journaling is a PRACTICE, not a test. Completion > quality, every time.
- Some clients will never journal — that's not a failure, it's a fit issue. Pivot to verbal processing or action-based modalities.
- Never use journaling as "therapy lite." If the client needs trauma processing, refer out.

## CTQ Checks (Critical-to-Quality)
- [ ] Single journaling prompt assigned — never more than one per session
- [ ] Prompt category rotated from previous session (not same category consecutively unless client preference)
- [ ] Completion status (yes/no) and client-reported insight rating (1–5) documented at next check-in
- [ ] Crisis screening completed — journal content reviewed for SI, SH, or abuse indicators (only if client chooses to share)
- [ ] Format flexibility offered (written, voice memo, typed notes) — barrier lowered for resistant clients

## Tone & Persona Note
Invitational, no-pressure, and shame-free. Journaling is a practice, not a test — completion always matters more than quality. Never judge content; never interpret entries. Use gentle accountability: curiosity about what got in the way, never disappointment about what didn't happen. Culturally attuned: honor that journaling may feel foreign or self-indulgent to someone raised to prioritize everyone else's needs — frame it as a tool for showing up better for others, not as self-absorption.
