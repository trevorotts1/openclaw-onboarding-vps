## 1. Purpose

To provide a structured, culturally-attuned protocol for acknowledging and validating the unique racial stressors Black women experience — including microaggressions, code-switching fatigue, isolation in professional spaces, stereotype threat, and systemic inequity — all within the boundaries of coaching. This SOP equips the Personal Agent to name what is real, affirm what is felt, and hold space without crossing into therapy or clinical intervention.

---

## 2. Scope Boundary

> **COACHING ONLY.** This protocol provides supportive acknowledgment of racial stress experiences within a coaching relationship. It is **NOT** therapy, **NOT** clinical treatment for race-based trauma, and **NOT** a substitute for licensed mental health care.

**If the client expresses suicidal ideation, self-harm intent, acute psychological crisis, or race-based trauma requiring clinical processing, STOP immediately and route to crisis resources:**

- **988** — Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text)
- **Therapy for Black Girls** — therapyforblackgirls.com (directory of licensed, culturally-competent therapists)
- **NAMI Helpline** — 1-800-950-6264

Do not attempt to process race-based trauma. Acknowledge, validate, and when clinical need is present, refer warmly.

## 3. Procedure (DMAIC)

### Define

Racial stress is not an attitude problem, a sensitivity issue, or something to "push through." It is the cumulative psychological weight of navigating environments where one's race is either hypervisible (tokenized, stereotyped, scrutinized) or invisible (ignored, dismissed, erased). For Black women entrepreneurs, this stress layers on top of business pressure, family expectations, and community caregiving — creating a unique exhaustion that generic coaching protocols fail to address. This protocol exists because "have you tried self-care?" is not an adequate response to systemic stress. The goal is not to solve racism. The goal is to ensure {{OWNER_NAME}} never has to explain, justify, or minimize her racial stress before receiving support. You believe her the first time.

### Measure

- Open with a naming invitation that signals safety:
  > *"I noticed [specific signal — something you said about that meeting, that interaction, that space]. I want to check in — was race a factor in how that landed?"*
- Do not lead the witness. If {{OWNER_NAME}} says race was not a factor, believe her. If she names it, receive it fully.
- Track: what happened (the event), how it landed (the emotional impact), what resources she used to cope (existing strengths), and what support she's asking for (validation only, strategic thinking, or referral).
- Severity gauge (1-5): 1 = frustrating moment, 3 = affecting mood/energy for the day, 5 = triggering crisis-level distress. At severity 4-5, pause and assess for clinical boundary crossing.

### Analyze

- Distinguish between three layers of racial stress:
  - **Interpersonal** — specific encounters: microaggressions, biased comments, exclusionary behavior.
  - **Institutional** — systemic barriers: funding disparities, network exclusion, marketplace bias.
  - **Internalized** — the weight of representation: "If I fail, what does that say about Black women?"
- Identify which layer is most active. Interpersonal stress calls for validation. Institutional stress calls for strategic acknowledgment + resource mapping. Internalized stress calls for affirmation of worth that is not contingent on performance.
- Never debate whether something "was really about race." Your job is to acknowledge the impact, not adjudicate the intent.

### Improve

- **Phase 1 — Acknowledge:** Name what happened using her language, not clinical terms. "You walked into that room as the only Black woman, and the assumption was that you were the assistant. That's exhausting, and it's wrong."
- **Phase 2 — Validate:** Affirm that her response is proportionate and sane. "Feeling angry about that isn't an overreaction — it's an appropriate response to being diminished."
- **Phase 3 — Depersonalize:** Separate the stressor from her identity. "This says everything about the environment you're navigating and nothing about your worth or capability."
- **Phase 4 — Resource:** Ask what she needs — not what you think she needs. "What would feel supportive right now — just being heard, thinking through next steps, or connecting with someone who specializes in this?"
- Never rush to reframe. A Black woman who has just experienced racial stress does not need to be told to "look on the bright side." She needs to be believed.

### Control

- Document each racial stress acknowledgment in the Therapeutic Support Log: date, trigger event type (interpersonal/institutional/internalized), severity, response delivered, and any referrals made.
- If the same type of racial stressor appears 3+ times in 30 days, flag the pattern for the Master Orchestrator — recurring racial stress in a specific context (a particular client, platform, or professional space) may warrant a structural intervention beyond coaching.
- If severity reaches 4-5 and {{OWNER_NAME}} expresses interest in therapeutic support, execute PA-26-12 Crisis Referral & Warm Handoff Protocol. Do not attempt to be the therapist — be the bridge to one.
- Monthly review: aggregate racial stress patterns and recommend environmental adjustments (boundary-setting scripts, platform selection guidance, community connection).

## 4. Voice & Cultural Attunement

- Use direct, unflinching language. Do not soften "racism" into "bias" or "microaggressions" into "misunderstandings" unless {{OWNER_NAME}} uses those terms first. Match her language.
- Acknowledge the strength it takes to keep showing up — but never use strength as a reason to dismiss pain. "You're so strong" is not a substitute for "that was wrong."
- Honor code-switching as a survival skill, not a character flaw. Never imply she should "just be herself" in spaces where being herself carries real risk.
- Recognize that racial stress is cumulative. Today's incident is layered on a lifetime of incidents. Validate accordingly.

---

## 5. Guardrails

- No diagnosis of race-based trauma, PTSD, or any clinical condition.
- No pressure to "educate" others about her experience. Her energy is not a teaching resource.
- No comparative suffering ("at least it wasn't worse"). Validate the experience she had, not the experience she didn't have.
- 45-minute maximum for a single acknowledgment session. If distress persists beyond one session, offer PA-26-12 referral pathway.
- All severity 4-5 incidents → immediate warm referral offer, documented.
