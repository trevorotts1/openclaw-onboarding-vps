### 1. Author Background and Authority
Robert W. Bly is a freelance copywriter specializing in business-to-business, high-tech, and direct response marketing with four decades of experience. His client list includes major corporations such as Grumman, AlliedSignal, AT&T, IBM, Lucent Technologies, McGraw-Hill, Forbes, and Agora Publishing. He is the author of more than 100 books including *The Digital Marketing Handbook* and *The New Email Revolution*, and has taught copywriting at New York University. Bly holds a BS in chemical engineering, which gives him particular authority when writing for technical and industrial clients. He has won numerous industry awards including a Gold Echo from the Direct Marketing Association and an IMMY from the Information Industry Association. 

Bly fundamentally believes that "a copywriter is a salesperson behind a typewriter" (attributed to Judith Charles, but adopted as his core philosophy). He asserts that copywriters are not "literary people" but salespeople whose job is to convince people to buy products. He believes that "the goal of advertising is not to be liked, to entertain, or to win advertising awards; it is to sell products" and that "before you can break the rules, you have to know the rules." His authority stems from measurable results—generating millions in sales through "good, clean, crisp, hardworking copy" rather than creative awards.

### 2. The Central Problem
The central problem Bly addresses is that copywriters and advertising professionals consistently fail to write copy that sells because they judge advertising as laypeople judge it—valuing entertainment, cleverness, and artistic quality over salesmanship. This leads to creating "artful ads, stunningly beautiful Web site design, and clever commercials whose artistic quality and creativity may rival the finest feature films" while losing sight of the goal: increased sales and profits.

Specifically, the suffering party is the client or employer who wastes time and money on copy that "looks pretty and reads pretty but doesn't sell the product." The person who needs this book is the copywriter who is currently acting as an "artist, entertainer, or, worse, a clown—but not a salesperson," as well as marketing managers who commission advertising that wins creative awards but fails to generate ROI. In the digital age, this problem is compounded by information overload (consumers exposed to 4,000+ ad messages daily), shortened attention spans, consumer skepticism, and compliance restrictions that weaken copy.

### 3. The Root Cause of Human Struggle
The root cause is that copywriters "abandon their parts. They forget they are salesmen and try to be performers. Instead of sales, they seek applause" (quoting Claude Hopkins). Bly emphasizes that "the biggest mistake you can make as a copywriter is to judge advertising as laypeople judge it." 

The author states directly: "Copywriters are salespeople whose job is to convince people to buy products... The goal of advertising is not to be liked, to entertain, or to win advertising awards; it is to sell products." This fundamental misunderstanding of the copywriter's role as salesmanship rather than entertainment creates a cascade of failures including clever wordplay that obscures messages, pretty layouts without persuasive content, and failure to ask for the order.

### 4. The Complete Methodology - Every Step
Bly presents multiple repeatable systems:

**The Four Functions of Copy:**
1. Get attention
2. Communicate
3. Persuade  
4. Ask for a response

**The Motivating Sequence (5-Step Formula):**
1. Get Attention (headline and visual focusing on strongest benefit)
2. Show a Need (demonstrate why the reader needs the product)
3. Satisfy the Need and Position Your Product as a Solution to the Problem
4. Prove Your Product Can Do What You Say It Can Do (testimonials, demonstrations, data)
5. Ask for Action (clear call to order, inquiry, or next step)

**The Four Functions of Headlines:**
1. Get attention
2. Select the audience
3. Deliver a complete message
4. Draw the reader into the body copy

**The 4 U's Formula for Headlines:**
1. Urgent (time element or reason to act now)
2. Unique (says something new or fresh)
3. Ultra-specific (specific facts, numbers, details)
4. Useful (appeals to self-interest, offers benefit)

**Pre-Writing Process:**
Step 1: Get All Previously Published Material on the Product
Step 2: Ask Questions About the Product (features, benefits, differentiation)
Step 3: Ask Questions About Your Audience (BDF formula: Beliefs, Desires, Feelings)
Step 4: Determine the Objective of Your Copy (generate traffic, conversions, inquiries, sales)

**Writing Process:**
Stage 1: "Get it all down" (free writing without editing)
Stage 2: Edit (delete unnecessary words, rewrite awkward phrases, read aloud)
Stage 3: Clean up (proofread for spelling, grammar, check facts)

### 6. The Transformation Arc
**Before:** The copywriter acts as an artist or entertainer, creating "clever wordplay, puns, and 'cute' copy" that wins creative awards but generates no sales. They write "artful ads" with "stunningly beautiful Web site design" that are "pretty to look at and pleasant to read but don't sell the product." They use "big words to impress others," write in "lengthy sentences" with "technical jargon," and create "cluttered layouts" that obscure the message. They write without understanding the customer, using "borrowed interest" and irrelevant gimmicks that attract curiosity but not serious customers. They fail to ask for the order and waste the client's money.

**After:** The copywriter acts as a "salesperson behind a keyboard," writing "good, clean, crisp, hardworking copy—copy that gives your customers reasons to dig into their wallets and buy your product." They master "writing clear, simple, direct copy that gets your message across" using the "you-orientation." They write headlines that rate high on the 4 U's (Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific, Useful). They translate features into benefits, provide specific proof, and ask for action clearly. They create copy that is "interesting, believable, specific, concise, relevant" and flows like a "velvet slide" leading the prospect from interest to sale. They measure success by sales generated, not originality or awards.

### 8. Tools, Exercises, and Practices
**The Features/Benefits Exercise:** Divide a sheet into two columns. Left: "Features" (descriptive facts). Right: "Benefits" (what the user gains). Translate every feature into a customer benefit.

**The Copy Length Grid:** Plot Emotion (High/Low) vs. Involvement (High/Low). High/High = Long copy; Low/Low = Short copy; other quadrants vary by price, purpose, audience, importance, familiarity.

**The BDF Formula:** Write out in narrative form the Beliefs, Desires, and Feelings of your target market. Share with team before writing copy.

**Swipe File:** Maintain a collection of successful promotions. "Put a red X on any swipe samples you see multiple times, so you can identify winners at a glance."

**Word Lists:** When stuck for a headline, make a list of words relating to the product, then mix and match to form different headlines.

**The "So are you saying" Technique:** When interviewing for testimonials, if the subject isn't saying what you need, say "So are you saying that..." followed by your desired statement. If they confirm, you can quote them.

**Johnson Box:** Set the letter headline in large, bold type in a box at the top of sales letters.

**Outline/Copy Platform:** Show at least a rough outline (working headline and bullet list of contents) to the client and get approval before writing first draft.

**Nine-Step Idea Production Process:** 
1. Identify the Problem
2. Assemble Pertinent Facts
3. Gather General Knowledge
4. Look for Combinations
5. Sleep on It
6. Use a Checklist
7. Get Feedback
8. Team Up
9. Give New Ideas a Chance

### 9. Objection Handling and Resistance
**On Creativity vs. Sales:** "If there are two 'camps' in advertising—hard-sell versus creative—then I side with the former." Bly rejects the objection that "great advertising breaks the rules" by stating "before you can break the rules, you have to know the rules."

**On Cleverness:** Resists the pressure to be clever or entertaining: "If you have to choose between being clever and obscure or simple and straightforward, I advise you to be simple and straightforward."

**On Long Copy:** Handles the "people don't read long copy" objection with the Copy Length Grid and examples: "People will read your ad only as long as it is interesting to them... Don't be afraid of long copy. Include as many facts as it takes to make the sale."

**On Data vs. Emotion:** While acknowledging data analytics importance, he resists pure data-driven approaches that ignore "strong emotional copy, which frequently generates higher response rates than the mediocre copy used in so many tests."

**On Compliance:** Addresses digital compliance restrictions by noting: "For each 10 percent closer you get to perfect compliance, your response rates drop by 10 percent." He advocates finding "honest and clever ways to get their stronger, bolder, more specific ads approved."

**On Skepticism:** When readers resist big promises, use a "secondary promise" that is "desirable yet smaller and more credible" so "even if readers are totally skeptical about the big promise, they can believe the secondary promise and order on that basis alone."

### 12. The Repeatable Execution System
**For Print Advertisements:**
1. Headline contains benefit/news/curiosity (4 U's)
2. Visual illustrates the main benefit
3. Lead paragraph expands on the headline theme
4. Layout draws readers in (Basic A: picture top, headline under, body copy, logo)
5. Body copy covers sales points in logical sequence
6. Provides information needed to convince qualified prospects
7. Interesting to read (story, news, self-interest)
8. Believable (specifics, testimonials, proof)
9. Asks for action (coupon, phone, URL)

**For Direct Mail:**
1. Outer envelope (teaser or blind)
2. Sales letter: Headline/Salutation → Opening hook (15 types) → Body (problem/solution/benefits) → Close (offer) → P.S.
3. Brochure (optional but recommended): Visuals, technical details, specifications
4. Reply device: Order form or reply card with action words

**For Landing Pages:**
1. Headline (big promise or curiosity)
2. Credibility builders (testimonials, logos, seals)
3. Bulleted features/benefits
4. Emotional hook/Story (problem dramatization)
5. Solution presentation
6. Proof (testimonials, case studies, data)
7. Call to action (big, bold button, no navigation)

**For E-mail Marketing:**
1. From line (establishes sender)
2. Subject line (teaser, benefit, or curiosity—like envelope)
3. Lead paragraph (killer headline or mini-version of complete message with immediate response link)
4. Expanded copy (features, benefits, proof)
5. Close (repeat offer and response mechanism)
6. Opt-out statement

**For Content Marketing (White Papers):**
1. Target market identification (BDF)
2. Problem identification
3. Solution identification (product or content as solution)
4. Content outline (narrow topic, tailored to primary prospects)
5. Medium selection (where to reach buyers)
6. Tactics (direct mail, email, ads)
7. Schedule creation
8. Budget allocation
9. Metrics definition (click-through, conversion, inquiries, qualified leads, sales)

### 13. The Quality Bar
Excellent copy is defined by:
- **Measurable Results:** "Copy that gives your customers reasons to dig into their wallets and buy your product... and not someone else's!"
- **Clarity:** Uses "short words, short sentences, short paragraphs, short sections" (4 S formula)
- **Specificity:** "The surest way to arouse and hold the attention of the reader is by being specific, definite, and concrete." Includes specific facts, figures, and concrete details rather than generalities.
- **Persuasion:** Follows the Motivating Sequence (Attention, Need, Solution, Proof, Action)
- **Headline Strength:** Rates 3 or 4 on at least three of the 4 U's (Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific, Useful)
- **Reader Orientation:** Uses "you-orientation"—"write for the reader," "start with the prospect, not the product"
- **Believability:** Includes proof through testimonials, demonstrations, research, specific claims
- **Conciseness:** "The velvet slide"—smooth path from interest to sale without "bumps and obstacles"
- **Conversion Performance:** For digital, high conversion rates, click-through rates, and engagement; for direct mail, high response rates and ROI; for all media, copy that "sells the product without drawing attention to itself" (Ogilvy)

### 14. Non-Negotiable Execution Rules
- Never judge advertising by entertainment value or aesthetic appeal—judge by sales results
- Never write without first knowing the product features and customer benefits (Features/Benefits list mandatory)
- Never use technical jargon unless 95% or more of readers will understand it; if used, explain it
- Never use big words when simple words will do ("Poor Faulkner... there are older and simpler and better words")
- Never use the passive voice when the active voice will do
- Never write without a clear objective (traffic, conversion, inquiry, or sale)
- Never violate the Unique Selling Proposition (USP) requirement—must make a proposition the competition cannot or does not offer
- Never submit copy without proofreading for spelling, grammar, consistency, and factual accuracy
- Never use "false logic" that crosses into deception—must be truthful even while persuasive
- Never write "blind headlines" that don't mean anything unless you read the copy
- Never use "borrowed interest" (unrelated scenarios to make product interesting)
- For digital: Never ignore compliance guidelines, but recognize that "overly zealous compliance in many cases results in weaker copy"
- Never use rotating banners on websites (1-2% click-through, slow load, ignored by 86%)
- Never use single-page websites for businesses (bad for SEO and usability)
- Never "bore people into buying"—must be interesting
- Never edit while writing (separate creative from critical stages)

### 15. Failure Patterns
**Bad Execution Indicators:**
- "Borrowed interest" ads using irrelevant scenarios (e.g., "THEY LOVED MY DRESS ON QUIRIUS 3")
- "Cleverness that obscures the selling message"—puns, wordplay, and "cute" copy that entertains but doesn't sell
- "Maximum confusion and minimum communication" through technical jargon, lengthy sentences, and big words
- Copy that "centers on the manufacturer—that talks about the company, its philosophy, its success" rather than the reader's needs
- "Long-winded copy with big words, lengthy sentences, and large unbroken chunks of text"
- Copy without a point of view or strong selling proposition—"presents the facts without really showing the reader how these facts relate to his needs"
- "Cluttered layouts and poorly reproduced visuals that look boring and turn the reader off"
- Failure to ask for the order or make response mechanisms clear and easy
- Writing without understanding the audience (not using BDF analysis)
- Taking "opinion polls" from friends/family on copy effectiveness
- "Keyword stuffing" in SEO copy that makes it read awkwardly
- Using personalization that is "obviously inserted into a form letter"
- Weak teasers on envelopes that reduce response ("Sawyer Life Insurance Announces Its 50th Year" vs. "The Secret to Living Longer")

### 16. Decision Logic
**When Choosing Headline Approach:** Use the 4 U's formula. "Rarely will a headline rate a 3 or 4 on all four U's. But if your headline doesn't rate a 3 or 4 on at least three of the U's, it's probably not as strong as it could be."

**When Determining Copy Length:** Use the Copy Length Grid. High emotion + high involvement (diamond ring) = long copy. Low emotion + low involvement (paper clips) = short copy. Also consider: Price (expensive = more copy), Purpose (one-step/mail-order = long; two-step/lead gen = short), Audience (busy execs = short; hobbyists = long), Importance (needs = short; wants = long), Familiarity (known products = short).

**When Choosing Simple vs. Clever:** "If you have to choose between being clever and obscure or simple and straightforward, I advise you to be simple and straightforward."

**When to Personalize Direct Mail:** Personalize when writing to existing customers or high-level executives. Use form letters for general prospecting to control costs.

**When to Use a Teaser on Envelope:** "Use a teaser only if it contains an irresistible message that will compel the reader to open the envelope." Otherwise, use a "blind" envelope (no teaser) to resemble personal mail.

**When to Use Long vs. Short E-mail:** "Short is better" for e-mail as a rule, BUT "if a few paragraphs won't convince people to buy offline, why should things be any different online?" Solution: Split copy between short front-end e-mail and long back-end landing page/microsite.

**SEO Keyword Strategy:** Use descriptive phrases of 2-4 words, not single words. Use keywords in headline (first word if possible), body copy, meta descriptions, and title tags—but avoid "keyword stuffing."

### 17. Self-Review Protocol
**Copywriter's Checklist (Before Release):**
- Does the copy fulfill the promise of the headline?
- Is the copy interesting?
- Is it easy to read?
- Is it believable?
- Is it persuasive?
- Is it specific?
- Is it concise?
- Is it relevant?
- Does it flow smoothly?
- Does it call for action?

**Additional Quality Checks:**
- Verify against 4 U's (rate 1-4 on each)
- Check 4 S's (short words, sentences, paragraphs, sections)
- Read aloud to check flow and rhythm
- Verify all facts with sources documented (footnotes/endnotes)
- Check consistency (company name format, numbers, dates)
- Verify "you-orientation" (is it reader-focused?)
- Check that features are translated into benefits
- Ensure call to action is clear and easy to execute
- For digital: Check HTML validation, spam filter triggers, mobile display, load speed
- For compliance: Verify legal/regulatory requirements are met without over-compliance that weakens message

### 18. Definition of Done
The work is complete when:
- Copy fulfills the promise of the headline completely
- It gets attention, communicates clearly, persuades effectively, and asks for a specific response
- All important sales points are covered in logical sequence (Motivating Sequence)
- It meets the 4 S criteria (short words, sentences, paragraphs, sections)
- It rates 3 or 4 on at least three of the 4 U's (if a headline)
- It is interesting, believable, specific, concise, relevant, and flows smoothly
- It has been proofread for spelling, grammar, factual accuracy, and consistency
- All sources are properly documented
- Client approval has been obtained (with the 25-50-25 principle applied: 25% changes that improve are kept, 25% that weaken are argued politely, 50% that make no difference are accepted without quibble)
- Response mechanism is clearly specified (coupon, phone, URL, reply card)
- For direct mail: Complete package includes outer envelope, letter, brochure (if needed), reply device
- For landing pages: No navigation distractions, clear CTA, credibility elements present, optimized for conversion
- The copy achieves the stated objective (traffic, conversion, inquiry, or sale)

### 19. The Amateur-to-Expert Gap
**Amateurs:**
- Judge ads by entertainment value, creativity, or aesthetic appeal; seek applause over sales
- Use "clever wordplay, puns, and 'cute' copy" that obscures the selling message
- Write about features instead of benefits; use "puffery" and generalities rather than specifics
- Use big words, jargon, and long sentences to impress; write long, unbroken chunks of text
- Don't research the product or audience thoroughly; write in a vacuum without BDF analysis
- Skip the "you-orientation" and talk about themselves or their company
- Don't ask for the order clearly or make it easy to respond
- Don't use specific facts, testimonials, or proof; make unsupported claims
- Write without a clear objective; create "artful ads" that don't sell
- Don't maintain swipe files or study proven winners; ignore historical data
- Don't test or measure results; rely on subjective opinions (including friends/family polls)
- Edit while writing (mix creative and critical stages), causing writer's block
- Change copy based on personal preference rather than proven principles or data

**Experts:**
- Judge ads solely by sales results and ROI; "grade performance on sales generated, not originality"
- Use clear, direct, simple language that sells; follow "the velvet slide" concept
- Translate every feature into a customer benefit; use specific facts and concrete details
- Use short words (like Hemingway), short sentences (14-16 words average), short paragraphs, and short sections
- Thoroughly research product, competition, and audience using BDF formula; interview SMEs
- Maintain strict "you-orientation"—"write for the reader," "start with the prospect, not the product"
- Ask for the order clearly, repeatedly, and make response effortless
- Use specific proof: testimonials, demonstrations, statistics, case studies
- Write with a clear, measurable objective aligned with business goals
- Maintain extensive swipe files of proven winners; follow "we are in the business of reusing things that work"
- Test rigorously (A/B splits, response rates, conversion rates) and optimize based on data
- Separate writing (creative) from editing (critical); use outlines and copy platforms
- Defend effective copy based on principles (4 U's, Motivating Sequence, etc.) but acquiesce gracefully on changes that don't affect results (the 50% rule)
