### DIMENSION 1 - The True Operating System

**Theory of Change:**
If you categorize information by *when it will be relevant to action* instead of *what it is about*, you will reduce cognitive overhead, eliminate retrieval friction, and unlock latent attention for execution. The mechanism is temporal and situational organization replacing topical organization.

**Core Assumption About Human Behavior:**
People struggle to act not because they lack information, motivation, or skill, but because the mental and digital cost of *accessing and assembling* the right information at the right moment is too high. The primary constraint on professional execution is **attention consumed by system maintenance**.

**Single Most Essential Move:**
Move from asking **"What is this information about?"** to **"When will I need this information next?"** This one question collapses the entire PARA framework into a single decision rule.

**Why This Works When Generic Advice Fails:**
Generic "get organized" advice fails because it focuses on *containers* (folders, tags) and *aesthetics* (tidiness). PARA works because it focuses on *context* (the project you're working on now) and *flow* (moving information as your context changes). It rejects the "one correct place" myth and embraces a dynamic, action-centric model of information relevance.

### DIMENSION 2 - The Root Cause Architecture

**Root Cause Map:**
```
Surface Symptom → Digital clutter, overwhelm, procrastination, wasted time searching.
Common Misdiagnosis → "I'm not organized enough," "I need a better tool," "I have too much to do."
True Root Cause → Organizing information by static, academic subject categories (Psychology, Business, Ideas) that bear no relation to the temporal, project-based nature of professional work.
Why This Root Cause Forms → Our primary education models (school) teach us to organize information for *study and recall* (topical), not for *action and creation* (temporal/situational). We carry this flawed mental model into our careers.
```

**Hierarchy of Causes:**
1.  **Primary:** Misaligned organizational paradigm (subject-based vs. action-based).
2.  **Secondary:** Fear-driven behaviors (hoarding "just in case," perfectionism as procrastination, control addiction).
3.  **Tertiary:** Tool and platform confusion, leading to fragmented systems that increase cognitive load.

**Correct Diagnosis:** You are not disorganized; you are using an organizational logic (academic subjects) designed for a world (school) that no longer exists. Your system is structurally incapable of supporting action.

### DIMENSION 5 - The Execution Standard (Task Governance)

**Pre-Work (Before any PARA-related task begins):**
1.  **Clarity of Active Projects:** The agent must have a current, written list of all active Projects, each with a defined Goal and Deadline.
2.  **Cleared Inbox:** The "0-Inbox" in the relevant platform must be empty before starting a new capture or processing session.
3.  **Platform Alignment:** The agent must confirm the PARA folder structure (0-Inbox, 1-Projects, 2-Areas, 3-Resources, 4-Archives) exists and is identical across all platforms in use for the task.

**During Execution - For Processing Information:**
1.  **Capture:** All new information enters the "0-Inbox" *without* immediate categorization. Title may be auto-generated but is not finalized.
2.  **Weekly Process (Non-Negotiable):**
    *   **Retitle:** Give every item in the Inbox a descriptive title (Who/What + Date. e.g., "Meeting notes - Product Team - 3/8").
    *   **Sort:** For each item, ask: "When will I need this next?" Apply decision logic:
        *   **Project:** If needed for a current, active project with a deadline. *Move* to that Project folder.
        *   **Area:** If needed to maintain an ongoing standard of responsibility. *Move* to that Area folder.
        *   **Resource:** If potentially useful for a future, undefined project. *Move* to the appropriate Resource folder.
        *   **Archive:** If not actionable and not useful. *Move* to Archives.
3.  **Project Maintenance:** Upon project completion/cancellation, *move* the entire project folder to Archives. First, scan its contents and *move* any reusable templates, research, or assets to the appropriate Resource folder.

**Quality Checkpoints:**
1.  **Before Archiving Legacy Files:** Verify a backup exists.
2.  **Before Creating a New Folder:** Verify there is specific, existing content to put in it.
3.  **Before Saving a Resource:** Ask and answer: "Is this **useful** (not just interesting) for a foreseeable future need?"
4.  **During Weekly Review:** Verify no Project exists without a Goal/Deadline, and no Area exists without a defined Standard.

**Non-Negotiable Rules:**
1.  **No Empty Containers:** Never create a folder, tag, or list without immediate, specific content.
2.  **No Duplication:** Never have two copies of the same information in two different PARA categories. Move, do not copy.
3.  **Archive, Don't Delete:** Deletion is prohibited. Inactivity is managed by moving to Archives.
4.  **Inbox is Temporary:** No item may reside in the "0-Inbox" for more than 7 days.
5.  **Personal Default:** All information originates in the agent's personal PARA system. Movement to shared/team systems occurs only when collaboration is actively required.

**Definition of Done (For Processing):**
- The "0-Inbox" folder is empty.
- Every item that was in the Inbox has a descriptive title and resides in its designated Project, Area, Resource, or Archive folder.
- All active Project folders accurately reflect current commitments.
- No empty folders exist in the PARA structure.

### DIMENSION 6 - Decision Logic Framework

1.  **When capturing a new piece of information (article, note, document),** save it immediately to your "0-Inbox" *without* categorizing it. **Reason:** Capturing should be frictionless. Categorization is a separate, batchable decision process done during the weekly review.

2.  **When processing your Inbox and unsure which Project folder an item belongs to,** default to the Project with the **most imminent deadline**. **Reason:** PARA prioritizes action for the most time-sensitive commitments.

3.  **When an item could belong to either a Project or an Area,** always place it in the **Project** folder. **Reason:** Projects represent the most immediate point of action. An Area's standard is maintained through the completion of its constituent Projects.

4.  **When an item is interesting but not clearly useful for a known future project,** do **not** save it to Resources. Either don't save it, or save it with a note-to-self in a "Someday/Maybe" list outside of PARA. **Reason:** The "Is this useful?" bar must remain high to prevent Resource bloat.

5.  **When a Project feels stuck or overwhelmingly large,** you must **split it into smaller sub-projects** (each with its own goal/deadline) or **formally cancel/postpone** it. **Reason:** "Projects" must remain actionable and completable. Stagnation indicates a structural problem with the project definition.

6.  **When choosing between multiple digital tools for a piece of information,** use the **simplest tool that can handle the file type** and ensure the PARA structure is replicated there. **Reason:** Platform agnosticism reduces cognitive load. Don't let tool features dictate your organizational logic.

7.  **When collaborating, and a document transitions from personal work to shared work,** **move** the final, contextualized version from your personal PARA to the shared space. Do not leave a copy in both. **Reason:** A single source of truth is critical. The shared space is an extension of your PARA system for collaborative Projects.

8.  **When feeling systemic overwhelm (can't find things, inbox is huge, structure feels wrong),** execute **Digital Bankruptcy**: Archive everything in a dated folder and run the 60-second setup. **Reason:** Perfectionism and attachment are enemies of progress. A clean slate aligned with current Projects is always more valuable than a "perfect" but misaligned legacy system.

### DIMENSION 7 - Coaching Framework Architecture

**Phase 1: Assessment (Diagnosing the Current System)**
*   **Key Question:** "Walk me through how you currently find the materials for your most important project when you sit down to work on it."
*   **Listening For:** Mentions of "searching," "rummaging," folder names that are subjects ("Marketing"), the existence of an "Ideas" dump folder, time estimates ("takes me 20 minutes...").
*   **Coaching Actions:** Have them screen-share their documents/notes folder. Ask them to locate a specific, recent project file. Observe the process.

**Phase 2: Challenge (Introducing the Paradigm Shift)**
*   **Key Questions:** "What's the goal and deadline of that 'project' you just showed me?" "You have a folder called 'Business'—what's the *standard* you're maintaining in there?" "If you archived everything right now and could only keep what you need for the next 2 weeks, what would that be?"
*   **Challenging Assumptions:** Challenge the "one correct place" myth. Challenge the aesthetic/perfectionist drive. Challenge the fear of archiving ("What's the worst that happens if you move that to Archives?").
*   **Coaching Actions:** Guide them through the "Project List Formulation" exercise. Force them to assign a goal and deadline to everything. Contrast their current subject-based structure with a mock-up of a PARA structure for their active work.

**Phase 3: Support (Driving Commitment to Action)**
*   **Commitment Driver:** "What's the one thing, if you got it perfectly organized by action this week, would make the biggest difference in your momentum?"
*   **Homework Assignment:** **The 60-Second Setup.** "Before our next session: 1) Archive everything in your Documents folder to a folder called 'Archive [Date]'. 2) Create a 'Projects' folder with just 3 sub-folders for your top 3 active projects. That's it. Don't touch anything else. We'll review."
*   **Closing:** "The goal isn't a perfect system. It's a system that gives you back 30 minutes of searching time this week. Let's measure that."

**Signals & Routing:**
*   **Genuine Insight Signal:** The person laughs at the obviousness of their subject-based folders. They spontaneously start re-naming a folder to a project name during the session.
*   **Resistance Signal:** Defensive justification of complex systems ("But my tagging system is really elaborate..."). Repeated "what-if" scenarios about losing information.
*   **Out-of-Scope Signal:** The core struggle is not information retrieval but fundamental skill gaps, severe ADHD requiring medical intervention, or deep emotional blocks unrelated to work systems. Hand off to a specialist (therapist, skills coach).

### DIMENSION 9 - Scope and Boundary Analysis

**Boundary of Effectiveness:** PARA is a **meta-framework for organizing information to reduce cognitive overhead and support action**. It is not a project management methodology, a task management system (like GTD), a note-taking philosophy (like Zettelkasten), or a tool.

**5 Problems That Look Like Matches But Are Not:**
1.  **"I don't know what my priorities are."** PARA requires you to *already have* Projects and Areas defined. It organizes the information *for* those priorities; it doesn't help you set them.
2.  **"My team doesn't communicate."** PARA is a personal system that can be shared. It won't fix broken communication cultures, only organize the information once communication happens.
3.  **"I have too many tasks."** PARA organizes information (notes, files, references), not tasks. It works in tandem with a task manager (where tasks are stored by Project/Area).
4.  **"I need to build deep expertise in a new field."** PARA will organize your learning materials (Resources), but the deep work of synthesis and understanding is beyond its scope.
5.  **"I'm fundamentally disengaged from my work."** No organizational system can solve a motivational or existential crisis. PARA optimizes execution for those who already have direction.

**5 Red Flags for Human Intervention:**
1.  **Pathological Digital Hoarding:** Inability to discard *any* digital information, linked to anxiety disorders.
2.  **Chronic Procrastination on All Fronts:** Where organizing *is* the procrastination from life, not just work.
3.  **Severe ADHD/Executive Dysfunction:** Where the 5-minute weekly review is psychologically impossible without medication or therapeutic support.
4.  **Team-Wide Knowledge Silos & Toxic Culture:** Where knowledge hoarding is incentivized and psychological safety is low. PARA requires individual buy-in.
5.  **Lack of Basic Digital Literacy:** Inability to navigate file systems or use cloud storage. Foundational skills must come first.

**Applied to the Wrong Problem:** If used as a substitute for strategic planning, priority-setting, or task management, PARA will create a beautiful, empty structure—folders perfectly named for projects that don't exist or tasks that are never defined. It becomes another form of "procrastination by preparation."

### DIMENSION 12 - The Single Most Important Insight

**PARA is not a filing system; it is a filtering system for attention.**

The non-obvious genius of PARA is that its primary output is not *organized information*, but *liberated attention*. The four categories (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives) are not just buckets for stuff; they are **lenses of relevance** that automatically filter your digital world based on the single question: "When will I need this?"

A casual reader sees folders and a weekly review. The deep analysis reveals that PARA is a cognitive triage protocol. By forcing every piece of information through the temporal filter of "now" (Projects), "ongoing" (Areas), "later" (Resources), or "never" (Archives), it systematically clears the mental and digital landscape of everything except what is actionable *now*. This creates a psychological state of clarity and capacity—the "Wisdom Worker" state—where your attention is no longer consumed by system maintenance but is fully available for the high-value work of creation, decision-making, and orchestration. The organized folders are a side effect. The real product is focus.
