### 2. The Central Problem
The central problem is that most companies waste enormous amounts of money on marketing that gets no results because their message is confusing. Specifically, graphic artists and designers are hired to build websites and brochures who know design but have not studied writing good sales copy or clarifying messages so customers listen. The result is "pretty websites" that don't sell things because "words sell things." The specific person suffering is the business leader or marketer who feels like they are "inside the bottle trying to read the label"—unable to see their own message objectively. They suffer from anxiety about spending precious dollars on marketing efforts that fail, wondering if their product is actually good or what went wrong. They are losing sales because customers cannot figure out what their offer is within five seconds of visiting their website. The problem manifests as "noise"—clutter and confusion sprayed all over websites, e-mails, and commercials that costs millions and kills more ideas than taxes, recessions, or lawsuits.

### 3. The Root Cause of Human Struggle
The root cause is twofold: First, brands fail to focus on the aspects of their offer that will help people survive and thrive. Second, they cause customers to burn too many calories trying to understand their offer. As Miller states, "The problem is simple. The graphic artists and designers we’re hiring to build our websites and brochures have degrees in design and know everything about Photoshop, but how many of them have read a single book about writing good sales copy?" The brain's overriding function is to help an individual survive and thrive, constantly scanning for information to help them meet primitive needs (economic/social resources, safety, relationships, meaning). When companies ramble about their manufacturing plant or internal goals, they position themselves as "chairs" in the room rather than "exits"—unnecessary information that doesn't help survival. Processing information demands the brain burn calories, and burning too many calories acts against survival. Therefore, the brain tunes out confusing sources to conserve calories. "If we pay a lot of money to a design agency without first clarifying our message, we might as well be holding a bullhorn up to a monkey. The only thing a potential customer will hear is noise."

### 4. The Complete Methodology - Every Step
The complete methodology is the **StoryBrand 7-Part Framework (SB7 Framework)**:

**1. A Character:** The customer is the hero, not your brand. The brand must identify something the customer wants as it relates to the brand, opening a "story gap."

**2. Has a Problem:** Companies sell solutions to external problems, but customers buy solutions to internal problems. The villain (root source, relatable, singular, real) causes three levels of problems: External (physical/tangible), Internal (frustration/self-doubt), and Philosophical (what ought/shouldn't be).

**3. And Meets a Guide:** Customers aren't looking for another hero; they're looking for a guide. The brand must position itself as the guide by expressing **empathy** ("We understand how it feels to...") and demonstrating **authority** (testimonials, statistics, awards, logos).

**4. Who Gives Them a Plan:** Customers trust a guide who has a plan. Two types: **Process Plan** (steps to buy or use product, 3-6 steps) and **Agreement Plan** (list of agreements to alleviate fears, often works in background). Plans create clarity and remove risk.

**5. And Calls Them to Action:** Customers do not take action unless challenged. Two types: **Direct Call to Action** (buy now, schedule appointment) and **Transitional Call to Action** (free PDF, webinar, testimonial video—on-ramping customers).

**6. That Helps Them Avoid Failure:** Every human being is trying to avoid a tragic ending. The brand must show the cost of not doing business with them, using "fear appeals" moderately—like salt in a recipe. Stakes must be defined.

**7. And Ends in a Success:** Never assume people understand how your brand can change their lives. Tell them. The brand must paint a picture of what life looks like after success (external, internal, and philosophical resolution), answering three dominant desires: winning power/position (status), being unified with something that makes them whole, or experiencing self-realization/acceptance.

### 7. Coaching Questions
- "What do you offer?"
- "How will it make my life better?"
- "What do I need to do to buy it?" (The three Grunt Test questions)
- "Where are you going?" (The hitchhiker analogy—what does the customer want to know when approaching your brand?)
- "Who does our customer want to become?"
- "What kind of person do they want to be?"
- "What is their aspirational identity?"
- "How does your customer want to be described by others?" (How do they want friends to talk about them?)
- "Can this brand really help me get what I want?" (The story question opened when defining desire)
- "What’s at stake?" (What will the hero’s life look like if she does or does not get what she wants?)
- "Do you believe in your product?" (Implicitly asked when assessing boldness of calls to action)
- "Can I trust this person?" and "Can I respect this person?" (Harvard Business professor Amy Cuddy's two questions applied to brand first impressions)

### 8. Tools, Exercises, and Practices
- **StoryBrand BrandScript:** A free digital application (mystorybrand.com) that organizes the brand narrative on a single page, mapping the seven elements of the SB7 Framework.
- **The Grunt Test:** Imagining a caveman in a bearskin T-shirt looking at the website—can he grunt the answers to what you offer, how it makes life better, and how to buy it?
- **One-Liner Exercise:** Creating a four-part statement (Character, Problem, Plan, Success) that answers "What do you do?" in a compelling way. Example format: "We help [character] who [problem] achieve/through [plan] so they can [success]."
- **Before/After Grid (Ryan Deiss tool):** Mapping how the customer's life will look after engaging the brand—external changes, internal feelings, average day, new status.
- **Villain Brainstorming:** Identifying a singular, personified villain that is a root source (not just frustration), relatable, and real.
- **Three Levels of Problem Analysis:** Brainstorming external, internal, and philosophical problems for each offering.
- **Empathy Statements:** Crafting statements beginning with "We understand how it feels to..." or "Nobody should have to experience..."
- **Authority Demonstration:** Collecting testimonials (specifically three brief ones), statistics, awards, and logos to demonstrate competence.
- **Plan Creation:** Developing either a Process Plan (3-6 steps pre- or post-purchase) or an Agreement Plan (listing agreements that alleviate fears).
- **Lead Generator Creation:** Developing free resources (PDFs, webinars, videos, samples) to collect email addresses as transitional calls to action.
- **Email Drip Campaign:** Creating a nurturing sequence (problem, plan, success pattern) with occasional sales emails.
- **Testimonial Interview Questions:** Five specific questions to ask customers to capture transformation stories: 1) What was the problem? 2) What did the frustration feel like? 3) What was different about our product? 4) Describe the moment you realized it was working. 5) What does life look like now?
- **Referral System Automation:** Creating systems to invite and incentivize happy customers to spread the word (coupons, rewards, affiliate programs).

### 9. Objection Handling and Resistance
**Objection: "My business is too diverse to reduce to a simple message."**
Response: Find an umbrella theme that unifies the various streams. Use the industrial painting company example—despite powder-coating auto parts, applying sealant to concrete, and sterilized hospital painting, the umbrella message is "We Paint All Kinds of S#*%" with a "Get a Quote" button. Create subplots for divisions using different BrandScripts under one umbrella.

**Objection: Positioning as guide feels weak/positioning as hero feels right.**
Response: The fatal mistake is positioning the brand as hero (citing Tidal/Jay Z failure where artists positioned themselves as heroes instead of customers). Heroes are ill-equipped and filled with self-doubt; guides have authority. "The day we stop losing sleep over the success of our business and start losing sleep over the success of our customers is the day our business will start growing again."

**Objection: Fear of overselling/being too aggressive.**
Response: "Of the thousands of clients we’ve worked with, we’ve yet to encounter anybody who oversells. Most people think they’re overselling when, in truth, their calls to action fall softer than a whisper." Being passive communicates lack of belief in the product.

**Objection: Need to include everything (27 things customers want).**
Response: "Stories are best when they are simple and clear. We are going to have to make choices." Pare down to a single focus until known for delivering that promise. "Varying too far from these chords means you risk descending into noise."

**Objection: Using fear/mayhem seems manipulative.**
Response: "Fearmongering is not the problem 99.9 percent of business leaders struggle with. Most of us struggle with the opposite. We don’t bring up the negative stakes enough." Fear is like salt—use too much and ruin the flavor, leave it out and the recipe tastes bland.

**Counterintuitive Truth:** "People don’t buy the best products; they buy the products they can understand the fastest." Even inferior products win if communicated more clearly. "If we’re going to continue reading this book, I have to warn you, I’m going to ruin movies for you. I mean, these things really are formulaic. They’re predictable. And they’re predictable for a reason."

### 12. The Repeatable Execution System
The repeatable execution system is the **SB7 Framework implementation protocol**:

1. **Create the BrandScript:** Using the tool at mystorybrand.com, fill in all seven modules (Character, Problem, Guide, Plan, Call to Action, Failure, Success) and the Identity Transformation foundation.

2. **Website Implementation:** Edit website to include five critical elements:
   - Offer above the fold (promising aspirational identity or problem solution)
   - Obvious calls to action (direct button top right and center, transitional secondary)
   - Images of success (happy people, not buildings)
   - Bite-sized breakdown of revenue streams (umbrella message with clear divisions)
   - Very few words (scannable, "Morse code" brevity)

3. **One-Liner Development:** Create and memorize the four-part statement (Character, Problem, Plan, Success). Teach to entire staff. Feature on business cards, email signatures, and marketing collateral.

4. **Lead Generator Creation:** Develop a specific valuable resource (PDF, video, webinar, free trial) to collect email addresses. Feature with pop-up on website after 10-second delay.

5. **Automated Email Drip Campaign:** Create nurturing sequence (problem-plan-success pattern) alternating with sales emails (problem-product-life description-call to action). Use software like MailChimp or Infusionsoft.

6. **Testimonial Collection System:** Use the five specific questions to interview customers and create transformation stories. Feature in emails, on website, in podcasts.

7. **Referral System Implementation:** Identify ideal customers, give them educational content to share, offer rewards/affiliate commissions, automate the process.

8. **Internal Culture Implementation (for larger orgs):** Create BrandScripts for divisions, develop onboarding curriculum based on customer story, hold regional meetings and retreats reinforcing the narrative, eliminate the "Narrative Void."

### 14. Non-Negotiable Execution Rules
- **Never position your brand as the hero:** "Always position your customer as the hero and your brand as the guide. Always. If you don’t, you will die." (Chapter 6)
- **Never confuse the customer:** "If you confuse, you’ll lose." Every element must serve the customer's story; "anything that doesn’t serve the plot has to go."
- **Must define a singular desire:** Do not dilute the hero's desire with too many ambitions. Pare down to a single focus per BrandScript until known for delivering that promise.
- **Must identify a singular villain:** "One villain is enough. A story with too many villains falls apart for lack of clarity." The villain must be a root source, not just frustration.
- **Must express empathy + demonstrate authority:** The guide must have both to move the hero. Empathy without authority fails; authority without empathy repels.
- **Must provide a clear plan:** Either a Process Plan (3-6 steps) or Agreement Plan. "Not having a plan is a guaranteed way to confuse your customers."
- **Must include direct calls to action:** There should be one obvious "Buy Now" button, different color, repeated throughout. "Soft" calls to action are unacceptable.
- **Must define the stakes:** Must include both failure (salt in recipe) and success (vision of transformation). "A story without stakes is boring."
- **Website above-the-fold rule:** The offer must appear above the fold with minimal words. No paragraphs above the fold.
- **No internal language:** Remove jargon and inside references. Use language customers understand (e.g., "Take those great pictures where the background is blurry" instead of "f-stop").

### 15. Failure Patterns
**Bad execution (amateur mistakes):**
- **Creating Noise:** Using "random information" that creates clutter and confusion. Example: Industrial painting company website looking like an Italian restaurant with a painting of the building and thousand links to FAQs and company history.
- **Hero Positioning:** Positioning the brand as the hero (Tidal example with Jay Z and musicians) instead of the guide. "When a brand comes along and positions itself as the hero, customers remain distant."
- **Calorie Burning:** Making customers work too hard to understand the offer. Using complex paragraphs, inside jokes, or technical jargon. "If we haven’t clarified our message, our customers won’t listen."
- **Skipping Internal Problems:** Only selling solutions to external problems while ignoring the internal frustration. "By assuming our customers only want to resolve external problems, we fail to engage the deeper story they’re actually living."
- **No Plan:** Failing to provide a process or agreement plan, leaving customers at the edge of a creek wondering if they'll fall into the waterfall.
- **Soft CTAs:** Hiding the "Buy Now" button or using passive language like "Coffee is nice, isn't it?" instead of "Will you marry me?" Being afraid to look like the "chainsaw mattress guy."
- **No Stakes:** Failing to define what’s at stake (failure) or what success looks like. "If there is nothing at stake in a story, there is no story."
- **Too Many Desires:** Trying to mention all 27 things the company offers instead of focusing on one story gap. "Clutter the story by diluting their hero’s desire with too many ambitions."
- **Vague Aspiration:** Using blob-like vague desires not connected to survival (e.g., "Inhale knowledge, exhale success" vs. specific survival benefits).
- **Ignoring the Grunt Test:** Websites that answer questions customers aren't asking (company softball games, random backstory) instead of the three critical questions.

### 16. Decision Logic
**When filtering content:**
- **Keep or Cut:** "If it doesn't serve the plot, it has to go." (Alfred Hitchcock's "life with the dull parts taken out")
- **Music vs. Noise:** Does this organize information or create confusion? Story formulas put everything in order so the brain doesn't have to work.

**When business is diverse:**
- Find an **umbrella theme** that unifies all divisions, then create separate BrandScripts for each division as subplots.

**When determining how much fear to use:**
- **Salt analogy:** Use a pinch, not too much. Moderate amounts of fear-rousing content are most effective; high levels make people block it out, low levels are too weak.

**When choosing CTAs:**
- Use **both** direct (marriage proposal) and transitional (date request) calls to action. If direct fails, use transitional to deepen relationship.

**When creating plans:**
- **3-6 steps maximum.** If more than six, break into phases. "Having more than four steps may actually add to, rather than reduce, confusion."

**When writing copy:**
- **Grunt test standard:** Can a caveman grunt it? If not, simplify.
- **Morse code standard:** Brief, punchy, relevant.

**When to declare work done:**
- When customer has resolved external, internal, and philosophical problems and achieved aspirational identity transformation.

### 17. Self-Review Protocol
**The Grunt Test:** Review website/marketing and ask: Could a caveman look at this and immediately grunt what you offer, how it makes life better, and how to buy it? Must be answerable within 5 seconds.

**The StoryBrand BrandScript Audit:** Check that all seven elements are populated and filtered through the framework. Ensure no element is skipped or vague.

**The Three Questions Test (for any marketing piece):**
1. What does the hero want? (Character)
2. Who or what is opposing the hero getting what she wants? (Problem/Villain)
3. What will the hero’s life look like if she does (or does not) get what she wants? (Success/Failure)

**Website Five-Point Inspection:**
1. Offer above the fold?
2. Obvious calls to action (repeated, high contrast)?
3. Images of success (happy people)?
4. Bite-sized revenue streams (clear divisions)?
5. Very few words (scannable)?

**One-Liner Test:** Repeat the one-liner to strangers/friends. Do they look interested? Do they ask for your business card?

**Narrative Void Check:** Can every team member explain the company's story? Is the organization "on mission" or suffering from disengagement?

**Email Campaign Metrics:** Are open rates above 20% industry standard? Are unsubscribe rates low (indicating qualified list)?

**Transformation Verification:** Can you clearly articulate who the customer wants to become (aspirational identity) and how your brand participates in that transformation?

### 19. The Amateur-to-Expert Gap
**1. Positioning:** Amateurs position themselves as the hero of the story (talking about company history, internal goals, how great they are). Experts position the customer as the hero and themselves as the empathetic, authoritative guide.

**2. Communication Style:** Amateurs create "noise"—complex, confusing messages that burn cognitive calories. Experts create "music"—simple, predictable, story-based communication organized through the SB7 Framework.

**3. Problem Depth:** Amateurs sell solutions to external problems only (the physical need). Experts address all three levels: external, internal (emotional frustration), and philosophical (what ought to be).

**4. Calls to Action:** Amateurs hide their CTAs or use soft, passive language ("Coffee is nice, isn't it?"). Experts make direct calls to action obvious, bold, and repeated (the "Buy Now" button is a different color, appears multiple times, unmissable).

**5. Message Focus:** Amateurs try to communicate everything (27 different desires/products) creating conflicting story gaps. Experts pare down to a single focus per campaign, becoming known for one thing before adding subplots.

**6. Clarity vs. Cleverness:** Amateurs use inside language, jargon, and complicated clever phrases ("Inhale knowledge, exhale success"). Experts use "Morse code"—brief, punchy, grunt-test passing clarity that connects to survival needs.

**7. Plan Provision:** Amateurs assume customers know how to do business with them. Experts provide clear Process Plans (3-6 steps) or Agreement Plans that remove risk and confusion.

**8. Stakes Definition:** Amateurs assume customers understand the stakes. Experts explicitly define both the tragic ending (failure) and the aspirational identity (success transformation).

**9. Team Alignment:** Amateurs suffer from the "Narrative Void"—no unifying story, confused employees, reliance on mission statements. Experts have the entire team operating "on mission" with everyone able to repeat the one-liner and understanding their role in the customer's story.

**10. Execution Discipline:** Amateurs read the book but don't implement. Experts complete the BrandScript, edit the website, create the lead generator, build the email campaign, and execute the roadmap despite human nature toward complacency.
